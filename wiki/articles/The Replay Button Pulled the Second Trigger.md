## Argument
Charlie Kirk's assassination on September 10, 2025 was not just a murder — it was an event engineered by its environment to become feed-native content, and the platforms that distributed it are causally implicated in the civilizational harm that follows. The trigger was pulled once; the replay button did the rest. The piece argues that algorithmic distribution transforms single acts of violence into infinite loops, that virality is "cheap physics for attention" that accelerates anything with low friction and high emotional mass, and that political murder is "tragically, perfectly feed-native." The systems are working exactly as designed — the design is the problem.

## Structure
Three sections plus field notes and ethics recommendations. "The Spark" documents the Kirk assassination at Utah Valley University and the immediate feed dynamics — the push notification before the echo crossed the quad, graphic video proliferating faster than text write-ups. "The Pattern" makes the historical comparison (Sarajevo to Dallas to Memphis — violence always had an audience; now it has analytics) and argues that the algorithm finds "your edges and builds you a home there." "The Protocol" breaks down the six-step pipeline from political violence to profitable content: Pre-Production → Capture → Distribution → Optimization → Narrative Hardening → Mobilization. Field Notes from a Recovering Insider and ethics recommendations for platforms, journalists, and users.

## Key Examples
- The six-step content pipeline: political violence selects environments with cameras guaranteed (campus quad, stage, lectern); capture is low-cost because the "crew" brings itself; distribution is autoplay, duets, stitches, remixes; optimization is the algorithm discovering a cohort willing to linger; narrative hardening splits the clip into two incompatible universes within hours; mobilization converts grief into donations, subscriber drives, and list-building
- The Kennedy vs. 2025 comparison: Zapruder film took days to reach TV; in 2025, push notification before the echo finishes crossing the quad
- Platform interventions proposed: friction by default (blurred preview + hold-to-view + interstitial context), crisis throttle (temporary down-ranking of near-duplicate uploads in hot zones), provenance tags that follow files across re-uploads, non-viral embeds for verified newsrooms
- PM whiteboarding "cognitive glue" — a formula for keeping users engaged; another wondering about detecting "pre-viral accelerants" in live video based on mic peaks and motion vectors — insider account of how engagement optimization works
- "If you optimize for engagement, you're de facto optimizing for intensity. And intensity has a limited inventory: sex, fear, anger, status threats, and the occasional puppy."

## What It Leaves Open
- Whether the six-step pipeline is inevitable given current platform architecture, or whether design choices at any stage could interrupt it without destroying the business model
- The legal status of the pipeline: "Tell me which step is illegal. Tell me which step isn't profitable." — this question is left open
- Whether the "neurodivergent aside" (ADHD and autistic users particularly exploited by engagement optimization) points toward a discrimination or accessibility frame for platform regulation
- The relationship between individual refusal ("refuse the instant replay") and systemic change — the piece is skeptical that individual action scales
