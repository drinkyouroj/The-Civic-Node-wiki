## Summary

Overview piece documenting the global ghost worker ecosystem: millions of laborers earning $1–2/hour who label training data, moderate traumatic content, and rate AI outputs that power ChatGPT, self-driving cars, and search results. The work is concentrated in economically vulnerable countries (Kenya, Venezuela, Philippines, India) and deliberately obscured behind subcontracting networks. Workers face psychological trauma with no protections, while enabling trillion-dollar AI industries.

## Key Points

- Ghost workers earn $1–2/hour globally for AI training data labeling, content moderation, and output rating
- Work concentrated in Kenya, Venezuela, Philippines, India — geographies chosen for low wages and weak labor protections
- Subcontracting structures: tech companies contract with agencies that contract with workers; each layer provides plausible deniability
- Psychological harm: content moderators process graphic violence, child abuse imagery, and extremism with minimal mental health support
- Platform: primarily Amazon Mechanical Turk and equivalent platforms (Sama, Scale AI, Surge AI, Remotasks)
- Worker invisibility is structural, not incidental: API abstraction makes humans look like function calls

## Newsletter Angles

- **The infrastructure of AI**: ChatGPT doesn't work without millions of human data workers. This is the foundational labor story of the AI era — and it's being treated as a footnote.
- **Geographic arbitrage as design**: Concentrating this work in the Global South isn't an accident of market forces. It's a deliberate choice to exploit regulatory and wage gaps. The AI companies building in California and exporting extraction to Kenya is a form of resource extraction with the resource being human cognitive labor.
- **The trauma externality**: Content moderation creates psychological damage (PTSD-like symptoms documented) that companies externalize onto workers in jurisdictions with no mental health protections.
