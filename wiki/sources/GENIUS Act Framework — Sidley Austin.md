## Summary

Sidley Austin law firm's analysis of the GENIUS Act, published on signing day (July 18, 2025). Covers payment stablecoin definition, permitted issuer categories, reserve requirements, foreign issuer rules, securities law implications, and timing. Includes note on SEC Chairman Paul Atkins welcoming the law.

## Key Points

- Three PPSI categories confirmed: bank subsidiary, OCC-approved federal issuer, state-qualified issuer under $10B.
- Reserve assets: coins/currency, Fed account credits, bank demand deposits, short-dated T-bills/notes/bonds, overnight repos, money market funds, tokenized forms of the above.
- Rehypothecation restrictions: reserves cannot be pledged or reused (narrow exceptions for margin, custodial services, liquidity).
- Monthly reserve reports, examined by accounting firms; annual audited financials if over $50B and not public company.
- Foreign issuer requirements: comparable regime determination by Treasury; OCC registration; U.S. reserves; not in sanctioned jurisdictions; must comply with freeze/burn orders.
- State preemption: federal framework preempts state money transmission laws for federal PPSIs; does not preempt state consumer protection laws.
- GENIUS Act does not address whether PPSIs will have access to Federal Reserve accounts — a gap.
- SEC Chairman Paul Atkins: "Payment stablecoins will play a significant role in the securities industry moving forward."
- SEC Commissioner Hester Peirce also praised passage.
- Broker-dealers as "digital asset service providers" must incorporate GENIUS Act compliance into programs.
- Timing: effective January 18, 2027 or 120 days post-final-rules; existing issuers have until July 18, 2028 to comply.

## Newsletter Angles

- The "GENIUS Act does not address whether PPSIs will have access to Federal Reserve accounts" is a significant gap — stablecoin issuers need Fed accounts for efficient dollar settlement.
- The three-year transition period (to July 2028) means Tether and other non-compliant stablecoins can continue circulating in the U.S. for years.
- SEC Chair Atkins welcoming the law shows regulatory alignment between executive and legislative branches.
