## Summary

Long-form journalism synthesizing the global "ghost work" economy that underpins AI systems — data labelers, content moderators, and annotation workers who do the human labor that AI is marketed as replacing. Documents the geographic concentration of exploitation in Kenya, the Philippines, India, and Venezuela; the corporate subcontracting architecture that enables plausible deniability; and the emerging organizing resistance from workers.

## Key Points

- Every major AI system requires enormous quantities of human-labeled data; this is not a temporary phase but a permanent structural dependency
- Workers earn $1–2/hour (median hourly wage on Amazon Mechanical Turk: $2; only 4% earn more than $7.25/hour)
- In Venezuela: 90 cents to $2/hour for data labeling; same work pays $10–25/hour in the US
- Scale AI (valued at $29B) coordinates hundreds of thousands of global workers for Meta, OpenAI (until recently), and the US military; takes profit while avoiding employer responsibility
- Amazon Mechanical Turk: launched 2005; 500,000+ registered workers by 2011; named after the 1770 chess-playing automaton hiding a human inside
- More than 140 Facebook content moderators in Kenya diagnosed with severe PTSD after reviewing murders, child abuse, and extreme violence for less than $2/hour
- Workers classified as "independent contractors" — shields companies from minimum wage, overtime, health insurance, workers' compensation
- May 2024: nearly 100 Kenyan data labelers wrote to President Biden describing conditions as "modern-day slavery"
- Corporate subcontracting architecture: Meta → intermediary (Scale AI, Appen, Sama) → local subcontractor → workers; enables plausible deniability at every level
- When African content moderators sued Meta for psychological harm, Meta argued it wasn't their employer — the local subcontractor was
- The data annotation market projected to reach $6.98–8.2B by 2028–2029
- EU AI Act includes transparency provisions for data workers in AI supply chains
- Kenyan data labelers filed lawsuits against Meta; TikTok moderators in Turkey formed union, faced aggressive union-busting
- 2025 summit: moderators from Kenya, Philippines, India, Latin America united around common demands: direct employment, mental health support, higher wages, right to organize
- The "AI illusion" (ILO term): presenting human labor as machine automation to justify lower prices, reduce accountability, avoid employment regulations

## Newsletter Angles

- The ghost work story is the inverse of the Mechanical Turk Pattern: it's not about a single product being mislabeled — it's about an entire industry's business model depending on invisible labor
- The geographic concentration of exploitation is deliberate: companies target countries with high unemployment, educated workforces, and weak labor protections — a race to the bottom that is global and structural
- Content moderation trauma is the AI industry's deepest ethical scandal. 140+ Facebook moderators in Kenya with PTSD. This is not a side effect — it is the product
- The organizing story is underreported: workers across four continents are building cross-border solidarity. This is labor internationalism for the AI age
