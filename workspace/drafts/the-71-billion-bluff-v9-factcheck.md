## MUST FIX (2)

### 1. "95% of the world's DRAM revenue" — Overstated

**Draft claim (appears twice):**
- ¶3: "the two companies that, together with Micron, control roughly 95% of the world's DRAM revenue"
- ¶7: "Three companies control 95% of global memory production"

**Cited source:** TrendForce Q3 2025 DRAM revenue report (Nov 26, 2025)

**What the source actually shows:**
- SK Hynix: ~33.2%
- Samsung: ~33-34%
- Micron: ~25.7%
- Combined: ~92-93%
- Nanya and smaller players hold the remainder

**Verdict:** "Roughly 95%" overstates by 2-3 percentage points. Should be "over 90%" or "roughly 93%." This figure anchors the structural concentration argument and appears twice, so accuracy matters.

**Suggested fix:** Replace both instances of "95%" with "over 90%" or "roughly 93%."

---

### 2. CXMT/YMTC timeline: "enter volume production" in 2028 — Contradicts cited source

**Draft claim (¶8):** "The real price correction probably arrives in 2028, when Chinese competitors CXMT and YMTC enter volume production."

**Cited source:** Tom's Hardware — "China's CXMT and YMTC to expand memory output"

**What the source actually shows:**
- CXMT's new Shanghai fab: equipment installation underway, volume production targeted for **2027**
- YMTC's Wuhan fab: also coming online around **2027**
- YMTC targets 15% of NAND flash market by 2028

**Verdict:** The cited source says volume production begins in 2027, not 2028. The article's claim that the "price correction arrives in 2028" may be editorially defensible (ramp takes time, market impact lags), but attributing it to when they "enter volume production" contradicts the source.

**Suggested fix:** "The real price correction probably arrives in 2028, when Chinese competitors CXMT and YMTC — both targeting volume production in 2027 — ramp to market-moving scale."

## SHOULD FIX (2)

### 3. Opening price claim: "$400 for a 32GB DDR5 memory kit" — Imprecise

**Draft claim (¶1):** "There's a number that doesn't make sense right now, and it's $400. That's what a 32GB DDR5 memory kit costs in April 2026."

**Cited source:** Tom's Hardware RAM Price Index 2026

**What the source actually shows (April 2026):**
- Cheapest available 32GB DDR5 kit: ~$269 (Corsair Vengeance, sells out within hours)
- Crucial DDR5-5600: ~$376
- Corsair Vengeance RGB DDR5-6000: $370-$409
- Kits under $359 are regularly wiped out by demand
- Typical available-to-buy price: $370-$400+

**Verdict:** $400 is within the range but not the floor. The article presents it as a categorical statement ("That's what a 32GB DDR5 memory kit costs") when the actual range is $269-$400+. The $269 deals evaporate instantly, so $400 is closer to the real purchase experience, but a pedantic reader checking Tom's Hardware will find cheaper listings.

**Suggested fix options:**
- "That's what a 32GB DDR5 memory kit typically costs in April 2026"
- "That's what you'll actually pay for a 32GB DDR5 memory kit in April 2026"
- Specify the kit/speed tier: "That's what a 32GB DDR5-6000 kit costs in April 2026"

---

### 4. Stargate framing: "killed after Oracle's financing collapsed" — Oversimplified

**Draft claim (¶5):** "the Stargate data center expansion in Abilene, Texas was killed after Oracle's financing collapsed"

**Cited source:** Bloomberg, March 6, 2026

**What the source actually shows:**
- The Abilene **expansion** was shelved (correct)
- Cause: combination of difficult financing terms AND OpenAI's shifting capacity forecasts
- The broader 4.5 GW Oracle-OpenAI agreement remained intact
- "Oracle's financing collapsed" implies Oracle went bust or defaulted, which is not what happened

**Verdict:** The word "expansion" is accurate, but attributing it solely to "Oracle's financing collapsed" is reductive. The financing didn't collapse in the way readers will infer.

**Suggested fix:** "killed when financing terms fell apart" or "killed after Oracle's financing fell through and OpenAI's demand projections shrank"

## MINOR NOTE (1)

### 5. HP memory cost baseline: "up from 15%" — Low end of range

**Draft claim (¶2):** "HP says memory now accounts for 35% of a PC's build cost, up from 15%"

**Cited source:** Tom's Hardware

**What the source shows:** The prior-quarter figure was 15-18%, not precisely 15%. The article uses the low end of the range.

**Verdict:** Not inaccurate, but "up from 15-18%" would be more precise. Low priority — the 35% figure and the directional doubling are both confirmed.
