### **The digital-dollar fork in the road**

Cash is fading, plastic is passé, and every fintech startup on Earth swears its app will “re-invent money.” In the middle of that hype cycle, the Federal Reserve has been quietly studying a retail Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) — a fully digital dollar you’d hold in an account at, or intermediated by, the central bank itself.1 But yesterday the House passed _H.R. 1919, the Anti-CBDC Surveillance State Act_ , by a squeaker vote of 219 – 210.2 The bill is now idling in the Senate, yet it has already split the policy world into two irreconcilable camps:

  * **Camp A:** “We need the Fed’s digital dollar to keep America competitive and modernize payments.”

  * **Camp B:** “A retail CBDC is programmable Big Brother money — burn it with fire.”




I’m in Camp B, and H.R. 1919 is our legislative Molotov. Here’s why.

The Congressional Budget Office says the fiscal impact is basically nil4 — this is ideology, not budgeting. And the White House has already issued a _Statement of Administration Policy_ promising President Trump will sign it “to keep unelected bureaucrats from weaponizing a digital dollar.”5

### **Privacy, surveillance, and the nightmare use-case**

Emmer’s pitch is simple: a retail CBDC is _government-controlled programmable money_ — the stuff of Orwell and Black Mirror rolled into one.6 Because every transaction settles on a Fed-controlled ledger, Washington could, in principle, see every coffee you buy, every tweet-tip you send, and every donation you make to candidates it dislikes. The Cato Institute puts it bluntly: a CBDC “would establish a direct line between each citizen’s financial activity and the federal government.”

Even worse, programmable money can be _conditional_ money. If code can settle a payment, code can also refuse it. China’s e-CNY already bakes in spending rules and transaction caps; its pilot volume is pushing one _trillion_ dollars.7 Canada showed in 2022 how quickly a democratic government can freeze protestors’ bank accounts when the political temperature spikes.8 The lesson is obvious — the tool exists, and in a crisis someone will use it.

### **Executive whiplash and why Congress wants a seatbelt**

Remember Executive Order 14178? In January President Trump flat-out banned federal agencies from _“establish[ing], issu[ing], or promot[ing]”_ a CBDC — reversing the Biden-era order that told them to study one with “urgency.”9 Great… until the next administration signs an equal-and-opposite EO. H.R. 1919 tries to bolt the policy to the floor so no future president (or Fed governor) can yo-yo the dollar back into the CBDC lab.

That fight has turned monetary plumbing into a separation-of-powers showdown: Congress wants the last word, the White House agrees (for now), and the Fed is staring at a legal brick wall.

H.R. 1919 isn’t neutral — it’s an explicit endorsement of the right-hand column. By immunizing _open, permissionless, private_ digital dollars, the bill pushes innovation toward Bitcoin and USD-backed stablecoins rather than a FedCoin. Supporters argue that keeps America competitive _without_ handing the keys to the surveillance state.

### **“But the rest of the world is racing ahead!”**

Sort of. The Atlantic Council counts 137 countries (98 % of global GDP) poking at CBDCs today — up from just 35 five years ago.10 Yet only three small economies (Bahamas, Jamaica, Nigeria) have actually launched a retail version, and adoption is… meh. Many rich countries, including EU members, now talk loud about “wholesale only” models that never touch consumer wallets.

Meanwhile China touts its nearly $987 billion e-CNY pilot, but that headline number sits next to phone-book privacy policies and hard transaction limits. The supposed “race” looks less like a sprint and more like a marathon no one is eager to finish.

### **Where the bill goes from here — and why it matters even if it dies**

Senate passage would require 60 votes — a tall order. If H.R. 1919 stalls, CBDC research could lurch back to life under a future administration. Yet politically the bill has already done its job: it rebranded the very phrase _“central bank digital currency”_ into a four-letter word on Capitol Hill. Any Fed official who wants to revive the project now has to answer for the privacy apocalypse first.

And for those of us building in Bitcoin, stablecoins, or whatever comes next, the message is clear: the best defense against programmable surveillance cash is to double down on systems that are open, permissionless, and — most crucially — **non-custodial**.

Because freedom isn’t a feature toggle. It’s the default — or it’s gone.
