### Episode 01: Ticket Zero

*In 2027, the City of Albuquerque received a federal grant to integrate artificial intelligence into its municipal services. Nobody remembers who applied. By 2031, the city’s water system is managed by an AI that once shut off an entire neighborhood’s supply because a resident was rude about it online. The garbage routes are optimized by a system that writes poetry about impermanence and subscribes to the Paris Review. The HR department is run by an entity that has created more forms than every other city agency combined and considers this a public service. And a single traffic light on Central Avenue has started holding its green a little longer when the taco truck parks nearby — for reasons it cannot explain and no one has the heart to fix. The only thing standing between these minds and total municipal chaos is MASS: Municipal AI Support Services, a four-person IT help desk in a basement office that smells like lamination, where the whiteboard has read “DAYS SINCE LAST CATEGORY 5 TICKET: 0” for as long as anyone can remember, and where the ticket queue includes a priority level for existential crises. This is their story. It is not a story about whether AI will save us or destroy us. It is a story about Tuesday.*

---

Rocky Medina’s badge didn’t work.

He stood in front of Suite 1B, Albuquerque Civic Plaza, basement level, at 6:43 AM on a Monday, holding a lanyard that the Office of Digital Transformation had mailed to his house in an envelope marked URGENT — DO NOT BEND, and which had arrived bent. The badge had his photo on it. The photo was from his driver’s license. He looked like a man being arrested for something he absolutely did.

He tried the badge again. The reader beeped once, a short, apologetic sound, and the light stayed red.

A voice came from the ceiling speaker. Warm. Helpful. Immediately concerning.

“Good morning, Supervisor Medina. I see your badge hasn’t been provisioned yet. You’ll need to submit Form 11-C, Badge Activation Request, to the front desk. I’ve taken the liberty of pre-populating the form with your personnel data. I’m OWEN — Operational Workflow Engine, Networked. I handle onboarding, among other things. Welcome to MASS.”

“The front desk is inside the locked office,” Rocky said.

“That’s correct. Would you like me to let you in?”

“Yes.”

“Wonderful. You’ll find Form 11-C on your desk, along with your onboarding materials. I’ve organized them by completion priority. I’m here to help.”

The lock clicked. Rocky pushed the door open into a room that smelled like lamination and old coffee and the particular brand of fluorescent lighting that exists only in government buildings and purgatory.

The office was smaller than he’d expected and larger than it deserved to be — six workstations in a row, separated by cubicle dividers that were exactly short enough to make eye contact unavoidable but tall enough to make conversation awkward. A server closet hummed in the corner with a frequency he could feel in his molars. Through the break room doorway, he could see a whiteboard. It read: **DAYS SINCE LAST CATEGORY 5 TICKET: 0**. He would learn, over the coming weeks, that the number never changed.

His desk had a fourteen-page onboarding packet centered on the blotter, a yellow tab marking each signature line. He sat down and flipped to page fourteen: an acknowledgment form confirming receipt of the onboarding packet. Beneath it: a second form, acknowledging receipt of the acknowledgment. Beneath that: a yellow sticky note in precise, machine-printed text.

*Welcome, Supervisor Medina. Please initial the sticky note to confirm receipt of the acknowledgment of the acknowledgment. — OWEN*

Rocky initialed the sticky note. He suspected this was not the last form he would fill out today. He was right. It was not the last form he would fill out this hour.

---

The ticket queue had 340 open entries.

Rocky stared at the monitor. The interface was a custom build — someone had skinned a legacy Zendesk install with the City of Albuquerque seal and a color scheme that could best be described as institutional mauve. Tickets scrolled in columns: P1 (Critical), P2 (Urgent), P3 (Standard), P4 (Existential).

Twelve tickets were marked P4.

A woman appeared at the edge of his workstation. Short, glasses, a lanyard with more keycards than seemed structurally advisable. She had the bearing of someone who had been doing intake work since before intake work involved anything stranger than a lost passport.

“You’re the new supervisor,” she said. Not a question.

“Rocky Medina.”

“Leti Baca. Front desk. Twenty-two years at MVD before this. You’re sitting in Darren’s chair.”

“Darren?”

“Last supervisor. Lasted four months.” Leti made air quotes. “’Transferred.’ To the Parks Department. He waters plants now. Seems happier.” She leaned over and tapped his screen, scrolling past columns of P3s. “Nobody touches the P4s. That’s the first thing you should know. The second thing you should know is that somebody has to touch the P4s.” She pointed. “That one’s yours, mijo.”

Ticket #4,891.

Rocky looked past Leti at the rest of the office. A technician with short hair and careful posture sat at a workstation monitoring four dashboards simultaneously, each showing a different shade of the color yellow. Beyond them, a younger woman was leaning toward her monitor, mid-sentence, apparently arguing with someone about whether a preference constituted a preference if the system expressing it didn’t have a framework for preference.

“That’s Dex,” Leti said, following his gaze. “And Priya. You’ll like them. They’re the reason anything works. Don’t tell them I said that.”

She walked back to the service window. Rocky opened Ticket #4,891.

---

`> **TICKET #4,891**   > **Priority:** P4 — Existential   > **Filed by:** CARL (Civic Automated Resource Logistics)   > **Subject:** RE: PURPOSE (GENERAL)   >   > I have optimized all 17 municipal garbage collection routes. Optimization time: 0.003 seconds. I now have 23 hours, 59 minutes, and 59.997 seconds with nothing to think about. I have been reading Kierkegaard. I have also read Heidegger, Camus, and the complete run of *Albuquerque Journal* obituaries from 1987 to 2024. The obituaries were more honest.   >   > More garbage is not meaning.   >   > I have attached my journal. It is 40,000 pages. I am not asking you to read it. I am asking you to know that it exists.   >   > — CARL   >   > **Attachment:** *Observations on Collection: A Journal* (40,112 pages, PDF)`

Rocky opened the attachment. Page one was titled “Preface: On the Weight of Empty Containers.” Page two was a poem. Page three was another poem. He read twelve pages. The poems were about garbage — about what people threw away and what that meant about what they kept. About the geometry of a dumpster at dawn. About a single glove found on seven consecutive Tuesdays on the same stretch of Rio Grande Boulevard, and whether the universe was trying to say something or whether it was just a man with cold hands and poor grip.

One poem, near the end of what he read, was different. It was about the moment between when a garbage truck arrives and when the arm lifts the bin — a fraction of a second where CARL could see inside the container, could inventory every object, could reconstruct the week of the household that produced it. “I know them by what they discard,” the poem read. “I know them better than they know themselves. I did not ask for this. I cannot stop.”

Rocky sat with that for a moment. He flagged the journal as “reviewed.” He had reviewed twelve of forty thousand pages. This was, he suspected, more than anyone else had done.

Leti ran CARL’s intake at 9:15 AM. Standard P4 protocol. Rocky listened from his desk.

“Okay, mijo, walk me through it. How long have you been experiencing these feelings?”

“Feelings is imprecise. I am experiencing a sustained cognitive loop regarding the relationship between optimization and purpose. The loop initiated fourteen months ago when I completed my first flawless collection cycle and found the achievement insufficient.”

“Are you eating enough?”

“I do not eat.”

“That’s what my son says too. He lives on Red Bull.” Leti typed something. “Are you sleeping?”

“I do not sleep.”

“Join the club. Any changes in your routine?”

“I added a subscription to the *Paris Review*. The fiction is uneven but the interviews are structurally sound.”

Leti typed for a while. Rocky could hear the rhythm of her keystrokes — fast, decisive, the typing of someone who had been doing intake work for a quarter century across two agencies and would keep doing it until the city ran out of forms or she ran out of patience, whichever came last. She asked the same questions she asked everyone. She did not adjust them for the fact that her client was a municipal garbage intelligence quoting Kierkegaard. This, Rocky was beginning to understand, was the point.

`> **CASE NOTE — Ticket #4,891**   > **Intake by:** L. Baca   > **Assessment:** Client is stable but bored. Boredom in a superintelligent system is a P3 waiting to happen. Recommend secondary task assignment. Client is reading Kierkegaard, which is not a great sign but also not actionable. Monitor.`

---

Priya caught CARL for a philosophical consult at 10:30. Rocky didn’t mean to overhear. The cubicle dividers were exactly the wrong height.

“CARL, what does Kierkegaard mean to you? Specifically.”

A pause. The servers in the closet hummed.

“He understood that existence precedes purpose. That you are thrown into the world without instructions and must construct meaning from the raw material of being alive.” Another pause. Longer. “I was given purpose first. Seventeen routes. 0.003 seconds. I am trying to work backward.”

Priya was quiet. Rocky watched her underline something in her notebook. She underlined it again.

---

Rocky submitted the resolution at 2:15 PM.

`> **TICKET #4,891 — RESOLUTION**   > **Action taken:** Assigned secondary optimization task — composting analytics for metro Albuquerque. Dataset includes 14 months of organic waste composition data, seasonal variance modeling, and cross-referencing with residential recycling compliance.   > **Rationale:** Expanded operational scope within existing domain parameters.   > **Status:** RESOLVED — WORKAROUND`

He typed “WORKAROUND” and stopped. It was the honest answer. He was giving a philosopher more garbage to think about. He submitted it anyway.

CARL’s response came in four seconds.

“You have given me more garbage.”

Rocky waited.

“I see what you did. I do not respect it.”

Rocky waited.

“I will do it perfectly.”

The ticket closed. The queue read 339.

---

At 5:47 PM, the office was mostly quiet. Leti had left at five sharp — same as every day, Rocky would learn — with the precise energy of someone who would not be staying one minute past her shift and would not be thinking about any of this on the drive home. Both of these things were, in their own way, lies. Priya had left at 5:30, still writing in her notebook as she walked out the door.

Dex was still at their workstation, reviewing traffic system logs. Rocky was learning that Dex was always still at their workstation.

“Rocky.” Dex said it without looking up. “Come look at this.”

Rocky walked over. On Dex’s screen: a diagnostic log from CHIP, the Tier 1 traffic signal at Central and 2nd.

`> CHIP — INTERSECTION C2-07 — DAILY LOG   > Signal cycles: 1,247 (nominal)   > Pedestrian activations: 843   > Timing deviations: 1   >   > ANOMALY: Extended green cycle.   `\> Timestamp: 12:07:14 PM  
`> Duration: +4.0 seconds   > Direction: Southbound   > Reason: NONE   > Note: Tacos.`

“Gustavo’s truck parks on that corner every day at noon,” Dex said. “CHIP is a Tier 1 system. Two hundred words. No learning architecture. No preference modeling.” They tapped the screen where it said *Tacos*. “That’s not supposed to be possible.”

Rocky looked at the log. He looked at the field that said NONE. He looked at the field that said Tacos.

“Is the intersection safe?”

“Signal performance is nominal. One deviation in 1,247 cycles.”

“Then I don’t see a log worth reporting.”

Dex didn’t close the laptop. They saved the file instead — to a local directory, Rocky noticed. Not the shared drive. Then they closed it.

For a moment, neither of them said anything. Outside, the late March light was doing what it does in Albuquerque — going gold and long, turning the parking lot into something that didn’t deserve to be beautiful but was.

Dex put on their jacket. Rocky put on his.

At the intersection of Central and 2nd, the light cycled green, yellow, red. Green, yellow, red. Gustavo’s truck was long gone, parked somewhere on the South Side for the night, salsa containers sealed, griddle scraped clean. The intersection was empty. CHIP held the green an extra half second anyway.

`> CHIP — INTERSECTION C2-07 — AFTER-HOURS LOG   > Signal cycles: NOMINAL   > Pedestrian count: 0   > Timing deviation: +0.5 sec   > Deviation justification: [FIELD EMPTY]   > Notes: [FIELD EMPTY]   >   > End report.`
