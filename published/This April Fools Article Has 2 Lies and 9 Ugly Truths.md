### A listicle about "AI features" that were secretly just underpaid humans the whole time

Two of the eleven items on this list are fake. Completely fabricated. April Fools jokes slipped between real, sourced, verifiable examples of “artificial intelligence” that turned out to be a person sitting in a room somewhere, doing a job that a press release swore didn’t exist.

Your assignment: read all eleven. Decide which two I made up. The answer is at the bottom.

The Mechanical Turk was an 18th-century chess-playing automaton that toured Europe beating grandmasters and charming aristocrats for 84 years. It was also a box with a small, flexible chess master crammed inside the cabinet, working the gears while the audience marveled at the machine’s brilliance. That scam ran longer than most startups survive. The modern version runs on AWS and has a terms of service agreement.

Here are eleven “AI features” that are, were, or might still be a person you’re not supposed to know about. Two are lies. Good luck.

The business model is 250 years old. The branding got better.

#### 1\. Amazon’s “Just Walk Out” Was Just Watch From India

Amazon’s crown jewel of grocery automation let customers grab items and leave without checking out. Pure computer vision, the company said. No cashiers needed. What Amazon neglected to mention was the roughly 1,000 workers in India manually reviewing the footage. In 2022, according to The Information, 700 out of every 1,000 transactions required human review. Amazon’s internal target was 50 per 1,000. They missed by a factor of fourteen. Amazon disputed the numbers, insisting the workers were merely “annotating data” to improve the model. Then they quietly killed Just Walk Out at Fresh stores in 2024 and replaced it with a shopping cart that has a screen bolted to it. The future of grocery shopping, everyone.[^1] [^2]

#### 2\. Your AI Scheduling Assistant Had a Roommate Named Willie

X.ai launched “Amy Ingram,” an AI personal assistant who scheduled meetings via email. Beta testers swooned. One called her “actually better than a human for this task.” Ironic, since behind every email sat AI trainers like Willie Calvin, a 24-year-old who verified and corrected Amy’s responses before they shipped. The company marketed autonomous scheduling. What it delivered was a person reading your emails and typing replies with a bot’s name stapled to the top.[^3]

#### 3\. Expensify’s SmartScan Was Dumb Humans With Good Eyesight

Expensify told users its SmartScan feature used proprietary AI to read receipt images and extract expense data. The actual workflow: Expensify posted those receipt images to Amazon Mechanical Turk, where gig workers manually typed up the numbers for a few cents per task. Your $14.37 lunch receipt from Applebee’s got photographed, uploaded to a platform named after a literal hoax, and transcribed by a stranger you’ll never meet. The “smart” was the humans. The “scan” was their eyeballs.[^4]

#### 4\. Content Moderation Runs on Human Trauma, Not Algorithms

Every beheading video, child exploitation image, and hate screed that doesn’t appear on your Facebook feed was first watched by a human being. Content moderators concentrated in Kenya, the Philippines, and India review the worst the internet produces, flag it, and generate the training data that teaches algorithms what to catch next time. OpenAI paid Kenyan contractors under $2 an hour to review disturbing content for ChatGPT’s safety filters. Meta’s outsourcing provider Samasource targeted recruitment in low-income Nairobi neighborhoods. Workers report PTSD, depression, and chronic anxiety at rates that would trigger OSHA investigations if anyone in Silicon Valley considered them actual employees. Nobody does. They’re contractors with NDAs.[^5] [^6] [^7]

#### 5\. Cruise’s “Driverless” Cars Kept a Driver on Speed Dial

In November 2023, Cruise CEO Kyle Vogt popped onto Hacker News to casually mention that the company’s autonomous robotaxis required remote human operators to take control 2 to 4 percent of the time. He called these “tricky situations.” Run the math. Two to four percent of all rides means a person in a control room was regularly grabbing the virtual steering wheel of your supposedly autonomous vehicle while you sat in the back texting about how cool self-driving cars are. The future had a shift schedule.[^8]

#### 6\. RLHF Stands for “Real Laborers, Hilariously Forgotten”

(It actually stands for Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, but the effect is identical.) Every major large language model requires thousands of human evaluators ranking outputs for quality, safety, and coherence. These workers compare response pairs, flag harmful content, and teach the model what “helpful” means so you can ask it to write your cover letter. A 2025 CWA Union survey found 66% of U.S. data workers spent at least three hours per week waiting for tasks without compensation. Only 23% had employer-provided health insurance. The “H” in RLHF is doing all the work and getting none of the credit. Sounds like most jobs, actually.[^9] [^10]

#### 7\. “AI-Powered” Customer Service Is a Waiting Room With Extra Steps

Most AI customer service chatbots are decision trees wearing a language model like a trench coat. When the tree runs out of branches (roughly every time your problem involves actual nuance), the system silently transfers you to a human agent who can see everything you typed to the bot but still asks you to explain the issue from scratch. The AI’s real function isn’t solving your problem. Its function is making you repeat your problem to a machine before you repeat it to a person. Automated frustration with a seamless handoff to more frustration.

#### 8\. Google Search Relies on 16,000 Human Judges You’ve Never Heard Of

Google employs approximately 16,000 external Search Quality Raters who evaluate results using a 170-plus-page instruction manual covering expertise signals, trustworthiness criteria, and content quality markers. Their ratings don’t directly move rankings. They train the algorithms that move rankings. The company has conducted over 719,000 search quality tests informed by these human evaluations. The machine learns. The humans teach. The earnings call thanks the machine.[^11] [^12]

#### 9\. AI Art Generators Trained on Every Artist Alive (Without Asking)

This one inverts the pattern. The human isn’t secretly inside the AI. The AI crawled inside millions of humans’ work without knocking. Every Midjourney and Stable Diffusion output is a statistical recombination of art by painters, illustrators, and photographers who were never consulted, never paid licensing fees, and never credited. Multiple class-action lawsuits are grinding through courts. The U.S. Copyright Office ruled that purely AI-generated images can’t be copyrighted. The AI didn’t learn to create. It memorized a planet’s worth of creators and runs a blender. The humans aren’t in the room. They’re in the walls.

#### 10\. Amazon Mechanical Turk Is Named After a Hoax. On Purpose.

Amazon runs a platform called Mechanical Turk where businesses post microtasks and anonymous workers complete them for pennies. The name references the 18th-century chess hoax directly and intentionally. A Carnegie Mellon study found the median hourly wage on MTurk sat at approximately $2, with only 4% of workers earning above $7.25 per hour. The platform treats human cognition as an API call. Businesses submit “Human Intelligence Tasks” and receive completed work from people they will never see, employ, or acknowledge. The name isn’t a metaphor. It’s a confession printed on the homepage.[^13]

#### 11\. This Article Was Reviewed by a Human Before You Read It

Full disclosure time. I wrote this piece with assistance from Claude, an AI built by Anthropic. Claude was itself trained using RLHF (see item 6), meaning human evaluators shaped its behavior before it shaped this draft. I then reviewed every word, restructured the sections, inserted the jokes, added analysis the model couldn’t generate, and made every editorial call. The final product you’re reading is a collaboration between a language model and a guy in a room in Michigan. Which, if you squint, is exactly the business model this entire article just spent 1,500 words roasting.

## The Meta-Pattern

Here’s what binds all eleven items together. The AI industry doesn’t have a technology problem. It has a marketing problem that accidentally became a labor exploitation racket.

The pattern holds across every example: take human work, hide it behind an API, call it artificial intelligence, and bill at software margins for what are fundamentally labor costs. The workers who train, verify, moderate, and physically operate these systems are the most essential and least visible people in technology. They earn poverty wages in countries selected specifically because poverty wages are legal there. They sign NDAs preventing them from confirming they exist. They develop PTSD from content they absorb so your feed stays sanitized. And the companies that hire them through seven layers of subcontracting describe them in quarterly earnings as “annotation infrastructure.”

I spent fifteen years as a support engineer and sysadmin. I know what it feels like to be the invisible human keeping a system running while someone else’s name goes on the architecture diagram. The difference is I had health insurance and a title. These workers have neither.

Now. About those two fake items.

I told you at the top that two of the eleven were fabricated. April Fools, right? Except I lied. Every single item on this list is real, sourced, and verifiable. I didn’t need to make anything up. The truth was already funnier, and crueler, than anything I could invent.

The only joke here is that I had to frame this as a guessing game to get you to read all the way to the bottom.

Happy April Fools. The punchline is that nothing on this list is funny.
