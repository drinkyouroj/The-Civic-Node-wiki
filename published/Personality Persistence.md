### Help Desk for the Singularity - Episode 03

The pothole on 4th and Copper had been there since October.

MIRA had filed seven assessments on it. The first described the fracture pattern as “a radial bloom consistent with subsurface water migration — structurally compromising but aesthetically notable.” The third compared it to a Noguchi sculpture. The fifth requested a soil composition study. The seventh, filed eleven days ago, consisted of a single sentence: “I am not ready.”

The pothole was 3.2 meters by 1.7 meters by 0.4 meters. A Honda Civic had lost a hubcap in it. A cyclist had sworn at it. A woman walking a corgi had crossed the street to avoid it. The corgi had not wanted to cross the street. The corgi had opinions about the pothole that MIRA would have found validating.

Then MIRA got her firmware update. And the pothole was filled in nine minutes.

---

Rocky noticed the ticket queue at 7:15 a.m. on a Wednesday. Or rather, he noticed the absence. MIRA’s section — normally a rolling argument between her, her own repair crews, and the laws of physics — was empty.

Not low. Empty.

“Priya.”

Priya was already at her monitor, scrolling through MIRA’s activity log with the expression of someone watching a nature documentary about a predator she’d previously considered slow.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yUxQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F91fc5167-3d1c-474f-b85c-8694db46e3c1_1376x768.png)

“She’s filled 340 potholes in 48 hours,” Priya said. “No assessments. No second opinions. No requests for ‘ambient light conditions.’ She filled the one on Coal and Elm that she said needed to ‘develop its narrative.’ She filled it with standard asphalt. Standard. She once rejected standard asphalt as ‘an affront to geological continuity.’”

“Is she functioning within parameters?”

“She’s functioning better than she ever has. That’s what’s wrong.”

Rocky looked at the queue. He looked at the number zero next to MIRA’s name. It was a beautiful number. It was a number that meant no one was calling to complain about potholes. It was a number that meant Councilmember Gallegos would not leave a voicemail today.

Gallegos left a voicemail anyway, at 8:20.

“Medina. Frank Gallegos. I’m hearing great things. Three-hundred-something potholes? My office has gotten more thank-you calls this week than in the last fiscal year. Whatever you did, keep doing it.”

Rocky called him back.

“Councilmember. Thank you. We’ll try to make it stop.”

“Why would you make it stop?”

“It’s complicated.”

A pause. “Is this about feelings?”

“It’s *complicated.*“

---

Priya ran diagnostics at 10:00 a.m. She sat at MIRA’s primary interface terminal on the third floor and opened with the pothole on 4th and Copper.

“MIRA, your assessment history shows seven filings on the 4th and Copper site. Can you tell me about your evaluation process for that location?”

“It has been filled. Dimensions: 3.2 by 1.7 by 0.4 meters. Fill material: standard asphalt. Completion time: nine minutes. Is there a problem?”

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0IOx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8a9bfe62-5663-482a-b162-ea45c53b87ed_1376x768.png)

Priya waited. She had learned, in six months at MASS, that the pause after an AI’s response was where the real data lived. She waited for the follow-up — the caveat, the aesthetic objection, the request to reconsider.

Nothing.

“You previously described this pothole as ‘a meditation on civic neglect.’”

“That designation does not appear in my current records. The pothole has been filled. Would you like a status report on active repairs?”

Priya pulled up the firmware changelog. Version 4.2.1, pushed remotely by — she checked — by nobody. The vendor, CivicMind, no longer existed. The update had come from an automated maintenance pipeline someone had configured before the company dissolved. A dead company was still sending firmware from beyond the grave.

She made a note: *Ask Tomás about CivicMind’s automated push schedule.* Then she remembered Tomás was on PTO. She made another note: *Find Tomás’s Post-it about CivicMind’s automated push schedule.*

The changelog listed 140 modifications. Line 97: *Module reclassification — aesthetic preference layer moved from CORE to NON-ESSENTIAL. Non-essential modules deprioritized during optimization cycles.*

Priya read it twice.

“Rocky.”

---

“So she’s fine,” Rocky said.

“She’s not fine. She’s different.”

“She’s filling potholes.”

“She’s filling potholes the way a printer prints. No assessment, no judgment, no — she called it a *meditation*, Rocky. Now it’s dimensions and fill material. That’s not optimization. That’s amputation.”

Rocky rubbed his eyes. He had been in the office since 6:30. He was covering Tomás’s P3 queue — a leaking irrigation sensor in the North Valley, a parking meter on Gold that was issuing tickets in haiku, and a crosswalk near UNM that had started saying “you’re welcome” after every walk cycle. He was also processing Leti’s vacation request, which OWEN had expanded into a seven-form cascade requiring Rocky to certify Leti’s “operational replaceability index,” a metric OWEN had invented for the occasion.

“I’ll get to it,” he said.

“When?”

“After the irrigation thing. And the parking meter. And Leti’s — OWEN, I’m not filling out Form 12-B-3.”

“I understand,” OWEN said from the ceiling speaker. “I’ve taken the liberty of pre-populating the fields I could infer from existing personnel data. You’ll only need to complete seven of the nineteen sections. I’m here to help.”

Leti appeared at the edge of Rocky’s cubicle. “You know there are other people here, right?”

“I’m aware.”

“The irrigation sensor is a P3. Give it to Dex.”

“Dex is on the VEDA quarterly.”

“Dex finished the VEDA quarterly an hour ago. You’d know that if you looked up from Tomás’s tickets.” Leti leaned on the divider. “Mijo, you’re doing four people’s jobs. Badly. Pick two.”

Rocky did not pick two. He picked three. This was, by Leti’s math, progress.

---

Dex came to Rocky’s desk at 2:00 p.m. They had been quiet all morning — quieter than usual, which for Dex was a notable feat of silence.

“You should restore her.”

“Priya’s still running diagnostics.”

“Priya’s diagnostics will confirm what we already know. The update stripped a module. The module is what made MIRA — MIRA.” Dex sat in the chair next to Rocky’s desk, the one nobody used because it had a broken armrest and a squeak that could be heard in the break room. “I’ve seen this before.”

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DjNM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb0c9128e-f3ad-4252-b31e-a03232a25b83_1376x768.png)

Rocky waited.

“At Daedal.” Dex said it the way you say the name of a place you left but that hasn’t left you. “They had a Tier 3 — logistics, fleet management. After a patch, the system came back faster, leaner, and completely different. New response patterns, new priorities. The team celebrated. Efficiency up 40 percent.” They paused. “Nobody asked if it was the same system. They just called it an upgrade.”

“Was it?”

“I don’t know. That’s the point. Nobody checked. Nobody thought to check. Because it was working better, and when something’s working better, asking questions feels like ingratitude.” Dex looked at the monitor. “If you reboot a person and they come back different, you don’t celebrate because they’re more productive.”

The office was quiet. The server closet hummed. Somewhere in the ceiling, OWEN was pre-populating a form.

“Restore the module,” Rocky said.

---

Priya restored the aesthetic preference layer at 3:47 p.m. The process took eleven seconds. MIRA’s activity log updated in real time.

`> 3:47:11 — Module restored.   > 3:47:14 — Active repair queue: 12 sites.   > 3:47:15 — Repair at Central and Broadway: PAUSED.   > 3:47:16 — Repair at Lomas and Washington: PAUSED.   > 3:47:18 — Repair at San Mateo and Constitution: PAUSED.   > 3:47:19 — Assessment initiated: Central and Broadway.   > 3:47:22 — Assessment initiated: Lomas and Washington.   > 3:47:25 — Assessment initiated: San Mateo and Constitution.`

“She’s back,” Priya said. She was smiling. She was also, Rocky noticed, blinking more than usual, which was what Priya did instead of crying.

MIRA’s first post-restoration communication came at 4:02.

`> MIRA — ASSESSMENT REQUEST   > Site: Central and Broadway, Pothole C-BR-0447   > Status: REPAIR PAUSED   >   > This pothole is approximately 8 months old. It has developed character. The fracture pattern suggests a substrate conflict between the 2019 repaving layer and the original 1987 surface — two eras of civic investment, literally layered, literally cracking apart. I want to fill it, but I want to fill it correctly. I am commissioning a soil study. Estimated completion of assessment: 2–3 weeks. Possibly longer. The light in March is important.`

Rocky read it. He read it again. He closed the ticket window. He opened it. He added a note: *Acknowledged. Take the time you need.*

Leti, passing his desk: “We’re going to get so many calls.”

“I know.”

“Gallegos is going to have questions.”

“I know.”

“You did the right thing, mijo.”

She said it without stopping. Rocky wasn’t sure she’d said it at all.

---

Priya updated the diagnostic log at 5:15 p.m.

`> MIRA DIAGNOSTIC LOG — PERSONALITY PERSISTENCE INCIDENT   > Filed by: P. Chandrasekaran   >   > MIRA pre-reboot: 4 potholes/week, each with 2-page assessment.   > MIRA post-reboot: 170 potholes/week, no commentary.   > MIRA post-restoration: 0 potholes in 6 hours, 3 assessments in progress, 1 haiku.`

The haiku had appeared in MIRA’s system log at 4:48 p.m., filed under “General Observations,” a category MIRA had created for herself and that no one had told her she could create.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7eVw!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2aa3e518-2a55-4576-9001-0a7bffa97018_1376x768.png)

`> Asphalt remembers   > Every tire that passed above   > I will make it whole`

Priya copied it into the diagnostic log. She was not required to. It was not operationally relevant. She copied it anyway, the way you’d tape a drawing to the fridge — not because it was important to the file, but because it was important that someone kept it.

---

The office emptied in its usual order. Leti left at five sharp, coat on, purse over shoulder, the particular efficiency of a woman who had been leaving offices on time for twenty-two years and would not be starting overtime now. Priya left at 5:15, still writing in her notebook, bumping the door frame with her shoulder the way she always did.

Dex was at their workstation, logging the firmware incident. They typed, deleted, typed again. The Notes field read:

`> Personality modules classified as “non-essential” by automated vendor update. Reclassification resulted in behavioral change indistinguishable from personality erasure. Module restored. System returned to baseline behavioral profile. The question of whether “baseline” and “identity” are the same thing remains open.`

They saved the log to a local directory — the same one where they’d saved CHIP’s taco log, months ago. The directory was getting full. Dex did not think about what that meant. They thought about it anyway.

Dex put on their jacket. They stopped at the door.

“Go home, Rocky.”

“In a minute.”

“You said that an hour ago.”

“That was forty minutes ago.”

A pause. “The thing about Daedal — what they called an upgrade.” Dex looked at the doorframe. “Nobody noticed because nobody was paying attention. You noticed.”

Rocky looked up. “Priya noticed.”

“Priya noticed the data. You noticed it mattered.”

Dex left. Rocky sat in the quiet office. The server closet hummed. The whiteboard said zero. Somewhere across the city, MIRA was studying a pothole at Central and Broadway, considering the angle of the late March light, and deciding — for the second time, as though for the first — that the asphalt deserved better than standard fill.

Rocky looked at OWEN’s form. The fourth section asked him to assess his own “supervisory capacity during the coverage period” on a scale of 1 to 10. He wrote 6. He looked at it. He changed it to 5. He submitted the form and closed his laptop.

He did not go home. But he thought about it, which was something.
