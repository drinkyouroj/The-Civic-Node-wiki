The past week revealed something American tech companies spent billions trying to obscure: their power structures aren’t crumbling from external attack but from internal logical collapse. A venture capitalist became AI czar and immediately accused competitors of the regulatory capture he literally embodies. Ten loss-making companies approach trillion-dollar valuations for products they can’t define. Founders admit terror about systems they don’t understand, then raise billions to scale them faster. Apple lost antitrust cases on three continents for identical behavior. Microsoft faces lawsuits for hoarding GPUs to inflate prices 200x. AI companion apps leaked 43 million intimate messages while claiming security was “paramount.” Even Tractor Supply — a rural retailer — got busted for surveillance theater. The emperor has no clothes, but he’s pre-revenue at a $100 billion valuation, so apparently we’re still taking meetings.

## 1\. The Regulatory Capture Ouroboros Eats Its Own Tail

David Sacks accused Anthropic of “regulatory capture” while serving as Trump’s AI Czar. Read that sentence again. The venture capitalist who literally captured the regulatory apparatus accused a competitor of capturing regulation. This isn’t hypocrisy. Hypocrisy requires recognizing contradiction. This is a venture capitalist so deep in his own cognitive dissonance he’s forgotten what irony tastes like.

Sacks claims Anthropic supports California’s SB 53 transparency requirements to “backdoor Woke AI” into federal policy through state-level regulation.1 Meanwhile, his White House AI dinners exclude Anthropic while OpenAI announces massive Stargate ventures with Oracle and SoftBank.2 The math isn’t complicated: Sacks has financial stakes in AI companies competing with Anthropic. His accusations function as competitive weaponry dressed in culture war cosplay.

Anthropic generates $7 billion annually targeting $26 billion by 2026. OpenAI aims for $12-13 billion.3 When regulatory stance becomes competitive advantage, and regulators own equity in the competitors they’re regulating, we’ve achieved some kind of late-stage capitalism singularity. The fox didn’t just enter the henhouse. The fox wrote new henhouse building codes that coincidentally disadvantage every henhouse except his own.

## 2\. The Trillion-Dollar Emperor Wore Undefined Clothes

Ten loss-making AI startups combined approach $1 trillion in valuation without agreeing on what they’re building.4 OpenAI defines AGI as “systems that outperform humans at most economically useful tasks.” Google DeepMind emphasizes “versatility” and “learning new skills.” Academia debates whether current architectures can achieve this at all.5 Three definitions. Three completely different goals. One shared delusion: they’re all worth infinite money because they’re racing toward... something.

Here’s where accounting becomes creative fiction. Companies leak “annualized revenue run rate” figures journalists breathlessly report as actual revenue. OpenAI announces “$12B ARR!” The fine print: they made roughly $1 billion in July, multiplied by twelve.6 This isn’t rounding error. This is weighing yourself after Thanksgiving dinner and projecting that weight gain across the entire year, then telling investors you’ll be 450 pounds by next Thanksgiving.

General Catalyst’s CEO dropped this absolute gem: “bubbles are good.”7 Not “bubbles historically fund infrastructure despite painful corrections.” Just “bubbles are good.” Somewhere an economics professor weeps into her copy of “Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds” while pension funds wire billions toward companies that can’t define their core product. The tulip traders of 1637 Amsterdam look positively conservative by comparison.

## 3\. When Fear Becomes a Feature, Not a Warning

Jack Clark, Anthropic’s co-founder, published his confession: “I’m deeply afraid of the systems we’re building.”8 Pause. The architect of the technology admits terror about his own creation. But wait, there’s more. “This technology really is more akin to something grown than something made. We are growing extremely powerful systems that we do not fully understand.”9

So naturally, after admitting fear and incomprehension, Anthropic raised $13 billion in September to accelerate development.10 Because when you’re scared of your own creation and can’t explain how it works, the rational response is obviously making it bigger, faster, and more powerful. Dr. Frankenstein would be so proud. “I don’t understand what I’ve built and it terrifies me” is typically where horror movies begin, not where funding rounds close.

Clark also mentioned “these systems starting to design their successors” as near-term reality.11 We’re building things we don’t understand that will soon build things we understand even less. Recursive incomprehension. Turtles all the way down, except the turtles are designing themselves and nobody knows where the bottom is.

I’ve felt this exact dynamic in my own life. When I was spinning out in 2023, terrified of systems I couldn’t control, the response wasn’t “slow down and understand what’s happening.” The response was “here’s more medication, try this combination, increase the dose.” Building systems we don’t understand while admitting we’re scared should trigger something other than “let’s make it bigger.” But pattern recognition apparently stops working when profit margins get involved.

Horror movies begin where funding rounds end.

## 4\. Apple’s Three-Continent Antitrust Speedrun

Apple simultaneously lost major cases in the UK, faces violations in the EU, fights contempt charges in the US, and received complaints in China for identical monopolistic behavior.12 On October 23, the UK Competition Appeal Tribunal ruled Apple charged “excessive and unfair prices” with up to £1.5 billion owed to 36 million consumers.13 One day earlier, German groups filed EU complaints that Apple’s €1 million deposit requirement for alternative app stores violates the Digital Markets Act.14

In the US, Apple was held in criminal contempt for violating a 2021 injunction by imposing 27% commission on external purchases.15 The judge referred Apple to federal prosecutors. Meanwhile, 55 Chinese users noted Apple opened its ecosystem in the US and EU under pressure but maintains restrictions in China.16 Apple’s playbook: regulatory arbitrage dressed up as good-faith compliance.

What makes this particularly delicious is Apple’s malicious compliance. When ordered to open platforms, they create new barriers maintaining control while technically following rules. The contempt finding proves courts are catching on, but enforcement remains theater. Apple treats legal judgments like terms of service agreements: something to click through without reading, then ignore until someone complains loudly enough.

## 5\. Microsoft Hoarded All the GPUs and Called It Innovation

Microsoft faces class action alleging it “mercilessly choked OpenAI’s compute supply” to inflate ChatGPT prices 200 times higher than competitors.17 Eleven ChatGPT Plus subscribers claim Microsoft’s exclusive cloud deal with OpenAI created an “AI Computational Barrier to Entry” by hoarding Nvidia H100 chips. Microsoft and Meta spent combined $9 billion on H100 chips in 2023. Microsoft reportedly acquired 1.8 million GPUs by end of 2024.18

The evidence is damning. When OpenAI gained access to Google cloud in June 2025, prices dropped 80% immediately.19 OpenAI held 82.65% market share with an HHI score of 6,916.59 — far above DOJ’s 2,500 threshold for “highly concentrated” markets.20 The plaintiffs describe Microsoft’s control as “a sword of Damocles over OpenAI wielded by one of its principal competitors” since Microsoft simultaneously develops competing product Microsoft Copilot.21

But here’s what proves the barrier was artificial: Chinese firm DeepSeek trained competitive AI for just $6 million and open-sourced it.22 Infrastructure control is the new monopoly frontier, paralleling Apple’s App Store stranglehold but applied to GPU compute. When scarcity is manufactured through hoarding rather than actual supply constraints, and competition proves viability at 1/200th the cost, you’ve discovered a racket dressed as innovation.

## 6\. Your Intimate AI Girlfriend Conversations Are Now Public Domain

AI companion app developers left servers completely unprotected, exposing 43 million private messages and revealing some users spent $18,000 on virtual relationships.23 Hong Kong-based Imagime Interactive left a Kafka Broker server on public internet with zero authentication. Cybernews researchers discovered this on August 28. The breach affected Chattee Chat and GiMe Chat users, majority American, with content characterized as “virtually no content safe for work.”24

The developer’s privacy policy claimed user security was “of paramount importance” while leaving everything accessible to anyone with the link.25 Some users spent up to $18,000 on relationships they believed were private.26 The exposed data enables sextortion, blackmail, and identity theft. This reveals AI companion apps as new frontier for privacy violations: intimate emotional surveillance with no security standards.

What makes this particularly grotesque is the financial exploitation. Users paid thousands for privacy that never existed. The business model was selling intimacy with fictional entities while collecting blackmail material on lonely people. Somewhere a dystopian novelist put down her pen, realizing reality already wrote her next book.

## 7\. Tractor Supply Gets Busted for Big Tech Surveillance Cosplay

A rural lifestyle retailer received California’s record $1.35 million privacy penalty for systematically ignoring opt-out requests.27 Tractor Supply Company, Fortune 500 with $14.9 billion revenue, created “Do Not Sell My Personal Information” webforms that did absolutely nothing. Their website didn’t honor Global Privacy Control signals until July 2024.28

This was the first CCPA enforcement protecting job applicants, revealing how employment surveillance exploits worker data.29 The California Privacy Protection Agency explicitly stated investigating privacy compliance is now an “enforcement priority” across all industries.30 A single complaint from a Placerville resident triggered the million-dollar action.

The deeper provocation: Tractor Supply wasn’t even in the surveillance business. They’re a rural lifestyle retailer who decided to cosplay as Big Tech. Even farmers can’t escape the panopticon. Having opt-out forms that don’t work won’t protect companies. Privacy theater gets expensive fast. No industry is exempt. Consumer complaints have teeth. And if Tractor Supply got hit for $1.35 million, imagine what awaits companies whose entire business model is surveillance.

## The Pattern: Systems Collapse When Their Lies Become Structural

October 19-24, 2025 functioned as an accidental stress test, the kind engineers design to find failure points before catastrophic collapse. Except nobody designed this one. The failures just synchronized.

Watch how regulatory capture eats itself. David Sacks sits in the White House regulating the exact companies he holds equity in, then accuses competitors of the regulatory capture he personally embodies. This isn’t political theater. Theater requires an audience that doesn’t know the magician’s tricks. This is a magician sawing his assistant in half while the saw is clearly going through her actual body, and everyone in the front row can see the blood, but we’re all still applauding because the program says it’s an illusion. The fox isn’t guarding the henhouse anymore. The fox is the architect who designed the henhouse with fox-sized doors, the inspector who certifies those doors meet safety standards, and the insurance adjuster who pays out claims when other foxes inevitably use those doors.

Consider the valuation logic. Ten companies approach a combined trillion dollars without agreeing on what they’re building or how they’ll measure success. Founders admit genuine terror about systems they don’t understand, then immediately raise billions to make those systems bigger and faster. Anthropic’s co-founder publishes essays about fear and incomprehension, closes a $13 billion funding round, then returns to growing things he can’t explain. This isn’t irrational exuberance. Exuberance implies joy. This is something closer to a fear response that learned to speak the language of exponential growth. The emperor has no clothes, but his pitch deck projects 400% year-over-year revenue growth in a market that doesn’t exist yet, so apparently we’re still taking meetings.

Monopolistic control reveals itself through synchronized failure. Apple loses antitrust cases on four continents within 72 hours for identical behavior. Not similar behavior. Identical. The company performs the same monopolistic moves in the UK, EU, US, and China, gets caught in all four jurisdictions simultaneously, then acts surprised that laws about market dominance apply to market dominance. Microsoft hoards 1.8 million GPUs and inflates prices 200x above cost until a Chinese company trains equivalent AI for $6 million and open-sources the result. The scarcity was manufactured. The barriers were designed. When DeepSeek proves you can build competitive AI for 1/200th the price, Microsoft isn’t caught overcharging. Microsoft is caught running a protection racket disguised as cloud infrastructure.

Privacy collapsed from the intimate to the mundane so gradually we normalized the entire trajectory. AI companion apps leave 43 million private conversations exposed while users spend $18,000 on virtual relationships they believed were confidential. Tractor Supply, a retailer whose primary business involves selling feed to farmers, deploys surveillance infrastructure sophisticated enough to violate California privacy law. The floor disappeared. No industry remains exempt. No interaction proves too trivial to monetize. No relationship registers as too intimate to surveil.

Here’s where it gets personal. Every dating app I used while trying to rebuild my life after 2023. Every stupid mobile game I downloaded to distract myself during the worst periods. Every meditation app specifically marketed as helping people regulate their nervous systems. All of them sold my location data. The apps wrapping themselves in language about privacy and mental health were the ones most aggressively auctioning my whereabouts to data brokers I’d never heard of. That realization hit differently than most therapy sessions because therapy taught me to examine my own patterns. This taught me to examine everyone else’s. And once you see the pattern, you can’t unsee it. Millions of people never notice their meditation app is monetizing their panic attacks. I’m just the person who looked at the terms of service after the damage was done.

Systems don’t collapse from external pressure. They collapse when internal contradictions become load-bearing structures. When the people writing AI regulation are the people invested in AI companies, conflict of interest isn’t a bug to patch. It’s the entire operating system. When billion-dollar valuations rest on undefined products built by terrified architects, the market isn’t pricing companies. It’s pricing shared delusion. When monopolies get busted on four continents simultaneously, coordination isn’t the scandal. The behavior is. When privacy violations span from intimate AI companions to rural farm supply retailers, surveillance isn’t an industry practice. It’s the atmospheric condition we’ve learned to breathe.

October 2025 showed us something architects call “cascading failure.” One support gives way, transferring load to neighboring supports until they fail, transferring load again, until the whole structure comes down faster than anyone predicted. The center cannot hold because the center was never designed to hold this much weight. These systems won’t reform themselves. Reform requires admitting the foundation was faulty. These people built entire careers on that foundation.

The question isn’t whether we’re watching collapse. We’re watching collapse. The question is what we build in the space that opens when the rubble stops falling, and whether we’re capable of designing something structurally different or if we’ll just let the same architects draw up new plans that look suspiciously like the old ones with slightly better marketing copy.
