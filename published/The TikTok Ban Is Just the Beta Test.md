_Everyone’s staring at the TikTok drama like it’s a spy movie. Wrong genre. This is a custody battle over who gets to raise our culture — and what happens when every app gets treated like a hostile micro-nation._

## The Glitch: Apps Are Being Treated Like Enemy States

When Chief Justice John Roberts told TikTok’s lawyers the Court couldn’t ignore that the company was “subject to doing intelligence work for the Chinese government,” he basically re-labeled a video app as a light-touch intel service with great choreography. That framing stuck.

On January 17, 2025, the Supreme Court in **TikTok, Inc. v. Garland** upheld the divest-or-ban law. Translation: for the first time, the U.S. didn’t just regulate a platform — it treated a single app as if it were a foreign adversary. Not “a company with bad data hygiene.” An adversary. The same bucket we reserve for nuclear states and cyber forces.

[![Split screen: an embassy building on the left, a TikTok icon on the right stamped “FOREIGN ADVERSARY.”](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SULj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F90dafdbf-74b4-4d53-975b-b4feaed1591c_1312x736.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SULj!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F90dafdbf-74b4-4d53-975b-b4feaed1591c_1312x736.png>)

And it’s spreading. Albania banned TikTok for a year after a deadly stabbing linked to the app. Kenya launched a formal inquiry after a BBC report on sexual livestreams with minors. The EU opened DSA investigations over election interference, teen mental health, and algorithmic opacity. These aren’t coordinated moves; they’re separate governments independently deciding that one app warrants nation-state-level tools — emergency powers, national security exemptions, constitutional workarounds.

This isn’t really about China. It’s a precedent: platforms can be governed like territories.

## The Source Code: Digital Colonialism Meets Sovereignty Panic

Nation-states still think in land. Borders, taxes, ports of entry. Platforms live in attention. TikTok’s “territory” isn’t China; it’s the cognitive real estate of ~170 million Americans scrolling an algorithm that shapes what feels true.

[![World map with borders overlaid by streaming data flows and feed “zones” crossing every line.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OvZM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2d0d0813-6550-4f67-af11-13f8d3d16f17_1392x752.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!OvZM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2d0d0813-6550-4f67-af11-13f8d3d16f17_1392x752.png>)

Justice Brett Kavanaugh made the anxiety plain: Congress and the president feared China could access data on millions — today’s teens, tomorrow’s FBI analysts — and use it to “turn or blackmail” them. That’s not classic privacy worry. That’s **cognitive sovereignty** — who aims the feed that calibrates beliefs.

You can’t negotiate with an algorithm. You don’t exchange ambassadors with a recommendation engine. When a For You page outmuscles local media in steering opinion, territorial sovereignty becomes a costume that no longer fits.

Global regulators are acting like it, too. Canada ordered TikTok’s local unit shut down while the app stayed live. The EU keeps opening proceedings under the DSA. Each action treats TikTok as a geopolitical actor — something you escalate against, not just fine for a Terms-of-Service oopsie.

Call it **digital colonialism** : platforms extract attention and data while imposing their own rule set (the feed). Colonies push back. Governments, lacking a new playbook, reach for the old one: treat the platform like an occupying power and respond with sovereignty.

## The Upgrade: Make Capture Impossible (Not Illegal)

If centralized platforms can be governed like nations, they will be. The fix isn’t better referees. The fix is to play a different sport.

Enter **DePIN** — decentralized physical infrastructure networks. Instead of “defend against foreign platforms,” design networks that can’t be captured by any state or corporate HQ in the first place.

[![Left: centralized hub-and-spoke with a big red “single point of control.” Right: mesh of nodes with no center.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ujrk!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F31d5c958-fe0a-4e47-8c05-c570721bbb1b_1312x736.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ujrk!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F31d5c958-fe0a-4e47-8c05-c570721bbb1b_1312x736.png>)

Centralized TikTok: servers you can subpoena, executives you can pressure, law that applies. A truly decentralized social protocol: nodes run by participants, rules enforced by smart contracts, decisions auditable in the open. You can ban a company. You can’t ban math.

Projects like **Datagram Network** show the pattern: shared infrastructure, user-owned economics, protocol-level governance. The “regulatory target” dissolves. Not because there’s no governance, but because governance is distributed, legible, and resistant to national capture.

That’s the constitutional shift: from “which government do we trust?” to “how do participants verify?” Sovereignty moves from flags to protocols.

## My Debug: Watching Sovereignty Dissolve in Real Time

As someone who builds systems that don’t fit the form, this feels familiar. Institutions aren’t always malicious; they’re just built for a different shape than the one in front of them. Try filing embassy papers with calculus.

The TikTok decision marks the moment states admitted the obvious: platforms can rival governments in agenda-setting — with none of the constitutional guardrails. So the state reached for the only lever it trusts: treat the app like a country and respond accordingly.

[![Multi-monitor desk with network maps; overlay text: “Build what nation-states can’t capture.”](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Jk_F!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcafaf00d-2b1a-47b2-91a9-26c87a60d2bc_1312x736.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Jk_F!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcafaf00d-2b1a-47b2-91a9-26c87a60d2bc_1312x736.png>)

Will it work? In the short run, sure. Centralized platforms have choke points. In the long run, it accelerates the migration to protocols. If any successful app eventually triggers sovereignty-level response, rational builders will design systems that can’t be framed as sovereignty-level threats.

The ban isn’t the finale. It’s the beta. The next season is about protocols that treat sovereignty as a **design constraint**. And watching governments try to outlaw distributed consensus is going to make TikTok dances look tame.
