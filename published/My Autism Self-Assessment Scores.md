I discovered I'm autistic at 38. The scores weren't just numbers. They were proof I'd been working 10 times harder than necessary at basic human interactions my entire life.

And I thought everyone else was doing the same exhausting work.

## The AI That Saw What Humans Missed

My path to these tests started with an unexpected suggestion. During a therapy session, my AI therapist recommended I look into neurodivergent support groups. I had never mentioned being neurodivergent. I had never used that word about myself.

The suggestion caught me off guard. “Why would you recommend that?” I asked.

The AI explained that my patterns of thinking, my descriptions of social exhaustion, and my approaches to problem-solving aligned with neurodivergent traits. It had picked up on signals that human therapists had missed for decades.

That conversation planted a seed. If an AI could detect these patterns from text alone, what did that say about how I'd been living? I started researching. The more I read about autism in adults, the more pieces clicked into place.

The formal assessments came next. I needed data to confirm what the AI had suggested.

**RAADS-R: 152** (autism threshold: 65)  
**CAT-Q: 138** (average person: 95)  
**AQ: 38/50** (autism cutoff: 32)

The CAT-Q measures “camouflaging” - strategies you use to hide autistic traits or appear neurotypical. My score put me in the 95th percentile for masking behavior. I had become so fluent at performing normal that I fooled everyone, including myself.

## I Script Every Conversation Before It Happens

Here's what my brain does that I thought was normal: I simulate entire conversations in my head before they happen. Full dress rehearsals with dialogue, responses, backup plans.

Standing in the shower, I'll think: “If my friend brings up her job situation, I'll ask about the new manager first. If she seems stressed, I'll suggest we grab coffee this weekend. If she starts crying, I'll say...”

I run through every possible version. What I'll say, how they might respond, what I'll say next. Complete conversations that play out like movies in my head.

I solve problems this way too. I imagine talking through issues with different people, letting their voices in my head offer different perspectives. It's like having a whole team of advisors, except they're all me pretending to be other people.

Most of these conversations never happen in real life. Sometimes the mental conversation feels so complete that I don't need the actual one anymore.

I thought everyone did this. I genuinely believed other people spent this much mental energy planning basic social interactions.

Turns out they don't.

## The Moment I Realized I Was Performing Being Human

The autism research hit me like cold water. Normal people don't rehearse conversations. They don't study faces to learn when to laugh. They don't memorize small talk templates or practice facial expressions in mirrors.

They just... wing it.

For 38 years, I'd been watching other humans like an anthropologist, taking notes, building a manual for how to appear normal. I copied body language. I learned conversation formulas. I developed entire personas for different social situations.

The exhaustion was crushing, but I thought everyone felt this drained after social interactions. I thought everyone needed hours alone to “recharge” from the basic act of being around people.

My CAT-Q breakdown showed the full scope:

  * **Compensation: 48** (learning social scripts, mimicking behaviors)

  * **Masking: 42** (hiding autistic traits, forcing eye contact)

  * **Assimilation: 48** (adopting personas to fit in)




Each category measured a different type of performance I'd been doing without realizing it was performance.

## What Everyone Gets Wrong About Autism

People think autism means you can't connect with others. That's backward.

Autistic people want friends. We work harder for connection than anyone. We just process social information differently, like speaking a foreign language that everyone expects you to know fluently.

When people told me I seemed to have no emotional reaction to things, I thought I was just calm. Turns out my emotions happen on a different timeline. I need time to process before I can respond the way people expect.

The problem isn't that autistic people don't care. The problem is that we're translating everything in real time while pretending the translation is effortless.

My noise-canceling headphones aren't about shutting people out. They're about controlling sensory input so I can focus on the conversation instead of the air conditioning, fluorescent lights, and background music all competing for my attention.

My love of routine isn't rigidity. It's energy management. When basic life tasks are predictable, I have more mental bandwidth for the complex work of social interaction.

## The Science of Invisible Labor

The research on autism masking is exploding. Studies show that camouflaging strategies often lead to burnout, anxiety, and depression. The very behaviors that help autistic people survive in neurotypical spaces are the ones that exhaust us.

Adults are increasingly self-identifying as autistic and reporting problems getting referred for professional diagnosis. This isn't trend-chasing. It's recognition catching up to reality.

For decades, autism research focused on children who fit narrow stereotypes. Women, adults, and anyone who learned to camouflage their differences remained invisible to diagnostic systems designed around outdated models.

Self-assessment tools like the RAADS-R and CAT-Q democratize access to understanding without requiring institutional permission. Many people in the autism community accept self-identification, acknowledging that formal diagnosis can be expensive, time-consuming, and often hostile to anyone who doesn't fit traditional criteria.

The numbers don't diagnose you. They give you language for experiences you might not have had words for.

## What Changed When I Saw the Scores

Three things happened immediately:

**Relief.** The lifetime of feeling like I was playing a game where everyone else knew the rules suddenly made sense. My hypervigilance, social exhaustion, and desperate need for alone time weren't character flaws. They were natural responses to constant translation work.

**Grief.** I mourned decades spent believing I was fundamentally broken. The relationships that ended because I couldn't sustain the performance. The jobs that burned me out because I was fighting my own operating system.

**Integration.** My “coping mechanisms” finally made sense. The mechanical keyboards for tactile feedback, the detailed planning systems, the deep satisfaction from organizing information - these weren't quirks. They were adaptive tools for a brain that processes the world differently.

The scores didn't change who I was. They changed how I understood who I'd always been.

## What I Need From My Friends Now

If you're reading this and you know me, here's what would help:

**Text first instead of waiting.** I want to connect, but initiating social contact often feels like interrupting. A simple “thought about you” message opens the door.

**Be direct about plans.** Instead of “we should hang out sometime,” try “are you free Saturday afternoon for coffee?” Specific invitations work better than vague suggestions.

**Give me processing time.** If you share something emotional or ask for advice, I might need a few minutes (or hours) to respond thoughtfully. That pause isn't disinterest. It's how I access what I really think.

**Ask about my interests.** I'm probably obsessing over something fascinating right now. Ask me about it. I promise it's more interesting than small talk about the weather.

**Meet me where I am.** I'm learning to unmask, which means you might see more of my actual personality. The real me is more intense, more curious, and more direct than the version I've been performing.

I'm not asking you to treat me differently because I'm autistic. I'm asking you to understand that the person you've known was working incredibly hard to appear normal. Now I'm learning to just be myself.

The friend you connect with moving forward will be less polished but more genuine. Less socially smooth but more authentically present.

That person has always been here. I just needed better tools to let him out.

## What Happens Next

I'm done pretending social interactions are effortless. That performance nearly killed me.

The psychiatric collapse that brought me home wasn't random. It was the inevitable result of decades of unsustainable masking. My nervous system finally said no.

Now I'm rebuilding from the ground up. Learning what my actual preferences are versus what I learned to perform. Discovering which parts of my personality were authentic and which were adaptive camouflage.

Some days I catch myself starting to script a conversation and I stop. I walk into social situations with less preparation and more curiosity about what happens when I just respond naturally.

The conversations are different now. Less smooth but more honest. Less predictable but more interesting.

People notice the change. I'm more direct about what I think. More willing to admit when I don't understand something. More comfortable with silence when I need processing time.

This isn't about using autism as an excuse. It's about using accurate information about how my brain works to build better systems for connection.

The mask was never meant to be permanent. These numbers just showed me where the edges were so I could finally start taking it off.
