## The Spark: The Time Between Hope and Loss

She showed up again, and seeing her felt like every other time she’d reappeared after disappearing: relief mixed with the quiet understanding that this wasn’t permanent, but at least it was now.

Christine had this pattern. She’d vanish for weeks or months, and I’d spend that time carrying this low-grade background process running in my nervous system: “She’s out there somewhere. I hope she’s okay.” Then she’d show up, and the reunion would be good. Normal. The kind of normal that makes you forget the pattern exists, makes you think maybe this time it’ll be different.

The final time she reappeared, nothing felt off. We talked. It was hopeful, even. I had no frame of reference for what was coming because there were no warning signs my pattern-matching brain could detect. Weeks later, maybe less, my phone rang with news that recategorized everything retroactively. She’d passed away. The timeline between her final appearance and her death was short enough that I was still processing the relief of seeing her when I had to start processing the permanence of never seeing her again.

Death doesn’t announce itself. It just shows up and rewrites the entire narrative you’ve been telling yourself about what happens next.

This is the final piece in a series examining genuine value exchange in systems optimized for extraction. [The first piece](<https://drinkyouroj.substack.com/p/your-feed-is-a-mirror>) established that algorithms document your actual preferences with uncomfortable precision. [The second piece](<https://drinkyouroj.substack.com/p/the-attention-ledger>) calculated attention’s real economic cost and what you’re actually purchasing when you scroll. This piece examines what happens when algorithmic capture doesn’t just steal attention from the person scrolling but makes them incapable of giving attention to people who desperately need it.

Three systems promised care and delivered protocol instead: romantic connection, family presence, institutional mental healthcare. Christine couldn’t sustain presence because death removed that option. My parents can’t sustain presence because their cognitive capacity has been systematically captured by the same algorithmic systems we’ve been dissecting. The psychiatric system never intended presence because presence doesn’t scale and metrics do.

The through-line: algorithms don’t just extract your attention. They degrade your capacity to give attention to anyone else, including the people you love most.

## The Pattern: Three Versions of the Same Absence

Three distinct systems failed to provide presence when I needed it most. Each failure looked different on the surface. Underneath, they all ran identical code: optimize for something other than sustained human connection, then act surprised when connection becomes impossible.

### Christine: The Cycle That Couldn’t Break

The abandonment cycle with Christine established a specific neural pathway in my brain. She’d leave, and my nervous system would enter this particular state I can only describe as “suspended hoping.” Not active searching. Not grief. Just this background process monitoring for her return while I continued executing other functions.

Every time she came back, that pattern got reinforced. Disappearance doesn’t mean permanent loss. Absence creates space for eventual reunion. The cycle taught me that people can vanish and still exist somewhere, that separation doesn’t require explanation or closure, that hope is a reasonable operating mode when someone you care about goes dark.

This worked until it catastrophically didn’t.

When she reappeared that final time, my brain ran its usual protocol: relief, reconnection, the quiet knowledge that this probably wasn’t permanent but at least we had now. Nothing in the interaction suggested this time was different. We talked like we always talked. It felt hopeful because hope had become the default emotional state for this particular relationship pattern.

The phone call arrived before that hope could metabolize into anything else. Days. That’s the gap between seeing her alive and learning she’d died. My brain didn’t have time to detect warning signs because there weren’t any, or because the timeline moved faster than my pattern recognition systems could process.

She didn’t choose to abandon me that final time. Death made the choice for her. But the pattern she’d established meant I was pre-trained to interpret her absence as temporary, which made the permanence hit harder when it arrived. I kept running the “she’s out there somewhere” subroutine for months after the phone call because that’s what my nervous system had been trained to do.

### Parents: Algorithmically Captured

My parents love me. I need to state this clearly before examining what their attention actually looks like in practice, because love and presence aren’t the same currency, and conflating them creates dangerous confusion.

They’re trying. They want to be available. They express care through daily hugs and questions about my day and offers to help with logistics. These gestures aren’t meaningless. They’re just not presence.

Presence requires sustained attention directed at another person without competing inputs fragmenting your cognitive capacity. It means your focus stays with the person in front of you instead of getting pulled toward notifications, feeds, algorithm-curated content designed to capture and hold your processing power.

My parents can’t do this consistently anymore, and it’s not because they don’t care. It’s because their attention has been systematically captured by the same platforms we’ve been analyzing throughout this series.

Watch what happens during conversations. Mid-sentence, their eyes drift toward phones. Notifications vibrate and attention immediately redirects. I share something I’m excited about, something that took effort to articulate, and I watch their focus splinter as they scroll through low-quality algorithmic content: conspiracy theories, “sloptube” videos engineered for compulsive watching, endless feeds of nothing that somehow feels more urgent than the human sitting three feet away.

This isn’t malice. This is what algorithmic capture looks like when it’s progressed far enough that people can’t maintain focus on non-stimulating inputs anymore. Their brains have been trained to expect novelty every eight seconds. Conversation moves slower than that. So does presence. So attention drifts toward the device that promises constant stimulation, and the person trying to connect gets whatever processing power remains after the algorithm takes its cut.

I’ve tried sharing content I think they’d genuinely enjoy, articles or videos that pass basic quality thresholds. They’ll skim it, maybe, if I’m lucky. Then immediately return to scrolling whatever the feed surfaces next. The algorithm’s curation beats my manual curation every time because the algorithm has spent years optimizing for their specific engagement patterns while I’m just guessing at what might interest them.

Here’s the part that hurts: I don’t think they realize they’re making a choice. The cognitive capture has progressed far enough that reaching for the phone during conversations feels automatic, natural, not like choosing feeds over their son. But impact doesn’t care about intention, and the cumulative effect of thousands of micro-abandonments is the same whether they’re conscious decisions or trained reflexes.

They’re cognitively overdrawn. Their attention accounts are running deficits because they’re spending more cognitive currency than they’re generating, and the interest on that debt keeps compounding. By the time I need their focus for something important, there’s nothing left in the tank. The algorithm already took it.

This is what algorithmic capture does. It doesn’t just steal attention from the person scrolling. It makes them incapable of giving attention to anyone else, including the people they love most, including the people who desperately need them to be present instead of performing presence while their minds are elsewhere.

### The Psych Ward: Containment Economics

When I checked myself into the psychiatric hospital, I thought I was accessing care. What I got was containment optimized for institutional metrics instead of human outcomes.

The system wasn’t designed to help me heal. It was designed to ensure I didn’t become a liability while under their supervision. Every protocol served risk management, not recovery. The difference matters enormously.

My parents asked someone else to take me to the hospital instead of coming themselves. When I said I wanted to come home instead of being admitted, I was told I “couldn’t fly in my condition.” I still call bullshit on this. I’d been masking my entire life, performing neurotypical functionality when survival demanded it. I could have masked through a flight if someone had been waiting on the other side. But that would have required them to disrupt their schedule around holidays, and institutional containment was more convenient for everyone except me.

Inside the ward, the daily structure revealed what the system actually optimized for. No therapy sessions. No skills training. No engagement of cognitive capacity whatsoever. We watched TV. We ate. We waited for medication times. The acutely psychotic patients set the behavioral baseline, and everyone else got managed to that level regardless of actual need.

This is containment, not care. The goal wasn’t making me more capable. It was preventing incidents that might generate paperwork or liability. I was a metric to be managed: “patient remained stable, no self-harm events, no aggressive outbursts, medication compliance achieved.” Those metrics satisfied their institutional obligations while doing absolutely nothing to address why I’d checked myself in.

The system operates like an algorithm optimizing for engagement: treat humans as data points to be processed according to protocols that serve the institution’s needs while creating the appearance of care. Both systems mistake management for relationship, metrics for meaning, containment for connection.

When they finally discharged me, there was no follow-up care coordinated, no outpatient resources lined up, no transition plan beyond “here are your prescriptions, good luck.” They’d contained the immediate crisis, which satisfied their institutional protocols. What happened after I left wasn’t their metric to optimize.

I walked out more traumatized than I’d been walking in, with the additional knowledge that the mental healthcare system isn’t designed to heal you. It’s designed to make your crisis less disruptive to everyone else until you can convincingly perform stability again.

## The Protocol: The Contagion Model of Attention Loss

Algorithmic capture doesn’t stop with the person scrolling. It metastasizes through social networks, degrading collective capacity for sustained attention across entire relationship systems.

When someone’s cognitive bandwidth gets captured by algorithm-optimized content, they don’t just lose their own focus. They become incapable of receiving and processing focus from others. Modern algorithms pull signals not just from native platform usage but from browsing history, location data, device usage, and cross-platform behavior to predict engagement in real time, creating unprecedented precision in attention capture that makes escape progressively more difficult.1

My parents aren’t choosing phones over me in any conscious, deliberate way. Their brains have been trained by thousands of hours of algorithmic conditioning to expect constant novelty, emotional stimulation, and immediate reward. Conversation can’t compete with those reinforcement schedules. Neither can presence. Neither can sustained attention directed at another human who needs connection more than content.

The tragedy isn’t that they don’t love me. The tragedy is that love requires presence to transmit, and their presence has been systematically fragmented by systems engineered to capture and hold attention regardless of collateral damage to human relationships.

Research shows that platforms prioritize divisive content because it generates higher engagement rates, with algorithms exploiting human psychology by feeding echo chambers that reinforce biases and amplify polarizing narratives.2 This same exploitation makes people progressively less capable of the kind of sustained, non-stimulating attention that human connection requires.

Watch what happens: you try to share something meaningful, and mid-sentence their eyes drift to the phone. A notification arrives and their attention immediately redirects, pulled by stimulus-response conditioning so deeply embedded they don’t recognize it as a choice anymore. You’re talking, but they’re not present. They’re performing presence while their cognitive resources are elsewhere, and the performance becomes increasingly unconvincing as algorithmic capture progresses.

This is the hidden cost nobody calculates. We talk about screen time and mental health, polarization and misinformation, productivity loss and attention economics. We don’t talk enough about what happens to your capacity for human connection when your attention gets systematically captured by systems optimized for engagement over everything else.

You can’t be present for someone else when your presence has been captured by an algorithm. The math doesn’t work. There aren’t enough cognitive resources to run both processes simultaneously. Either you’re present with the human in front of you, or you’re present with the feed. You can rapidly context-switch between them, creating the illusion of doing both, but actual sustained presence requires resources that algorithmic capture has already claimed.

My parents want to be there for me. I believe this completely. But wanting isn’t the same as being capable, and capability requires cognitive bandwidth they no longer have available. The algorithm got there first. It’s been training their neural pathways for years, optimizing their behavior around engagement metrics instead of human connection, and now the capacity for sustained attention has degraded to the point where presence becomes functionally impossible even when love is genuinely present.

Christine’s absence was involuntary. Death chose for her. My parents’ absence is involuntary too, but in a different way. They’re still alive, still physically present, still expressing love through whatever channels remain available. But their attention lives elsewhere now, captured by systems that don’t care about my need for connection as long as engagement metrics keep climbing.

The psych ward’s absence was by design. Presence doesn’t scale, metrics do, so institutions optimize for protocols that manage populations rather than connecting with individuals. All three absences share the same underlying structure: systems that optimize for anything except sustained human attention, then generate predictable failures when humans need attention most.

## Personal Code: What Gets Built From Here

I can’t fix other people’s attention. This realization arrived slowly, painfully, like watching someone drown while standing too far away to reach them.

Christine died before I could figure out how to break the abandonment cycle or whether breaking it was even possible. That door closed permanently. No more iterations, no more chances to get the pattern right. Just the permanent absence where a person used to exist.

My parents are still here, still reachable in theory, but their attention lives behind so many layers of algorithmic capture that I don’t know if full presence is possible anymore or whether I’m trying to connect with people whose cognitive capacity has been too thoroughly captured for sustained focus to return. The optimistic read: they’re reading this, which means some part of them recognizes the pattern and wants to reclaim what’s been taken. The realistic read: recognition doesn’t automatically generate change, and habit pathways this deeply embedded don’t rewire just because you can name them.

The psychiatric system will keep optimizing for containment instead of care because institutional incentives reward risk management over human outcomes, and I can’t change those incentives from outside the system. All I can do is refuse to participate in my own containment again, refuse to mistake management for care, refuse to accept protocols as substitutes for presence.

Here’s what I can control: my own attention, my own patterns, my own participation in extraction systems that degrade collective capacity for connection.

I can stop scrolling. I can treat social media like a broadcast channel instead of a feed to consume: send out whatever noise I want, but don’t waste cognitive energy scrolling other people’s posts. I can guard my attention like the finite resource it is, because watching people I love lose their capacity for presence taught me exactly what gets lost when attention gets captured.

I can write pieces like this and hope they land with enough force to make people pause mid-scroll and ask whether the trade they’re making is worth what they’re giving up. Not just the economic cost we calculated in the previous piece, but the relational cost: the cumulative effect of thousands of moments where you chose the feed over the person right in front of you, where you performed presence while your mind was elsewhere, where love existed but couldn’t transmit because presence had been captured by something optimized for engagement instead of connection.

The galvanizing insight isn’t that algorithms are evil or that people who love you are failing. It’s that we’re all participating in systems that make presence progressively more difficult, and participation is a choice we can change even when we can’t change the systems themselves.

You can close the app. You can put down the phone. You can direct sustained attention toward humans who need it instead of feeds that want it. You can recognize when your capacity for presence has been captured and make the decision to reclaim it before the people you love stop trying to reach you because you’ve proven too many times that you’re not actually there anymore.

My parents might read this and recognize themselves in the description. They might not. Recognition doesn’t guarantee change. But the possibility that they’ll see the pattern and choose differently tomorrow means more than perfect presence yesterday that’s gone forever.

Christine taught me that presence isn’t permanent, that people can vanish without warning, that hope has an expiration date you don’t know about until it’s passed. My parents are teaching me that presence can vanish even when people are still alive and physically present, that love can exist without transmission when attention gets captured. The psychiatric system taught me that institutions optimize for their own needs regardless of your suffering.

All three lessons point toward the same conclusion: if you want presence to exist, you have to build it deliberately against systems optimized to prevent it. You have to guard attention jealously. You have to refuse the trade where immediate stimulation purchases the long-term capacity for sustained focus. You have to choose humans over feeds even when feeds are easier, more predictable, more immediately rewarding.

The algorithm will always be there, ready to capture your attention the moment you let your guard down. The people you love won’t be there forever. Some of them will leave voluntarily. Some will leave because systems capture their attention until they can’t give it anymore. Some will leave because death decides the timeline without consulting anyone.

What you do with your attention while they’re still reachable determines whether connection exists or whether you’re just performing proximity while your mind is elsewhere.

I hope my parents read this and feel the uncomfortable recognition that I’m talking about them. I hope it lands with enough force to make them pause and consider whether their attention is actually directed at me when we’re in the same room or whether they’re performing presence while the algorithm holds their focus. I hope it galvanizes change.

But I can’t control whether it does. I can only control my own attention, my own patterns, my own refusal to participate in extraction systems that steal the capacity for human connection. I can write the truth as clearly as I know how to articulate it and trust that people who need to hear it will let it land.

The next move is yours. The feed will always be there. The people you love won’t.
