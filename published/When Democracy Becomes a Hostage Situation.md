## The Glitch: Constitutional Crisis Dressed as Budget Theater

At 12:01 AM EDT on October 1, 2025, the federal government shut down. Within hours, the Trump administration transformed what should have been a negotiation into something far more disturbing: a weaponized purge of blue state infrastructure disguised as fiscal responsibility.

The sequence matters here. Office of Management and Budget Director Russell Vought announced $18 billion in cuts to New York City infrastructure projects — specifically targeting the Gateway Hudson Tunnel Project and Second Avenue Subway — mere hours into the shutdown.1 Then came $8 billion in climate project cancellations across 16 states, every single one of which voted for Kamala Harris in 2024.2 The pattern wasn’t subtle. It was brazen.

The Department of Housing and Urban Development’s website displayed a bright red banner declaring “The Radical Left in Congress shut down the government,” using taxpayer-funded platforms for partisan messaging that experts called a likely violation of the Hatch Act. Federal employees across multiple agencies reported receiving similar partisan language in internal communications, with the VA spokesperson claiming “radical liberals in Congress” were responsible for the shutdown.3

Trump’s own words on September 30th stripped away any pretense: “We can get rid of a lot of things that we didn’t want.” Not “things we can’t afford.” Not “wasteful programs.” Things _we didn’t want._ Then, on October 1st, he clarified exactly who “we” excluded: “We’d be laying off a lot of people that are going to be very affected. They’re going to be Democrats.”4

Economic terrorism with a partisan targeting system. That’s not hyperbole. That’s documentation.

For those of us who’ve spent years building decentralized alternatives precisely because we don’t trust centralized power structures, this moment crystallizes why. When one person can unilaterally weaponize the entire federal apparatus against states that didn’t vote for him, the system isn’t broken — it’s working exactly as designed for authoritarians.

## The Source Code: How Rescissions Became the Nuclear Option

Understanding the shutdown requires understanding the obscure legislative weapon Trump revived: rescissions. The Rescissions Act of 2025 passed the House by a single vote on June 12, 2025 — 214 to 212 — after Representatives Nick LaLota and Don Bacon initially voted no, then flipped their votes following pressure from House leadership.5

The bill rescinded $7.9 billion from international assistance programs and $1.1 billion from the Corporation for Public Broadcasting, totaling about $9 billion in cuts.6 That’s 0.1% of federal spending. But focusing on the dollar amount is like judging a sniper rifle by its weight. The weapon doesn’t need mass if it’s perfectly aimed.

Rescissions haven’t been successfully used in over a quarter century.7 Trump resurrected this legislative fossil for a specific reason: under the Impoundment Control Act of 1974, rescission bills cannot be filibustered in the Senate.8 Simple majority wins. No negotiation required. Democracy’s failsafe becomes its exploit.

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer warned in July: “Anything the Democratic Party negotiated to include in a budget would be undone by rescission.”9 Three months later, during the October shutdown, his prophecy materialized with surgical precision.

Speaker Mike Johnson pulled aside LaLota during the vote when it appeared doomed to fail.10 LaLota, who represents New York, had been pushing for assurances on state and local tax deductions — and after “several minutes of tense conversation,” he flipped to yes.11 Conservative Rep. Chip Roy immediately questioned on X: “Real question on the $9B rescissions package that narrowly passed - is how expensive were the 2 original no votes who flipped to yes…?”12

When your legislative victories require backroom horse-trading to flip a single vote, you’re not governing. You’re hostage-taking with parliamentary procedure as camouflage.

Here’s where Bitcoin enters the frame — not as savior, but as thermometer. As democratic institutions visibly collapsed into partisan warfare, Bitcoin surged past $125,000. The asset designed to profit from governmental dysfunction was fulfilling its core value proposition: when trust in centralized authority evaporates, decentralized alternatives become insurance policies.

I’m not celebrating this. I’m documenting it. Every dollar flowing into crypto represents a vote of no confidence in the system’s ability to function without weaponizing itself against half the country.

## The Upgrade: Decentralization as Structural Immunity

Centralized systems create single points of failure that can be weaponized. The shutdown demonstrated this with the subtlety of a sledgehammer. A NOTUS analysis of hundreds of energy projects found that Trump’s cuts exclusively targeted blue states — while identical projects in red states continued receiving funding.13

Minnesota lost $460 million for interregional transmission lines connecting separated parts of the power grid, while a similar $700 million grant connecting the Eastern and Western grids — running through red states — remained untouched.14 Same program. Same technical specifications. Same national benefit. Different electoral outcomes.

Polling showed 47% of Americans blamed Trump and Republicans for the shutdown versus 30% blaming Democrats, according to a Washington Post survey conducted October 1st.1516 NPR/PBS/Marist polling from late September found 38% would blame Republicans versus 27% blaming Democrats, with another 31% saying both parties share equal blame.17 Yet Trump controls the White House and both chambers of Congress. When you hold all the institutional power and still can’t govern without shutting down the government, the problem isn’t the opposition.

The public saw through it. The courts might, too. Legal experts and state officials called the project cancellations illegal, with Democratic Senator Martin Heinrich stating “Donald Trump’s decision to cancel 223 energy projects in 21 states was nakedly political, unhinged, and unlawful.”18 But legal challenges take time. Projects canceled during that limbo stay canceled. Jobs disappear. Infrastructure rots.

Decentralized systems solve this through architectural design, not political goodwill. No single actor can shut down Bitcoin because there’s no central point of control. No president can weaponize a blockchain against states that didn’t vote for him because the network doesn’t recognize electoral geography.

This matters beyond crypto. Decentralized Physical Infrastructure Networks — DePIN projects — apply the same principle to real-world systems. Instead of federal funding flowing through partisan gatekeepers, community-owned infrastructure networks distribute both governance and capital across stakeholders who can’t be targeted by a single hostile actor.

The Datagram Network, for example, builds decentralized infrastructure where participants own and operate the network nodes. No Russell Vought can announce on X that 16 states just lost their connectivity because they voted wrong. The system’s distributed ownership model makes partisan targeting architecturally impossible. You can’t defund nodes owned by the people using them.

Traditional infrastructure follows a hub-and-spoke model: federal government collects taxes, allocates funds, distributes to states, states implement projects. Five points of failure, any of which can be weaponized. Decentralized infrastructure inverts this: communities build and own their systems directly, with governance distributed across stakeholders. Zero central points of control means zero opportunities for partisan sabotage.

This isn’t theoretical. It’s happening now, in response to exactly this kind of institutional capture. When governments demonstrate they’ll use infrastructure as a political weapon, rational actors build alternatives that route around the weapon entirely.

## My Debug: Pattern Recognition in Real Time

Trump met with OMB Director Russell Vought on October 2nd — a meeting he previewed on Truth Social, saying they’d determine “which of the many Democrat Agencies” to cut “and whether or not those cuts will be temporary or permanent.”19 Not “wasteful agencies.” Not “redundant programs.” _Democrat agencies._

White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt said the number of federal workers who could be fired “is likely going to be in the thousands.”20 Vought told House Republicans on a private call that reductions in force would be “consequential” and begin in “one to two days.”21

Meanwhile, White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt told Fox News the cuts could be reversed: “Pass the clean, continuing resolution and all of this goes away.” But hours later, Energy Secretary Chris Wright contradicted her on CNN, saying “the decision to cancel the funding had nothing to do with the shutdown.”22

When your administration can’t keep its messaging straight for six hours, it’s because the cruelty is the point, not a means to an end. The confusion is tactical. Hostages can’t organize resistance when they don’t know which demands might actually work.

I’ve been watching this pattern unfold while simultaneously discovering I’m likely autistic — a late diagnosis that reframes decades of feeling like institutions operate on rules no one bothered to explain to me. Turns out I wasn’t wrong. The rules _do_ change arbitrarily based on who’s in power and what they want in that particular moment.

Centralized systems concentrate decision-making authority in single actors who can rewrite rules mid-game. Distributed systems encode rules into the architecture itself. No one can change how Bitcoin validates transactions because the validation logic lives in thousands of independent nodes, not in one person’s office.

The uncomfortable parallel: polling from the 2018-2019 Trump shutdown showed similar blame distribution — 47% blamed the president.23 Same percentage in 2025. Same president. Same tactics. But this time he controls Congress. The pattern didn’t change because the pattern is structural. This is what centralized power looks like when wielded without restraint.

Bitcoin hit $125,689 during the shutdown not because crypto solved anything, but because it offered an exit. A way to store value outside a system that weaponizes infrastructure against its own citizens based on electoral maps. That’s not triumph — it’s triage.

Decentralized systems aren’t perfect. They’re just architecturally immune to the specific failure mode we’re watching in real time: single-point weaponization. You can’t shut down a truly distributed network by targeting Democrats because the network doesn’t have a partisan topology. It has a cryptographic one.

Every major institution demonstrates eventually why concentration of power creates predictable failure modes. Not _might_ create. _Does_ create. Every time. The shutdown wasn’t a glitch. It was the operating system working as intended: accumulate power, identify enemies, deploy consequences selectively, blame victims for resisting.

The upgrade isn’t coming from inside the house. It never does. The upgrade comes from building parallel systems that route around institutional capture entirely — whether that’s Bitcoin protecting wealth from political retaliation, or DePIN networks ensuring infrastructure can’t be cut off by partisan budget directors with a Twitter account and a grudge.

Some lessons crystallize instantly when you see them executed at scale. Mine was: when someone shows you they’ll weaponize systems to control outcomes, believe them the first time. Build alternatives before the next shutdown. Because there’s always a next shutdown.
