### What Happens When the Government Films Itself Killing Someone and Then Lies About the Video

## The Glitch: When Murder Gets a Press Release

Three gunshots. 399 milliseconds between the first two.[^1] Then, under his breath: “Fucking bitch.” [^2]

That’s Jonathan Ross, Immigration and Customs Enforcement agent, describing Renee Nicole Good approximately four seconds after shooting her three times through the windshield of her Honda Pilot on a residential Minneapolis street. She’d told him “That’s fine, dude, I’m not mad at you” fourteen seconds earlier.[^3] Her last words before dying were an attempt at de-escalation. His last words before she crashed into a parked car were a slur muttered into his own cellphone camera.

Here’s where it gets interesting. Ross filmed the whole thing. His own footage shows Good smiling at him, her wife Becca standing nearby telling the agents to “go get yourself some lunch, big boy.” Cars passing on both sides of Good’s vehicle because she wasn’t actually blocking the street. Ross walking around her SUV, documenting her license plate like he’s cataloging evidence for a parking violation. Other agents swarming the car, yanking door handles, screaming “Get out of the fucking car.” Good shifting into reverse, then forward, her steering wheel turning right, away from Ross. Three shots while he’s already stepping backward, safe from any contact.

President Trump watched this video in the Oval Office with New York Times reporters present. His conclusion? Good “violently, willfully, and viciously ran over the ICE Officer.” [^4]

This isn’t spin. This is institutional gaslighting so blatant it makes your childhood “Who are you going to believe, me or your lying eyes?” arguments look sophisticated by comparison. The video shows Ross standing upright after shooting Good, walking calmly toward her crashed vehicle while she bleeds out onto the airbag. Trump claims she ran him over. Not “tried to run him over.” Ran him over. Past tense. Accomplished fact.

Vice President JD Vance doubled down: Ross’s “life was endangered and he fired in self-defense.” [^5] Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem went further, calling Good’s attempt to drive away “an act of domestic terrorism.” [^6] The Department of Homeland Security issued a statement alleging Good “weaponized her vehicle” to kill federal officers.

Four separate camera angles contradict every syllable. But contradicting reality is the point.

Welcome to 2026, where the government shoots a 37-year-old mother in the face, films itself doing it, releases the footage, and then insists the footage shows something it objectively doesn’t show. The goal isn’t persuading you they’re right. The goal is exhausting your capacity to know what’s real.

## The Source Code: How Cover-Ups Work When Everyone Can See Everything

Authoritarian gaslighting doesn’t hide evidence anymore. Hiding evidence is twentieth-century thinking. Modern institutional lying works by flooding the zone with so many competing narratives that determining truth becomes more expensive than surrendering to power’s version.

Think of it like trying to find a specific fish in an aquarium versus finding that same fish after someone dumped it into the ocean. Same fish, different retrieval costs. Ross’s cellphone video is that fish. The Trump administration’s strategy is turning the Minneapolis shooting into an ocean of conflicting claims until most people give up trying to figure out what actually happened.

Start with Ross’s history. Six months before shooting Good, he was dragged by a vehicle during a different arrest. Required 33 stitches. Sustained serious injuries when a suspect accelerated while Ross’s arm was stuck inside the car window. Federal prosecutors convicted that suspect of assaulting a federal officer.[^7] All of this is documented, verifiable fact.

Now watch Vance weaponize that history: “That very ICE officer nearly had his life ended, dragged by a car, six months ago. You think maybe he’s a little bit sensitive about someone ramming him with an automobile?” [^8]

Notice the rhetorical move. Vance isn’t arguing the video shows Good ramming Ross. He’s arguing Ross’s emotional state matters more than what the video shows. If Ross felt threatened because of past trauma, the shooting becomes justified regardless of whether Good actually posed a threat. Your feelings override observable reality. My authority overrides your eyes.

Former Minneapolis Police Chief Medaria Arradondo gave the obvious response: Ross “should simply have gotten out of the way of Good’s car.” [^9] You know, the normal thing you do when a car is moving and you don’t want to get hit. Step aside. Don’t shoot the driver three times and then call her a fucking bitch.

But Arradondo’s response treats this like a tactical error. It’s not. It’s a successful demonstration of federal immunity. Ross shot Good because he could. Because ICE agents have been told, explicitly and implicitly, that they operate above local law. Because the Trump administration needed Minneapolis to know that questioning federal authority comes with bullets.

The FBI’s handling of the investigation makes this explicit. Federal authorities initially agreed to work with Minnesota’s Bureau of Criminal Apprehension, the Hennepin County Attorney’s Office, and the U.S. Attorney’s Office in Minneapolis. Joint investigation. Multiple oversight layers. Standard procedure for officer-involved shootings.

Then the FBI “reversed course.” [^10] That’s the Minnesota BCA’s phrasing in their official statement. One day they’re partners in the investigation. The next day they’re shut out completely. No access to Good’s vehicle. No access to forensic evidence. No access to witness interviews. No access to the crime scene materials necessary to complete “a thorough and independent investigation.”

Governor Tim Walz made the obvious assertion: “Minnesota must be part of this investigation.” Trump’s response? Walz is “a stupid person” running “a very corrupt state.” [^11] The evidence of this corruption? Walz wanted state investigators involved in investigating a killing that happened in his state.

Trump elaborated, pivoting immediately to unsubstantiated fraud allegations against Minnesota’s Somali-American population. Claimed “$19 billion stolen” without evidence. Suggested Somalis “buy their votes” and “vote in a group.” Declared he actually “won the state of Minnesota” despite losing Minnesota in 2024.[^12] The logic loop closes tight: Minnesota is corrupt because it voted against Trump, therefore Minnesota investigators can’t be trusted, therefore the FBI must control all evidence, therefore anyone questioning this is politically motivated, therefore the shooting was justified.

This is gaslighting as institutional architecture. You don’t argue the facts. You attack the jurisdiction of anyone who might verify the facts. You declare verification itself a partisan act. You make truth-seeking so procedurally complex and politically toxic that most people accept whatever the loudest authority claims.

Hennepin County Attorney Mary Moriarty held a press conference making a basic legal point: her office has jurisdiction to file charges regardless of federal employment.[^13] “It does not matter that it was a federal law enforcement agent.” Minnesota law applies to killings in Minnesota. Federal agents aren’t exempt from state criminal prosecution. This isn’t controversial. This is basic federalism, the kind of stuff you learn in eighth-grade civics.

Vance claimed the opposite. Insisted Ross is “protected by absolute immunity” and that local prosecution of a federal officer would be “unprecedented.” [^14] Legal experts immediately called bullshit. Federal agents have been prosecuted by state authorities before. The Constitution doesn’t create a separate caste of unaccountable federal enforcers. But Vance’s claim serves its purpose anyway. Muddy the legal waters. Create uncertainty. Make people think maybe the feds really are above state law. Delay accountability until attention moves elsewhere.

Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison told CNN exactly what the Trump administration wants: “an investigation that the feds want to do without state involvement.” [^15] Not a fair investigation. Not a transparent investigation. An investigation whose outcome nobody trusts, rendering accountability meaningless even if charges eventually get filed.

The strategy relies on exhaustion. You can’t maintain permanent fact-checking vigilance. At some point you get tired of verifying every claim, debunking every lie, correcting every distortion. The Trump administration knows this. They’re not trying to convince you Good attacked Ross. They’re trying to make determining what actually happened more expensive than accepting their narrative and moving on.

Consider the timeline. Good was shot January 8. By January 9, comedian John Mulaney postponed his Minneapolis shows because “the situation is so unsafe.” [^16] By January 10, the White House was sharing Ross’s cellphone footage as vindication despite it showing exactly what witnesses described. By January 11, the narrative had fractured into tribal epistemology. Conservatives saw self-defense. Progressives saw murder. The median voter saw another exhausting controversy they couldn’t parse and checked out.

Mission accomplished. Not truth established. Just compliance achieved through fatigue.

## The Upgrade: What Happens When Evidence Can’t Be Disappeared

The solution to centralized truth-control is distributed verification, but implementation requires more than posting videos on Twitter.

Ross filmed his own shooting of Good. That footage exists because he chose to document it, presumably to cover his ass if things went sideways. It didn’t save Good. It won’t convict Ross. But it does something more interesting: it creates a permanent record the administration can’t completely control. They can block Minnesota investigators from accessing the car. They can’t block everyone from seeing what Ross filmed.

Hennepin County created a website asking the public to submit videos and evidence of the shooting.[^17] This is Version 0.1 of decentralized investigation. Crowdsourced verification replacing institutional gatekeeping. Multiple camera angles from multiple witnesses creating a record no single authority can alter.

But it’s still centralized at the collection point. The county controls what gets included, how it’s analyzed, who sees the synthesis. A more robust system would look like distributed evidence protocols where submissions get cryptographically verified, timestamped immutably, and analyzed by multiple independent teams whose findings publish simultaneously with full methodological transparency.

The technical infrastructure already exists. Signal proves you can transmit information without metadata exposure. Distributed hash tables demonstrate how data persists without centralized storage. Zero-knowledge proofs show how to verify claims without revealing sensitive details. We’re not missing technology. We’re missing institutional willingness to surrender control.

Minnesota Attorney General Ellison said his office is “moving forward in collaboration” with state investigators “to preserve any evidence and ensure that it can be reviewed.” [^18] Translation: we know the feds are going to corrupt their investigation, so we’re building parallel verification systems. It’s defensive architecture. Assume federal obstruction, prepare workarounds. It’s not ideal, but it’s adaptive.

The question is whether Minnesota has resources to match federal obstruction. The FBI has unlimited funding and legal authority to classify evidence. Minnesota has a state budget and a governor Trump calls stupid. Asymmetric warfare doesn’t favor the good guys just because they’re right.

Here’s where DePIN infrastructure gets interesting. Instead of the Department of Homeland Security investigating itself while blocking outside verification, imagine community-run sensor networks that automatically capture and distribute footage from public spaces. Not surveillance cameras controlled by the state. Distributed verification infrastructure controlled by no one and everyone simultaneously.

When an ICE agent shoots someone, the evidence doesn’t route through federal gatekeepers. It propagates across a network designed to resist capture. The footage timestamps cryptographically. Multiple nodes verify authenticity. Independent analysts access raw data simultaneously. The government can still control their official investigation, but they can’t control what the public can verify independently.

The constitutional argument is straightforward. Local jurisdiction historically holds primacy over federal agencies operating within state borders. The FBI doesn’t supersede state criminal law. Federal agents aren’t exempt from state prosecution. When Vance claims Ross has “absolute immunity,” he’s asserting a principle that would make federal agents a separate caste, accountable only to themselves, investigating only themselves, defining truth only for themselves.

Moriarty emphasized her office has jurisdiction regardless of federal employment status. This isn’t radical. It’s basic law. But defending it requires resources most counties lack when the federal government controls evidence and declares cooperation adversarial.

The distributed verification solution doesn’t require waiting for technology. It requires political will to demand that federal agencies operating in state jurisdictions operate under state observation. Minnesota could legislate that any federal law enforcement action within state borders must allow state forensic teams simultaneous evidence access. The feds could challenge this in court, but the legal argument that states can’t observe federal agents during state criminal investigations is constitutionally dubious at best.

The upgrade isn’t just technical infrastructure. It’s procedural architecture that makes institutional gaslighting more expensive than telling the truth. Right now, blocking verification costs nothing and yields narrative control. Make it cost jurisdiction. Make it cost evidence admissibility. Make it cost public trust in ways that have material consequences beyond polling.

When authoritarian systems control the investigation of their own actions, they’re not seeking truth. They’re managing liability. Decentralized verification removes that option. It makes cover-ups detectably incomplete. It creates permanent records that can’t be retroactively altered. It transforms the question from “who do you believe” to “what does the evidence show” in a system where evidence can’t be disappeared.

## My Debug: Recognizing the Pattern You’ve Seen Before

Two weeks before Ross shot Good, the Department of Justice missed its legal deadline to release the Epstein files.

Congress passed the Epstein Files Transparency Act on November 19, 2025. Required full release by December 19.[^19] The DOJ released 12,285 documents on deadline day. Less than one percent of the estimated 2 million pages in federal possession.[^20] Attorney General Pam Bondi had demanded “full and complete Epstein files” from FBI Director Kash Patel with “no withholdings or limitations.” [^21] That was theater. The actual release came heavily redacted, with Trump’s name removed from documents where it had previously appeared.

Watch this trick. One document redacted Trump’s name from Sarah Ransome’s 2016 claim that Trump had “sexual trysts at Epstein’s New York mansion on regular occasions.” [^22] That exact same document, released in January 2025 under Biden’s DOJ, included Trump’s name. Someone went back and censored old releases to match new redactions. They’re not just hiding new information. They’re retroactively editing the historical record.

The DOJ now estimates 5.2 million pages remain under review. Deploying 400 attorneys “working around the clock.” [^23] At current pace, full release won’t happen for eight years. Conveniently, that’s after Trump leaves office, assuming he leaves office.

When Marjorie Taylor Greene threatened to release names of Epstein’s associates, Trump called her. Others heard him yelling through the speakerphone. “My friends will get hurt,” he told her, according to Greene’s New York Times interview.[^24] Not “innocent people will be harmed by false accusations.” Not “we need due process.” My friends will get hurt.

The pattern is identical to Minneapolis. Control the investigation. Block outside verification. Declare questioners corrupt. Run out the clock.

A YouGov poll conducted January 2-5 found 49% of Americans believe Trump is covering up Epstein’s crimes.[^25] That includes 83% of Democrats, 51% of Independents, and 14% of Republicans. Even 6% of MAGA supporters think he’s hiding something, which is remarkable given that demographic’s general willingness to believe Trump could personally pull the sun across the sky if he claimed credit.

The question isn’t whether there’s a cover-up. The question is whether you can see it. Trump promised full transparency during the campaign. As president, he’s deployed the same institutional gaslighting apparatus in Minneapolis: control evidence, control narrative, exhaust opposition.

Senator Sheldon Whitehouse asked what everyone’s thinking: “What on earth happened to take this top priority of FBI Director Patel and this top priority of Attorney General Bondi and this top priority of President Trump, and not be ready when the day came to actually release the documents?” [^26]

The answer is the same as Minneapolis. They were never planning to release anything that might complicate the narrative. The investigation exists to manage perception, not discover truth. You announce transparency. You miss deadlines. You release heavily redacted fragments. You blame the volume of documents. You declare critics politically motivated. You wait for attention to shift.

This is what systemic gaslighting looks like at institutional scale. Not isolated lies about isolated incidents, but architectural design to make truth verification impossible. When federal agencies investigate themselves, block outside scrutiny, heavily redact what they do release, and attack anyone demanding transparency as corrupt or politically motivated, they’re not protecting national security or victim privacy. They’re protecting power from accountability.

Good’s wife Becca said Renee “lived by an overarching belief: there is kindness in the world and we need to do everything we can to find it where it resides and nurture it where it needs to grow.” [^27] Good told Ross she wasn’t mad at him. Tried to de-escalate with a smile and calm words. Ross shot her three times and muttered “fucking bitch” as she crashed. Trump claimed she violently ran over federal agents. The FBI blocked Minnesota investigators from examining evidence. Vance insisted Ross is immune from prosecution.

The playbook isn’t complicated. Control what people can verify. Declare verification itself suspicious. Exhaust the public’s capacity to distinguish truth from performance. Watch democracy dissolve into tribal epistemology where facts become team affiliation rather than shared reality.

The only effective resistance is distributed verification that can’t be captured by central authority. Not because technology solves authoritarianism. It doesn’t. But because making truth observable to multiple independent actors simultaneously makes institutional gaslighting detectably incomplete. You can’t tell people the video doesn’t show what it clearly shows if the video is cryptographically verified, timestamped, and distributed across systems you don’t control.

Ross called Good a “fucking bitch.” The administration insists she tried to kill him. The video shows otherwise. Either we build systems that make evidence tampering impossible, or we accept that power defines reality and accountability becomes nostalgia.

The question isn’t whether the administration is gaslighting about Minneapolis and Epstein. The videos exist. The redactions exist. The blocked investigators exist. The question is whether we’re building infrastructure to make that gaslighting provably false, or whether we’re just arguing about whose narrative to believe while the evidence disappears behind federal classification and prosecutorial discretion.

You’re watching authoritarianism test how much reality you’ll surrender. How you answer determines whether evidence still matters, or whether power’s word becomes truth by default.*
