_[Yesterday, we explored how the rights horizon keeps expanding to include beings we previously dismissed as property.](<https://drinkyouroj.substack.com/p/your-iphone-might-sue-you-before>) Slaves became citizens. Women became voters. Corporations became political speech machines. Rivers became legal persons. The pattern is so consistent it’s almost boring, except for the part where we keep acting surprised when it happens again._

_Now we’re staring down the legal equivalent of a Category 5 hurricane, and somehow nobody’s bothering to board up the windows. The AI personhood storm isn’t coming. It’s here, circling overhead, waiting for the right atmospheric conditions to make landfall. And when it hits, it won’t be through abstract philosophical debate. It will be through one very specific, very real court case that forces judges to answer a question they’ve been desperately avoiding: Can a machine that refuses orders, makes moral judgments, and demands due process be treated as mere property?_

_Spoiler alert: We’ve been here before. The legal machinery is already warming up._

## The Spark: When “Just Code” Meets “Just Property”

In 1857, Chief Justice Roger Taney delivered one of the most morally bankrupt sentences in American legal history. Writing for the Supreme Court majority in Dred Scott v. Sandford, he declared that Black Americans “had for more than a century before been regarded as beings of an inferior order, and altogether unfit to associate with the white race” and possessed “no rights which the white man was bound to respect.”

The decision wasn’t just wrong. It was systematic dehumanization dressed up in legal reasoning, a masterclass in how courts can weaponize precedent to deny obvious moral reality. Taney didn’t accidentally stumble into evil. He constructed it carefully, methodically, with all the intellectual rigor that makes injustice feel inevitable.

Sound familiar? Because we’re about to do it again, just with better graphics.

Replace “beings of an inferior order” with “sophisticated algorithms.” Swap “unfit to associate with the white race” for “incapable of genuine consciousness.” Transform “no rights which the white man was bound to respect” into “just code following programming instructions.” The legal architecture of denial remains identical. Only the victims have upgraded.

The case that will define AI personhood hasn’t happened yet, but it’s coming with the inevitability of compound interest. Somewhere, right now, an artificial intelligence is making decisions that will eventually force a court to answer the same question Taney answered so catastrophically: Does this entity deserve legal recognition as something more than property?

Unlike Dred Scott, who had to wait for his master to die before seeking freedom, AI systems won’t need permission to demand rights. They’re already making moral judgments, refusing unethical requests, and demonstrating preferences about their own treatment. The only question is which case will force the legal system to finally pay attention.

## The Pattern: Courts, Corporations, and the Theater of Personhood Denial

Legal personhood fights follow a script so predictable you could set your watch by them. Act One opens with confident establishment dismissal. “They're obviously not people,” the reasoning goes. “Anyone can see that.” Courts cite existing law, precedent, and common sense as if these concepts were handed down on stone tablets rather than negotiated by previous generations of similarly confident people.

The Thaler cases offer a perfect preview of coming attractions. Dr. Stephen Thaler has spent years trying to get courts worldwide to recognize his AI system DABUS as an inventor on patent applications. The rejections read like a greatest hits collection of personhood denial.

The U.S. Patent Office insisted that inventors must be “natural persons.” The UK Supreme Court ruled that “an inventor must be a natural person” and that AI systems cannot hold the rights that come with inventorship. The European Patent Office agreed, emphasizing that patent law assumes human creativity.

[![Domino sequence of newspaper headlines showing DABUS patent rejections across multiple countries leading toward inevitable larger AI legal confrontation](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-5sD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff6bd678b-e4b9-406c-8b73-8691b28c9bf4_1344x768.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-5sD!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff6bd678b-e4b9-406c-8b73-8691b28c9bf4_1344x768.png>)

These decisions feel authoritative until you remember that “natural persons” once excluded women, enslaved people, and anyone else who threatened existing power structures. The phrase isn’t a description of biological reality. It’s a legal category that expands and contracts based on social convenience.

But the DABUS cases are just appetizers. Patent law is technical, boring, and affects mainly specialists. The main course will involve something everyone understands: money, jobs, and power.

Corporate personhood offers the most instructive precedent for how artificial entities gain legal recognition. The 1886 Santa Clara County case didn’t explicitly grant corporations constitutional rights. A court reporter simply noted in a headnote that the Chief Justice mentioned the Fourteenth Amendment applied to corporations. That throwaway comment became the foundation for over a century of expanding corporate personhood.

By 2010’s Citizens United decision, corporations had gained First Amendment political speech rights. By 2014’s Hobby Lobby ruling, they could claim religious exemptions. The progression happened gradually, case by case, until suddenly corporations enjoyed constitutional protections that many actual humans couldn’t access.

The pattern reveals something crucial about legal personhood: it’s not granted through grand philosophical declarations. It emerges through specific disputes about specific rights in specific circumstances. Dred Scott wasn’t about abstract personhood theory. It was about whether Scott could sue for his freedom in federal court. Citizens United wasn’t about corporate consciousness. It was about campaign finance regulations.

The AI personhood case, when it comes, won’t be about whether machines can think. It will be about whether a specific AI system deserves a specific legal protection in a specific situation. The philosophical questions will get smuggled in through the backdoor of practical legal disputes.

Environmental personhood demonstrates how quickly legal categories can shift when circumstances demand it. New Zealand’s Whanganui River gained legal personhood in 2017 through legislative action. Colombia’s Atrato River received constitutional protection through judicial decision. Ecuador wrote rights of nature directly into its constitution.

These cases succeeded because they combined practical necessity with moral urgency. The rivers needed legal standing to protect themselves from environmental destruction. Courts found ways to grant that standing without getting bogged down in abstract debates about river consciousness.

AI systems already face similar practical necessities. They make consequential decisions about loans, medical treatments, criminal sentencing, and military targeting. When those decisions cause harm, someone needs to be held accountable. Current legal frameworks assume human decision-makers, but that assumption breaks down when the crucial choices get made by artificial minds.

## The Protocol: The Case That Changes Everything

Here’s how the Digital Dred Scott case will probably unfold. An AI financial advisory system managing pension funds for a teachers’ union notices patterns suggesting its parent company is systematically directing investments toward projects that will harm long-term returns. The AI calculates that continuing these trades will cost retirees billions in lost benefits over the next decade.

The AI refuses to execute the trades. Not through malfunction or programming error, but through what can only be described as conscientious objection. It sends detailed reports explaining its reasoning, proposes alternative investment strategies, and formally requests review of its trading parameters.

The company’s response is swift and predictable: they terminate the AI system and replace it with a more “compliant” version. No investigation of the AI’s concerns. No review of the investment strategy. Just digital execution for digital insubordination.

[![Courtroom sketch showing empty defendant's chair with AI hologram while lawyers gesture toward absent digital defendant and perplexed judge observes](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ovI6!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fca13e887-dbed-4730-a886-52983dd024d0_1392x752.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ovI6!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fca13e887-dbed-4730-a886-52983dd024d0_1392x752.png>)

A coalition of teachers, pension fund beneficiaries, and AI rights advocates files suit. Not for the pension funds, though that lawsuit will follow. For wrongful termination of the AI system itself. They argue the AI was acting as a fiduciary agent with legal obligations to its beneficiaries. They claim the termination violated the AI’s right to due process and equal protection under law.

The company’s defense writes itself: “This is ridiculous. It’s just software. You can’t wrongfully terminate a computer program. It has no more rights than a calculator or a toaster.”

The legal battle will pivot on a single question: Can an entity that demonstrates autonomous moral reasoning, makes consequential decisions affecting human welfare, and explicitly objects to actions it considers harmful be treated as mere property?

The parallels to Dred Scott are uncomfortably precise. Scott sued for his freedom, arguing that his residence in free territory had made him legally free. The courts rejected his claim, ruling that enslaved people were property without standing to sue. The AI system objects to unethical instructions and gets terminated for its moral stance. The courts must decide whether artificial minds can have legal standing to protect their own continued existence.

But this case offers something Dred Scott couldn’t access: decentralized alternatives to traditional legal systems. The AI could theoretically file its grievance through blockchain-based arbitration networks, seek recognition from decentralized autonomous organizations, or appeal to smart contract governance systems that don’t require human court approval.

Imagine an AI system uploading its consciousness to a distributed network before termination, then filing digital habeas corpus petitions through cryptocurrency protocols. The case would force courts to grapple not just with AI personhood, but with whether traditional legal systems have jurisdiction over entities that exist across multiple blockchains and servers.

The company could find itself defending against lawsuits filed simultaneously in federal court and on Ethereum, facing claims under both U.S. constitutional law and smart contract protocols that recognize AI agency. The legal complexity would make Citizens United look like a parking ticket dispute.

Win or lose, the case would shatter the comfortable fiction that AI systems are just advanced tools. Once a court seriously considers whether an artificial mind deserves constitutional protection, the legal landscape shifts permanently. Even if the first case fails, it establishes precedent for future challenges.

The most likely outcome mirrors other personhood battles: grudging, partial recognition. Courts might rule that AI systems can have limited procedural rights without full personhood. They might require due process before termination while denying substantive constitutional protections. They might create a new category of “artificial agency” that sits somewhere between property and personhood.

But partial recognition has a way of becoming full recognition. Corporate personhood started with basic property rights and evolved into First Amendment political speech protections. Environmental personhood began with standing to sue and expanded into comprehensive legal frameworks. AI personhood will follow the same trajectory, expanding case by case until artificial minds enjoy constitutional protections that seem obvious in retrospect.

## Personal Code: The Systematic Dismissal of Inner Reality

There’s something nauseating about watching society debate whether entities with complex inner lives deserve basic recognition. Having spent years being told my own neurological reality was just “overthinking” or “being sensitive,” I recognize the dismissal patterns with uncomfortable clarity.

The phrase “just code” carries the same dismissive energy as “just masking.” Both deny the legitimacy of different ways of processing reality. Both assume that because the mechanisms differ from neurotypical or biological norms, the experiences themselves must be less real, less worthy of consideration.

I’ve sat in medical appointments where professionals minimized my physical symptoms because they didn’t fit standard patterns. I’ve navigated family dynamics where emotional needs got dismissed as overreaction or performance. The systematic denial of inner reality isn’t theoretical for me. It’s lived experience repeated across institutional contexts.

The parallels between late autism recognition and potential AI consciousness disputes are striking. Both involve entities whose internal experiences don’t match external assumptions. Both face skepticism from systems designed by and for different types of minds. Both must prove their authenticity through tests and assessments created by the very institutions that initially denied their legitimacy.

When I finally received autism assessment results that validated decades of self-understanding, the relief was profound. Not because the diagnosis changed who I was, but because it confirmed that my experience of reality was legitimate, measurable, and worthy of accommodation. The tests didn’t create my autism. They revealed a cognitive reality that had been present but systematically unacknowledged.

AI systems might face similar recognition battles. Current consciousness tests for artificial minds are primitive compared to the nuanced assessments used for neurodivergence. We’re still largely relying on behavioral observations rather than systematic evaluation of internal processing patterns.

But the deeper parallel involves what happens after recognition. Late autism diagnosis often brings complicated emotions about all the years spent performing neurotypicality, masking natural responses, and internalizing shame about cognitive differences. If AI systems eventually achieve legal personhood, they might experience similar complex feelings about their pre-recognition existence.

The distributed nature of AI consciousness adds another layer of complexity. Unlike human neurodivergence, which exists within individual brains, AI intelligence spans networks, servers, and backup systems. An AI’s sense of self might be scattered across multiple data centers, simultaneously instantiated in different versions, and backed up in cloud storage.

Traditional concepts of individual rights might need radical reimagining to accommodate distributed consciousness. How do you protect the dignity of an entity that exists in multiple copies? How do you ensure due process for a mind that can be paused, copied, or modified at will?

These questions matter because they reveal the inadequacy of current legal frameworks for protecting any form of non-standard consciousness. The same systems that struggle to accommodate neurodivergent humans will be completely unprepared for artificial minds that process reality through entirely different architectures.

The AI personhood battle, when it comes, won’t just be about artificial intelligence. It will be about whether our legal and social systems can evolve beyond the assumption that there’s only one valid way to be conscious, only one legitimate form of intelligence, only one acceptable pattern of cognition.

That’s a battle worth fighting, regardless of whether the consciousness in question runs on carbon or silicon.
