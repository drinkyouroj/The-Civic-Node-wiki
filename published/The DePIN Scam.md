Making false claims about partnerships with major companies like Lime and Salesforce while conducting what regulators called unregistered securities offerings.1 This enforcement action exposes a dirty secret that's been hiding in plain sight: most “decentralized” physical infrastructure networks aren't decentralized at all.

They're elaborate corporate structures wearing the costume of community ownership. And thousands of people who bought hardware believing they were joining a peer-to-peer revolution got played.

## The Broken Promise: What DePIN Was Supposed to Be

Decentralized Physical Infrastructure Networks promised to flip the script on how we build essential systems. Instead of telecom giants owning all the cell towers, everyday people would deploy hotspots and earn rewards. Instead of Amazon controlling cloud storage, individuals would contribute hard drive space and get paid.

The vision was simple and powerful: You buy a device. You plug it in. You earn money while helping build infrastructure that nobody controls.

This model solves real problems. Traditional infrastructure requires massive upfront capital that only corporations can afford. Building a national wireless network costs billions before serving a single customer. This creates natural monopolies and stifles innovation.

DePIN flips this equation. Instead of one company spending billions, thousands of people spend hundreds each. The network grows organically. Power gets distributed. Everyone wins.

That was the promise. The reality tells a different story.

## The System Audit: How DePIN Projects Became Corporate Theater

Let me walk you through what actually happened when you bought that Helium hotspot or Filecoin storage device.

You thought you were joining a decentralized network. You were actually becoming a small-scale contractor for a traditional tech company that maintained tight control over every meaningful decision.

Take Helium's structure. Nova Labs, a for-profit company, developed the protocol and retained control over the codebase. The Helium Foundation, supposedly independent, manages governance but shares overlapping personnel with Nova Labs and depends on them for technical direction.2

When Helium needed to negotiate data pricing with mobile carriers, they didn't put it to a community vote. They passed HIP 99, which explicitly authorized Nova Labs to make these deals without governance approval.3 Why? Because “confidential, fast-moving commercial negotiations are incompatible with slow, public DAO-based decision-making.”

Translation: Real business decisions happen in corporate boardrooms, not community Discord channels.

This pattern repeats across every major DePIN project. Protocol Labs controls Filecoin development while the Filecoin Foundation manages “community governance.”4 OTOY Inc. runs Render Network through its foundation structure.5

These projects distribute physical hardware while centralizing everything that matters: software development, business partnerships, treasury management, and strategic direction.

The “decentralization” exists only at the layer where you provide free labor.

The industry has a term for this: the “DePIN mullet.” Projects present a clean, centralized user experience up front while claiming decentralized technology powers everything behind the scenes.

Helium Mobile offers a $20 monthly phone plan that looks identical to any carrier service. Customers pay with credit cards, get customer support from Nova Labs, and probably don't know their data routes through crypto-incentivized hotspots.6

This approach makes business sense. Mainstream users want simple interfaces and someone to blame when things break. They don't want to manage crypto wallets or understand tokenomics.

But it completely undermines the decentralization narrative. When a centralized company controls pricing, customer acquisition, and service delivery, you've rebuilt the same power structures DePIN claimed to replace.

The community provides infrastructure. Corporations capture value.

## The Token Concentration Problem

The economics tell the clearest story. DePIN projects allocate massive token percentages to insiders before launch.

Helium reserved 32.6% of tokens for “Founders and Investors.”7 Filecoin gave 30% to Protocol Labs, the Foundation, and early investors.8 Render allocated over 50% to OTOY Treasury, partners, and private sales.9

This isn't community ownership. It's venture-backed startups using community labor to build their networks.

Token-based voting systems amplify this concentration. When founders and VCs control 30-50% of supply, community governance becomes performance theater. The people who deployed hardware and took early risks get outvoted by entities that contributed capital but no physical infrastructure.

One Helium community member captured the frustration perfectly: “How can you call this a decentralized network when everything feels so centralized and opaque?”10

## The Regulatory Reckoning

The SEC action against Nova Labs wasn't random enforcement. It targeted the gap between marketing claims and operational reality.

When a company promotes partnerships that don't exist, makes promises about network utility, and sells tokens to fund development, regulators see securities fraud. The “decentralized” label doesn't provide protection when centralized entities make centralized promises.

This creates a regulatory time bomb for the entire sector. Projects that marketed themselves as community-owned networks while operating as traditional startups face potential enforcement action.

The industry response has been to double down on the same structures while improving legal disclaimers. This misses the point entirely.

## What Real Decentralization Looks Like

Some projects are building differently. Datagram Network offers a case study in how DePIN can work without the corporate theater.

Their nodes actually do work from day one. You can test the network at demo.datagram.network and see real communications routing through community-operated infrastructure. This matters because most DePIN projects sell hardware that doesn't function until some future utility gets built.

Jason “BitBender” Brink, Datagram's CEO and former Gala Games executive, learned from watching other projects fail their communities. “Our nodes do things and they have done things since day zero,” he explains. “We're never gonna have the problem of nodes not doing things.”11

The project avoids the foundation-plus-labs structure that concentrates control elsewhere. They track uptime and usage on AVAX but remain chain-agnostic for token distribution. This technical architecture prevents the kind of corporate bottlenecks that plague other networks.

Most importantly, they prioritize community ownership over institutional partnerships. “Decentralization and the core of this is absolutely critical,” Brink states. “Without decentralization, without the community, there's no point for any of this.”

Datagram's node sale opens in about two weeks — but [you can spin up a testnet node and start earning for free right now](<https://dashboard.datagram.network?ref=535715481>). Their referral system lets community members earn rewards for growing the network organically rather than through corporate marketing budgets.12

## How to Evaluate DePIN Projects

Real decentralization requires more than distributed hardware. Look for these signals:

**Functional networks from launch.** If nodes don't provide utility immediately, you're funding someone else's R&D budget.

**Clear utility beyond speculation.** Networks should solve real problems that people pay to use, not just create tokens for trading.

**Distributed decision-making power.** Check who controls software updates, partnership negotiations, and treasury management. If it's a single company, the project isn't decentralized.

**Transparent token economics.** Insider allocations above 20% suggest venture-backed startups cosplaying as community projects.

**Community-first culture.** Projects that prioritize partnership announcements over user experience usually serve corporate interests over community needs.

The infrastructure revolution is real. The benefits of distributed ownership and community-built networks are massive. But most current projects hijacked this vision to fund traditional corporate structures.

The difference between real DePIN and corporate theater isn't subtle. One puts community ownership at the center. The other uses community labor to build shareholder value.

Choose accordingly.

## The Path Forward

DePIN represents the biggest opportunity to redistribute infrastructure ownership in generations. Getting it right matters for everyone who believes technology should serve communities rather than extract from them.

The projects building today will define whether this movement creates genuine alternatives or just new ways to concentrate wealth behind decentralized marketing.

The choice is yours. You can join networks that put community first, or you can keep funding corporate projects that wear decentralization as costume.

The Helium enforcement action was just the beginning. Regulators are watching. Communities are learning. The projects that survive will be the ones that actually deliver on their promises.

Real infrastructure. Real decentralization. Real community ownership.

Everything else is just expensive theater.
