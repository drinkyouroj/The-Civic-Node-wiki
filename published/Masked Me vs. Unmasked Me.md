## The Spark: The Performance Ends

I didn't know I was wearing a mask.

I thought I was doing what everyone else did. Preparing conversations in advance. Mirroring other people's tone. Saying “how are you” even when I didn't want to talk. But after burning out, breaking down, and finally being honest with myself, I realized something: I wasn't being polite. I was being erased.

The moment I understood this changed everything. I was walking down a busy street after a particularly draining day at work when it hit me. The constant performance was killing me. I stopped in the middle of the sidewalk and yelled at no one in particular: “I can't keep doing this!” People stared. I didn't care. For the first time in years, what came out of my mouth was exactly what I felt inside.

That breakdown became my breakthrough. I used to think I was a high-functioning, gifted person who happened to struggle with anxiety and depression. The truth was quieter and harder to find. The version of me that everyone knew was built on performance. The real me had been buried so deep I'd forgotten it existed.

Research shows that autistic masking creates a state of cognitive dissonance where you hold two contradictory versions of yourself simultaneously.1 Your authentic thoughts, feelings, and interests conflict with the persona you've constructed to survive. This internal split doesn't just cause psychological stress. It creates a measurable physiological burden that accumulates over time.

I learned this the hard way. The person I'd been performing as could handle anything. The person underneath was exhausted, overstimulated, and desperately trying to communicate needs I didn't even recognize. The mask had worked so well that even I believed it was real.

But masks aren't meant to be worn forever. They're survival tools that become prisons when you can't take them off.

## The Pattern: A Life in Performance Mode

My masking started early, though I had no name for it then. I learned to survive by becoming a social chameleon. Every interaction became a multiple-choice test where I had to guess the right response.

The mechanics were exhausting. I'd rehearse conversations in the mirror before phone calls. I copied other people's humor, timing, and even their gestures. I avoided using people's names unless I felt completely safe, terrified of getting the social math wrong. Small talk became a scripted performance where I'd cycle through acceptable responses: “Good, how about you?” “Not much, just working.” “Yeah, this weather is crazy.”

The physical suppression was constant. I kept my body still when every fiber wanted to move. I forced eye contact even though it felt like staring into headlights. I learned to swallow my real reactions and produce the ones people expected. Happy became performed happiness. Confusion became performed understanding. Overwhelm became performed calm.

My interests went underground. The things that brought me genuine joy became secrets. I developed fake interests that were socially acceptable conversation fodder. I could discuss movies I'd never watch and hobbies I found boring, all to avoid the judgment that came with being “too intense” about something.

The cost was enormous. Studies indicate that individuals who engage in high levels of autistic masking show significantly elevated rates of anxiety, depression, and suicidality.2 The constant self-monitoring and behavioral suppression keeps your nervous system in a chronic state of threat response. Your body never gets to rest.

I lived in this state for decades. The performance became so automatic that I lost track of where the mask ended and I began. Friends knew my scripted responses but had never met the person underneath. Family members praised me for being “easy-going” when I was actually dissociating through most social interactions. Colleagues saw competence when I was burning through my resources at an unsustainable rate.

The tragedy wasn't that I was hiding my true self. The tragedy was that I'd forgotten it existed.

Every few years, I'd hit a wall. Complete burnout that forced me to change jobs, end relationships, or disappear from social obligations entirely. I thought this was normal. I thought everyone cycled through periods of high performance followed by mysterious collapse. I never connected the dots between the performance and the breakdown.

The pattern was clear in retrospect. Mask harder, crash harder. Perform better, pay more. The energy required to maintain the facade wasn't renewable. It was borrowed from a bank account I could never fully replenish.

## The Protocol: What Unmasking Actually Looks Like

The difference between masked me and unmasked me isn't subtle. It's a complete rewrite of how I exist in the world.

[![Comic-style split panel showing a stressed person juggling masks and conversation scripts on the left, relaxing with fidget toys and genuine interests on the right.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!V6Hz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffae077be-ed77-48d6-b4c7-04b217e80713_1504x1000.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!V6Hz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffae077be-ed77-48d6-b4c7-04b217e80713_1504x1000.png>)

**Masked Me:**

  * Mirrors tone, avoids names

  * Texts to appease, not express

  * Still body, suppresses stimming

  * High performer, always on the edge of burnout

  * Rehearsed, polished, agreeable

  * Contained emotions that eventually erupt

  * Scared to say the wrong thing, so says nothing real

  * Looks impressive. Feels invisible.




**Unmasked Me:**

  * Speaks less, but more honestly

  * Texts at my own pace

  * Micro-stims in plain sight

  * Capable, but no longer proving

  * Has outbursts, but they're real, not dangerous

  * Values comfort over charisma

  * Wants friends who meet me where I am

  * Isn't gifted. Is grounded.

Unmasking is the conscious process of reducing or stopping masking behaviors to live authentically.3 It sounds simple. It's anything but. When you've spent your life performing neurotypicality, removing the performance feels like removing your personality.

The process started small. I began honoring my body's signals instead of overriding them. I started wearing noise-canceling headphones in public. I chose clothes for comfort instead of appearance. I stopped forcing eye contact in conversations and discovered I could actually process what people were saying when I wasn't in sensory pain.

The social part remains the hardest piece. I'm still figuring out how people react to the unmasked version. Some interactions go smoothly. Others feel awkward in ways I'm still learning to navigate.

The challenge isn't just unmasking. It's re-introducing myself to people who only knew the performance. How do you tell a colleague that the agreeable person they've worked with for years was actually overwhelmed most of the time? How do you explain to friends that you need different things now?

I'm experimenting with small changes first. Testing what happens when I give a real answer instead of a scripted one. Seeing how people respond when I ask for what I actually need instead of what I think they want to give. Some people adapt. Others seem confused by the shift.

The physiological changes came faster than the social ones. My anxiety levels dropped. The chronic tension in my shoulders began to release. Sleep improved. The constant background stress of maintaining a performance lifted almost immediately.

But socially, I'm still learning. Still figuring out which relationships can handle authenticity and which were built too specifically on the mask to survive without it.

## Personal Code: Learning to Live Without the Script

Unmasking didn't feel like a revelation. It felt like confusion, grief, silence, small flashes of recognition.

I still don't know exactly who I am without the script. But for the first time in my life, I think the person underneath might be enough.

[![Person sitting peacefully in a cozy reading nook with headphones, cat, plants, and books, with a donation box of discarded masks nearby.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!R8Nz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1a17ce97-c263-4b11-9478-9400b6d57bd8_1504x1000.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!R8Nz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1a17ce97-c263-4b11-9478-9400b6d57bd8_1504x1000.png>)

The grief surprised me. I mourned the friends who never really knew me. I mourned the years spent performing what I thought was love, care, success. I mourned the idea that I was ever supposed to be extraordinary. Most of all, I mourned the version of myself I might have been if I'd never learned to hide.

But grief makes room for something new. I'm not trying to be remarkable anymore. I'm trying to be sustainable. I want to live in a way that doesn't require recovery. I want to relax without guilt. I want to make friends again, not as a persona, but as a person.

The internal dialogue between my masked and unmasked selves has simplified:

Masked Me: People might not like you if you stop performing.  
Unmasked Me: They never really knew me anyway.  
Masked Me: Fair point.

Research confirms what I've experienced firsthand. Unmasking is described as “critical” for autistic mental health because it allows the autonomic nervous system to finally down-regulate from chronic high alert.4 The constant drain on cognitive and emotional resources decreases significantly when you stop performing neurotypicality.

Living authentically isn't easier than masking. It's different. Instead of managing everyone else's comfort, I manage my own needs. Instead of reading social cues like a code I might crack wrong, I communicate directly. Instead of suppressing my natural responses, I work with them.

The world doesn't always accommodate this shift. I've had to become my own advocate in ways that sometimes feel as exhausting as masking. But this exhaustion serves a purpose. It's building something real instead of maintaining something false.

I'm learning that authenticity isn't a destination. It's a daily choice to honor what's true instead of what's expected. Some days are better than others. Some situations still trigger the old performance patterns. But now I have a choice.

The mask was never the problem. It was a survival tool that served its purpose. The problem was believing it was my face.
