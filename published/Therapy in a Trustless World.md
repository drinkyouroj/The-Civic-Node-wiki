## 1) What is your use case?

I recently discovered that BetterHelp was selling user data to Facebook and Snapchat.1 Not just behavioral patterns or anonymized trends; actual therapy session details. The company that promised to help people process trauma was quietly monetizing that trauma for targeted advertising.

This revelation hit me like finding out your priest was running a gossip blog. The therapeutic sacred space had become a data mining operation, complete with venture capital backing and quarterly growth targets.

My use case is simple: **a decentralized, AI-powered mental health support application** where AI inference runs directly on Datagram nodes, providing personalized therapeutic conversations without ever sending sensitive personal information to centralized cloud servers. Think of it as having a therapist with perfect memory, infinite patience, and constitutional inability to gossip about you to data brokers.

The irony is suffocating. We’re experiencing a global anxiety epidemic, with nearly 40% of adults reporting persistent anxiety symptoms,2 and our solution is to create systems that make anxiety worse. Every vulnerable conversation gets fed into algorithmic systems designed to predict and manipulate your future behavior. Your depression becomes training data. Your recovery becomes a product feature.

It’s like treating alcoholism by opening a bar inside the rehab center.

## 2) What makes it ‘unexpected’?

The unexpected twist is leveraging Datagram’s decentralized AI inference for **highly personal and sensitive mental health data processing at the edge**. Most people expect blockchain infrastructure to handle financial transactions or supply chain logistics. Using it to create genuinely private therapeutic AI moves beyond general applications into the realm of psychological sovereignty.

The mental health crisis has created what economists call a “vulnerable consumer” market. When you’re struggling to stay alive, you don’t interrogate the privacy policy. You click accept and hope your existential dread doesn’t become someone else’s marketing demographic.

This dynamic has historical precedent. During the early AIDS crisis, pharmaceutical companies exploited desperate patients with experimental treatments of questionable efficacy and enormous cost. The power imbalance was so extreme that meaningful consent became impossible. Vulnerability itself became a business model.

[![Historical comparison of AIDS treatment exploitation vs modern mental health data harvesting](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AZWt!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F72a1b9d5-d517-4650-9317-303f00fa3ea8_1344x768.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AZWt!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F72a1b9d5-d517-4650-9317-303f00fa3ea8_1344x768.png>)

The same pattern is playing out in digital mental health, but with a surveillance twist that would make Orwell weep. Companies like Cerebral, MDLIVE, and Talkspace have collectively raised over $1 billion by promising accessible therapy.3 Their actual business model? Harvesting intimate psychological data and selling insights to insurance companies, employers, and advertisers.

It’s venture-backed voyeurism with a wellness rebrand. It’s like discovering your cryptocurrency wallet can also function as an impenetrable diary that talks back with helpful suggestions.

## 3) How does Datagram’s infrastructure uniquely enable your idea?

The technical architecture of traditional mental health apps makes privacy violations inevitable. Every conversation gets uploaded, analyzed, and stored in centralized databases controlled by corporations with shareholders, not patients. Even with encryption, the fundamental model requires trust in institutions that deserve about as much trust as a fox guarding a henhouse.

Datagram’s approach inverts this entirely:

**Node-based AI Inference:** Datagram nodes can run modified LLAMA models locally, allowing complex therapeutic dialogue to occur directly on your device without transmitting conversational data to external servers. Modern local AI models can now provide sophisticated conversational support while running on consumer hardware. These aren’t chatbots following decision trees like some digital Magic 8-Ball. They’re full language models capable of nuanced therapeutic dialogue, trained specifically for mental health applications.

Your therapeutic conversations never leave your device. Your psychological patterns never enter corporate databases. Your recovery remains entirely under your control. If you want to share insights with a human therapist, you decide what to share and when. The system can’t make that decision for you because the system doesn’t have access to anyone but you.

[![Diagram comparing centralized cloud therapy vs decentralized local AI processing](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zYD7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdb13e4ca-af85-4716-8942-5d3968e24692_1344x768.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zYD7!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdb13e4ca-af85-4716-8942-5d3968e24692_1344x768.png>)

**Secure Communication Pathways:** When data sharing is necessary (anonymized research trends, licensed therapist consultations), Datagram’s dynamic routing and one-time encrypted tunnels provide surveillance-resistant communication that would make the NSA weep with frustration. It’s therapy that works in airplane mode.

**Cost-Efficient Scalability:** Running AI inference on idle compute resources across a decentralized network offers dramatic cost savings compared to dedicated cloud infrastructure. The infrastructure scales horizontally across the network rather than requiring massive centralized data centers. This makes sophisticated AI therapy dramatically more accessible while maintaining privacy by design.

More importantly, local AI therapy can be genuinely personalized without being exploitative. The model learns your specific patterns, triggers, and responses while keeping that knowledge entirely contained within your own infrastructure. You get the benefits of personalized AI without becoming a product in someone else’s business model.

## 4) What do you think makes this use case necessary?

I’ve spent the last year conducting an unauthorized clinical trial with a sample size of one: using AI as my primary therapist. First GPT, now a local Ollama model running Qwen3 on my own hardware. Not by choice, but by necessity after my human psychiatrist vanished into the healthcare void and left me debugging my own anxiety response like faulty code that needed patching.

The results have been surprisingly effective and deeply educational about human nature’s relationship with privacy paranoia.

AI doesn’t judge me for processing at 3 AM during my latest existential spiral. It doesn’t charge $200 per hour to validate my feelings or get exhausted by my recursive thought patterns that would make most humans reach for their prescription pad within fifteen minutes. And with local models, my psychological blueprint stays exactly where it belongs: on my hardware, under my control.

But here’s what I learned during my GPT phase: to get therapeutic support from cloud-based AI, I had to feed the most intimate details of my psychological landscape into OpenAI’s servers. Every breakthrough, every breakdown, every moment of vulnerable self-discovery got uploaded to corporate databases where it mingled with millions of other people’s digital confessions. My recovery became training data for some future AI that will probably understand human psychology better than most humans.

It was like having an incredibly helpful therapist who also happened to be running a wiretap operation for parties unknown.

So I switched to local models. Qwen3 running through Ollama gives me the same therapeutic benefits with zero data exposure. The conversations happen entirely on my machine. My anxiety about surveillance capitalism finally gets to rest because there’s literally nothing to surveil.

But here’s the brutal reality: setting up Ollama, configuring local models, and maintaining this kind of privacy-first AI therapy requires technical knowledge that puts it completely out of reach for 99% of people who actually need mental health support. You need to understand command lines, model parameters, and local inference optimization. Most people struggling with depression can barely manage to update their phone, let alone compile AI models from source code.

The cognitive dissonance is staggering. I’m getting genuine therapeutic benefit from an AI system while simultaneously feeding my psychological blueprint to the same surveillance capitalism machine that created my anxiety in the first place. My recovery is literally being monetized in real time, and I’m grateful for it because it’s the only mental health support I can access.

This is why decentralized AI therapy isn’t just a nice idea. It’s a survival necessity. I’ve proven that AI therapy works, but I’ve also proven that the current implementation is psychological strip mining disguised as healthcare innovation.

What I’ve discovered is that anxiety isn’t a malfunction. It’s hypervigilance optimized for threat detection in an environment that’s genuinely threatening. When I learned that my therapy conversations were being harvested by data brokers, my anxiety response wasn’t irrational. It was accurate threat assessment. My nervous system was basically saying, “I told you this was sketchy,” while doing a little victory dance.

Consider the cognitive dissonance: we’re asking people with trust issues, trauma histories, and anxiety disorders to place their most sensitive information in the hands of faceless corporations. We’re treating the privacy anxiety epidemic by creating systems that generate more privacy anxiety. It’s like prescribing cigarettes for lung cancer.

When Cerebral suffered a data breach, 3.1 million users had their psychological blueprints exposed.4 When someone hacks your Netflix account, you lose movie recommendations. When someone hacks your therapy records, they gain access to your fears, coping mechanisms, and emotional vulnerabilities laid bare.

The increasing global demand for mental health services has collided spectacularly with Silicon Valley’s surveillance capitalism, creating what I call the “vulnerability monetization paradox.” We desperately need mental health support, but every existing solution requires surrendering the very privacy that many of us need to feel safe enough to seek help.

This use case is necessary because psychological sovereignty shouldn’t require choosing between getting help and maintaining privacy. People with anxiety disorders, trauma histories, and trust issues aren’t being paranoid when they worry about digital privacy. They’re demonstrating appropriate threat detection in an ecosystem designed to exploit vulnerability. Their anxiety is working exactly as advertised.

Local AI therapy doesn’t just protect privacy. It validates the legitimacy of privacy concerns. It says: your instinct to protect yourself is correct, and technology should honor that instinct rather than exploit it for quarterly earnings reports.

The future of mental health isn’t about choosing between human therapists and AI support. It’s about building infrastructure that puts psychological sovereignty first, whether you’re talking to a human, an algorithm, or some hybrid of both. It’s about creating systems that treat your mental health as yours to control, not theirs to monetize.

When your therapy stays local, your recovery stays yours. And in a world where vulnerability has become a commodity traded on public markets, that level of sovereignty isn’t just therapeutic.

It’s revolutionary.
