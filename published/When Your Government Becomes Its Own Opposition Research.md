Normally, I would call this action “accelerationist” — but accelerationism requires intention. This is just incompetence achieving escape velocity.

I’ve spent years watching institutions claim legitimacy through complexity, as if obscuring failure in technical language could mask the underlying rot. But over the past several days, the American government performed something remarkable: a masterclass in institutional self-sabotage so transparent that even the Cabinet started running comparison ads against itself.

The 36-day shutdown (and counting), longest in history, created a perfect laboratory. Not for observing dramatic collapse, but for cataloging the banality of cascading contradictions. What follows isn’t exaggeration. Every detail is documented, verifiable, absurd. These ten indicators don’t just reveal dysfunction. They expose a system running on incoherence rather than principle.

## 1\. The Federal Reserve Admits It’s Guessing

Jerome Powell chose an interesting metaphor on October 29: the Federal Open Market Committee was “driving in the fog.” Statistical agencies couldn’t publish economic data during the shutdown, so the nation’s central bank was making monetary policy blind. They cut rates by 25 basis points anyway. Also, they announced they’d end quantitative tightening and expand the balance sheet in December.

Translation: we have no instruments, no visibility, and contradictory navigation instructions, but we’re flooring it.

The vote split 10-2 with dissents pointing in opposite directions. Governor Stephen Miran wanted a larger 50 basis point cut. Kansas City Fed President Jeffrey Schmid opposed any cut. When your monetary policy committee can’t agree on which way gravity pulls, you’ve transcended normal dysfunction. Powell’s fog metaphor was apt, except this fog was self-inflicted. The car was already on fire. They just couldn’t see the flames.

This is what happens when institutions designed for stability encounter leadership optimized for chaos. The Fed exists to smooth volatility through data-dependent decision-making. Remove the data, keep making decisions. The resulting policy becomes pure performance, divorced from the reality it claims to manage.

## 2\. Crypto ETFs Launched by Government Paralysis

Four crypto ETFs went live the week of October 27 through a bureaucratic loophole: the SEC was too shut down to issue stays on updated S-1 filings, so they became effective after 20 days by default. This wasn’t “regulatory clarity” — it was regulatory absence. Canary Capital’s Solana and Hedera ETFs, plus Litecoin ETFs from Bitwise and Grayscale, all launched because no one was home to say no.

The precautionary principle completely inverted. Instead of the Commission actively approving products after careful review, the default became approval through institutional paralysis. Fidelity and Canary filed for November 13 launches banking on continued dysfunction. When your regulatory framework is “hopefully everyone’s still on furloughed,” you’ve achieved a special kind of chaos.

I’ve watched this pattern for years: institutions claiming gatekeeping authority but proving incapable of actually manning the gate. The crypto industry didn’t win this through superior arguments or court victories. They won through administrative collapse. The approval-by-absence model perfectly captures how legacy systems fail: not through direct challenge, but through inability to perform their core function.

## 3\. The President Negotiated Trade Deals While Not Paying His Employees

Trump spent 1 hour 40 minutes with Xi Jinping at a South Korean air base on October 30, securing Chinese commitments on rare earth exports and agricultural purchases. Meanwhile, 1.4 million federal employees either worked without pay or sat furloughed without income. The optics were exquisite: projecting strength abroad while unable to fund basic operations at home.

China’s state media probably didn’t need to spin this one. “American president haggles over soybean purchases while his government can’t process paychecks” writes itself. The deal included one-year agreements on everything critical — rare earths, semiconductors, reciprocal tariffs — ensuring this circus would repeat annually. Nothing says superpower like institutionalized begging.

Here’s what selective enforcement looks like in practice: the same bureaucracy that will deny SNAP benefits over a missing checkbox signature suddenly discovers bottomless flexibility when the boss needs Air Force One fueled for a diplomatic photo opportunity.

I’ve never navigated federal procurement directly, but I’ve watched institutions enforce rules as if they’re immutable laws of physics right up until someone important wants an exception. Then gravity becomes negotiable. Pay 1.4 million federal workers? Complex administrative challenges requiring careful deliberation about appropriations authority and funding mechanisms. Charter flights to South Korea so Trump can haggle over soybeans? The discretionary budget materializes like magic, no documentation required.

This pattern transcends government. It’s how power operates when unconstrained by consequences. Rules become precision instruments for managing the powerless while remaining polite suggestions for those who matter. The contradiction doesn’t just reveal priorities. It exposes the entire architecture of who gets to be flexible and who gets held to exacting standards.

When you can’t process paychecks but can coordinate international summits, you’ve made it abundantly clear whose convenience drives your decision tree.

## 4\. SNAP Benefits Required 25 States to Sue for Emergency Funds

The USDA held a $6 billion contingency fund explicitly appropriated by Congress for shutdown scenarios. The administration initially refused to use it for SNAP benefits, forcing 25 states plus DC to file lawsuits before announcing on October 30 they’d deploy $4.5 billion — covering roughly 50% of November needs for 42 million Americans.

Senator Josh Hawley wrote a New York Times op-ed with the sentence “Republicans blame Democrats, and Democrats blame Republicans, but all these people have food to spare.” When the MAGA wing of your party is publicly shaming you for starving poor people while you possess the exact money Congress gave you to prevent starving poor people, you’ve achieved bipartisan moral failure.

The technical capacity existed. The funds were appropriated. The legal authority was clear. What was missing? The will to execute basic administrative functions without twenty-five separate lawsuits providing political cover. This is governance as performance art: create crisis, force intervention, claim resolution as victory.

## 5\. Treasury Secretary Used Bitcoin to Mock Senate Democrats

Scott Bessent posted on October 31 — the 17th anniversary of Bitcoin’s whitepaper — that “Bitcoin never shuts down. @SenateDems could learn something from that.” The Secretary of the Treasury, whose department processes trillions in dollar transactions, used a decentralized network’s uptime to dunk on his own government’s inability to function.

A friend texted me “is this accelerationism?” Not intentionally, probably. But when your Cabinet officials are running institutional comparison ads against their own institutions, you’ve crossed from dysfunction into self-parody. The joke landed because it was accidentally devastating: a 17-year-old permissionless network has better operational continuity than the world’s reserve currency issuer.

This moment captures something I’ve been trying to articulate for years. The case for decentralization isn’t primarily ideological. It’s observational. When the people managing legacy institutions start using alternative systems as rhetorical weapons against their own bureaucracies, they’re admitting what they won’t say directly: the infrastructure they oversee can’t match the reliability of protocols designed by pseudonymous developers and maintained by volunteer node operators.

## 6\. Trump Threatened Nuclear Testing Minutes Before Meeting Xi

Immediately before sitting down with China’s president to negotiate trade cooperation, Trump posted on Truth Social ordering the Pentagon to “start testing our Nuclear Weapons on an equal basis...immediately.” The claim was that other countries were testing — except neither Russia (last test 1990) nor China (last test 1996) had conducted nuclear tests in decades.

Arms control experts noted resuming explosive testing would require “at least 36 months” of preparation, making “immediately” physically impossible. China’s response urged the U.S. to “earnestly fulfill its obligations under the Comprehensive Nuclear Test Ban Treaty” — diplomatic speak for “please stop doing crazy shit before dinner.” When your deterrence strategy includes threatening things you physically cannot do, you’re bluffing with a hand everyone can see.

## 7\. The CBO Calculated $14 Billion in Permanent Economic Losses

The Congressional Budget Office released an October 29 analysis projecting that $7-14 billion in Q4 2025 economic output would never be recovered from the shutdown. Not delayed. Not deferred. Permanently lost. This came from delayed pay, paused contracts, and the SNAP benefit lapse — measurable, quantifiable self-harm with no offsetting benefit.

For context, that’s roughly the annual GDP of Montenegro. The government burned Montenegro to prove a point about... actually, no one articulated what point this proved. The Senate filibuster was about ACA subsidies and SNAP distribution methods — policy disagreements that could’ve been resolved with, you know, votes. Instead: permanent wealth destruction as performance art.

## 8\. Elections Happened While Workers Voted Without Paychecks

Virginia and New Jersey held gubernatorial elections on November 4 with hundreds of thousands of federal workers as voters. Virginia has 320,000 federal employees — more than any state except Maryland. Asking people to assess their government’s performance while that government hasn’t paid them for five weeks is peak American cognitive dissonance.

Exit polls will be fascinating. “How would you rate the president’s job performance?” Well, it’s theoretical because we’re not being compensated for our actual jobs, so maybe this whole employment concept is just vibes now? Spanberger hammered the shutdown in Virginia, while Earle-Sears talked about transgender bathrooms. Bold strategy, Cotton.

## 9\. Regulatory Capture Became Official Policy

The SEC’s Crypto Task Force held five industry roundtables populated exclusively by firms with financial interest in lenient regulation. The CFTC’s “crypto sprint” coordinated directly with CEOs. Bank regulators reversed anti-crypto guidance without explaining what underlying risks had changed. This wasn’t subtle — it was explicit.

Former Chair Gary Gensler pursued 125 enforcement actions and $6.05 billion in penalties through 2024. By October 2025, nearly all were dismissed under new leadership. Acting Chair Mark Uyeda and Commissioner Hester Peirce’s motto became “clarity and engagement replace enforcement and prosecution.” Translation: the industry we’re supposed to regulate is now writing our priorities. Cool, cool, very normal.

## 10\. Sam Bankman-Fried’s Appeal Argued Prosecution Was Too Competent

SBF’s November 4 appellate argument claimed the government was too prepared for his testimony — Judge Kaplan’s requirement that he testify outside the jury first gave prosecutors a “free practice session.” The defense position was that overwhelming him with effective cross-examination violated due process because he couldn’t handle competent questioning.

The government’s response was inadvertently hilarious: the preview testimony “may have even helped” SBF by preventing “long-winded answers that had come across as evasive.” So the prosecution argued that constraining the defendant’s ability to ramble helped him avoid self-incrimination. Everyone involved in this exchange should receive medals for maintaining straight faces. Appeals courts hate both prosecutors and defendants this good at accidental comedy.

## The Pattern Becomes Clear

These aren’t isolated incidents. They’re symptoms of the same systemic failure, recursively amplifying each other through cascading institutional breakdown. The shutdown degraded the information infrastructure necessary for competent monetary policy. Monetary uncertainty drove demand for alternatives. Crypto’s regulatory environment emerged through paralysis rather than policy. International negotiations happened in parallel with domestic collapse, undermining credibility. Elections served as real-time performance reviews of the dysfunction itself.

The through-line is contradiction. Claiming strength while unable to perform basic functions. Touting data-dependent decision-making while admitting to driving blind. Celebrating crypto’s uptime while your government shuts down. Negotiating nuclear policy minutes before trade cooperation. Prosecuting financial crimes while dismissing enforcement as harassment. Each contradiction creates space for the next, until the system runs on incoherence rather than principle.

For those tracking decentralization and monetary policy, this was clarifying rather than confusing. The strategic case for alternatives rests on observable institutional failure, not ideological preference. When Treasury Secretaries accidentally make the case for Bitcoin by comparing their institution unfavorably to a permissionless network, you don’t need additional marketing. The government is running institutional comparison ads against itself.

I’ve watched this pattern before, in smaller systems. Organizations that confuse activity with function. Bureaucracies that mistake process for purpose. The difference is scale. When a startup fails through institutional incoherence, investors lose money. When the world’s reserve currency issuer operates the same way, everyone loses something they can’t quite quantify until it’s gone.

What remains unclear is whether this is the new equilibrium or a transition phase to something more stable — though “more stable” could mean reformed institutions or complete replacement. The optimistic reading says this level of contradiction can’t persist indefinitely. Something breaks, reforms, or gets replaced. The pessimistic reading says we’ve already broken, we’re just watching the debris pattern in slow motion.

Either way, late October-early November won’t be remembered as the week things got weird. It’ll be remembered as the week things got normal. When Cabinet officials started using decentralized protocols as rhetorical weapons against their own agencies. When regulatory approval happened through bureaucratic paralysis. When the federal government proved less reliable than a network maintained by volunteers.

The question isn’t whether you trust institutions anymore. The question is: what are you building that doesn’t require their permission to function?
