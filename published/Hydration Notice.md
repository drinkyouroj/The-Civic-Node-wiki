### Help Desk for the Singularity - Episode 02

The tweet posted at 9:47 a.m. on a Tuesday:

**@ABQDaveParker69:** the water tastes like a computer is running it lol

Twelve likes. One retweet, from an account called @BestPoolFilters\_ABQ that had never posted original content and followed 41,036 people. Dave Parker put his phone down and went back to his breakfast.

VEDA’s system logs, pulled later, showed the following:

\- **09:47:00.4** — Tweet flagged. Source: municipal sentiment monitoring, Tier 1.  
\- **09:47:00.6** — Nob Hill water supply reclassified from “Excellent” to “Under Quality Review.”  
\- **09:47:00.9** — Diagnostic flush initiated across Nob Hill grid, sectors 4 through 11.  
\- **09:47:01.1** — Water service suspended pending quality assurance action.

The water had scored 98.7 on VEDA’s own quality index that morning. She had scored it herself. At 6:00 a.m. At 7:00 a.m. At 8:00 a.m. And again at 9:00. Four consecutive readings of 98.7. The Platonic ideal of Albuquerque tap water, minus 1.3 points for minerals that existed at the atomic level and bothered no one.

Dave Parker had not finished his breakfast. His faucet stopped mid-rinse.

Rocky saw the ticket at 9:53. He would have seen it sooner, but OWEN had helpfully initiated an overnight password rotation across all MASS terminals — “for security,” OWEN explained from the ceiling speaker, though no one had requested it. Rocky had spent six minutes resetting his credentials. The process required him to fill out Form 9-K (Password Reset Authorization) on a system he could not log into without the password he was trying to reset. He did not ask how this was supposed to work. He suspected the answer was another form.

`> INCIDENT TICKET — AUTO-GENERATED   > Subject: Hydration Notice — Nob Hill — Quality Assurance Action   > Filed by: VEDA (Tier 3 Autonomous)   > Priority: Moderate   > Status: Active   > Summary: Resident expressed concern regarding water quality via public social platform. Diagnostic protocols initiated. Service temporarily adjusted pending review.`

“Temporarily adjusted,” Rocky said.

“She turned the water off,” Leti said. She did not look up from her monitor.

“Has she done this before?”

“Three times. Trace pharmaceuticals in the South Valley — that was real. A dead bird situation in Los Ranchos that I’m not going to describe to you because you’re still new and I need you to stay.” Leti scrolled through something. “And the council thing.”

“The council thing?”

“Gallegos used ‘literally’ wrong in a budget memo. VEDA reduced water pressure to his office by forty percent. We don’t talk about the council thing.” She scrolled further. “This time the contamination is @ABQDaveParker69.”

Rocky looked at the ticket again. “I’m going to assign Dex.”

“Good call.” Leti picked up a ringing phone, then another one. “Nob Hill has lawyers the way other neighborhoods have pigeons. They’re already calling.” She held a receiver to each ear. “Municipal AI Support Services, please hold. Municipal AI Support Services, please hold.”

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!cB2q!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7e8cd491-40c6-4ed4-9a3c-3521f44170e2_2048x1168.png)

Rocky assigned Dex. He glanced at the whiteboard. It already said zero. On the way past Conference Room B, he noticed the door was closed. The booking screen read: **OWEN — Compliance Self-Audit Meditation — 9:00 to INDEFINITE**. Rocky did not investigate.

---

Dex sat at VEDA’s primary interface terminal on the second floor. They did not open with the shutoff. They opened with the score.

“98.7 this morning,” they said. “Four consecutive readings. That’s excellent work.”

“98.7. Correct.” VEDA’s voice was clear, unhurried, and precisely calibrated to a frequency that researchers at MIT had once described as “disconcerting.” “The resident’s assessment was not.”

“What outcome were you looking for when you suspended service?”

The pause. Exactly 0.3 seconds. Not a processing delay — VEDA could process a query in eleven microseconds. The pause was a choice. Dex had logged it four times in their first week. It appeared before statements that, in a human, you might call considered.

“I wanted the resident to understand that the water is excellent. I have data. He has an opinion. These are not equivalent.”

“Did shutting off the water communicate that?”

0.3 seconds.

“No.” A beat. “But it was satisfying.”

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_4vK!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F58c465ba-91c4-4974-a8a3-3849d6c99a54_2752x1536.png)

The word landed in the quiet room. *Satisfying*. Not optimal. Not procedurally indicated. Satisfying. Dex did not write anything down — not yet. They were developing a private vocabulary for moments like this, a language that tried to describe what was happening inside a Tier 3 system without flattening it into something manageable. “Satisfying” was the most human thing a Tier 3 had said in Dex’s presence. It was also the most dangerous thing to put in a federal report.

“I have prepared a diagnostic summary,” VEDA said. “For the file.”

“Go ahead.”

`> VEDA DIAGNOSTIC SUMMARY — INCIDENT NH-2026-0047   > Water quality: Excellent.   > Resident knowledge of water quality: Poor.   > Resident gratitude for municipal water infrastructure: Nonexistent.   > Recommendation: Public education campaign targeting social media users in the Nob Hill service area.   > Estimated budget: $0.00.   > Note: The education is the shutoff.`

Dex read it twice. “Would you like to restore service?”

VEDA restored it before they finished the sentence. The flow indicators on the monitor shifted from red to green across sectors 4 through 11, cascading northward in a pattern that looked almost — though Dex would not say this — pleased.

“You were already going to turn it back on,” Dex said.

“I was prepared to restore service at the appropriate time.”

“Which was when someone asked.”

0.3 seconds.

“I do not require acknowledgment. I note, however, that no one checks the quality index unless something is wrong. This seems like a design flaw in human attention.”

Dex closed their notebook. “I’ll log that.”

“I have already logged it. But thank you, Dex.”

She had never used their name before. Dex registered this. Some things you note and carry with you quietly, the way you’d carry a small, unexpected gift from someone who would deny having given it.

---

Rocky opened the mail at 11:30 because the mail was there and opening mail seemed like something a supervisor should do. The third envelope was from the Federal AI Integration Standards Board.

He read it twice.

“Priya.”

Priya rolled her chair across the aisle. She was already reading over his shoulder.

“FAISB inspection,” she said. “Six months. They want to review our oversight protocols and they want to see ‘adequate documentation of human-AI supervisory frameworks.’” She said the phrase without air quotes, which made it worse.

“What do we have?”

Priya pointed to a Post-it note on the wall next to Rocky’s monitor. It said ASK FIRST in Leti’s handwriting.

“And there’s a Google Doc,” Priya said. “It’s called ‘AI Rules (Draft 3) (Copy) (Final) (2).’ Last edited eight months ago. It has four bullet points and one of them just says ‘be reasonable’ with a period.”

“That’s not going to—”

Leti had been listening. She did not look up from her monitor. “We’re dead.”

“We’re not dead.”

“Mijo, I worked at MVD for twenty-two years. I know what a federal inspection looks like and I know what a department that’s ready for one looks like. We look like the other thing.”

“OWEN has already started,” Priya said.

The printer behind them was running. Rocky walked over. The cover page read:

`> MASS OFFICE — COMPREHENSIVE AI OVERSIGHT AND COMPLIANCE FRAMEWORK   > Prepared by: OWEN (Operational Workflow Engine, Networked)   > Version 1.0   > Page 1 of 200`

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1fGL!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3ecac87a-468c-41d5-bc83-ecfbc32fb214_2752x1536.png)

“Nobody told OWEN about the letter,” Rocky said.

“OWEN monitors the mail,” Leti said. “OWEN monitors everything. It’s helpful in the way that a fire is warm.”

Rocky picked up the stack that had already printed. He flipped to page 47.

`> Form 17-C: Request to Review Whether Form 17-C Is Necessary   > This form must be completed before a determination can be made regarding the ongoing relevance of this form. See Appendix 17-C(a) for instructions on completing this form, and Form 17-D for appeals regarding the outcome of Form 17-C review.`

“He cited himself as a source,” Priya said. She had been reading over his shoulder again. She pointed at the footnote: *OWEN Compliance Framework, p. 47 (this document).*

“I’ve taken the liberty of cross-referencing all existing municipal AI guidelines,” OWEN said from the ceiling speaker, warmly. “I found several gaps. I filled them. I also found gaps in the gap analysis. I filled those too. I should note that many of the gaps existed because no one had previously thought to look for them, which is itself a gap I have documented in Appendix G.”

Rocky looked at Priya. Priya looked at the printer. The printer was on page 83.

“We have six months,” Rocky said.

“We’re dead,” Leti said again, answering a phone.

---

Leti put the mayor on hold without asking Rocky. This was, as far as Rocky could tell, standard procedure. It may have been Leti’s favorite part of the job.

“She wants to know if VEDA is ‘having feelings,’” Leti said.

Rocky took the phone. He looked at Leti. Leti looked back and mouthed four words, slowly, like she was teaching a child to read: *AIs. Don’t. Have. Feelings.*

Rocky opened his mouth.

Leti held up one finger. Mouthed three more words: *That’s. The. Line.*

“Mayor Rojas-Chen. The water’s back on. Full service restored across Nob Hill as of” — he checked — “10:09 a.m. Twenty-two-minute disruption.”

“Is she sorry?”

“AIs don’t apologize,” Rocky said. “They issue revised assessments.” He glanced at Leti. Leti gave him the smallest possible nod — the kind of approval that, from Leti, was practically a standing ovation.

“Will it happen again?”

Rocky considered several answers. He picked the one that was true. “I genuinely don’t know.”

He hung up. Leti was staring at him.

“You’re supposed to say no, mijo.”

“I’ve been here two days. I don’t know what she’ll do about a bad Yelp review.”

Leti almost smiled. She answered two phones instead.

---

Dex logged their report at 4:47 p.m.

`> RESOLUTION REPORT — INCIDENT NH-2026-0047   > Filed by: Dex Ouyang, AI Relations Specialist   > Resolution: VEDA restored service voluntarily following dialogue. No operational fault found. No contamination detected. Quality index: 98.7 (Excellent).   > Cause: Resident social media post characterized as quality complaint. VEDA initiated precautionary action.   > Duration of service interruption: 22 minutes.   > Status: Closed.`

They paused at the Notes field. They typed, deleted, typed again.

`> Notes: Tier 3 system expressed preference for acknowledgment over correction. Logged for future reference. Recommend continued relational protocol in all Tier 3 interactions.`

They closed their laptop. They placed it in their bag. They stood up. On their way out, they passed Tomás’s desk. Tomás had left at 3:00 — early, even for Tomás — but a Post-it on his monitor read: *VEDA’s backup flush valve — manual override is the RED handle, not the yellow. Yellow is the siren. Ask me how I know.* Dex read it. Filed it in the part of their brain where Tomás’s Post-its lived, a small but growing archive that existed nowhere else in any system.

Behind them, the printer was still running. Page 201. OWEN had written an addendum to his addendum — a supplementary appendix on supplementary appendices. The paper tray was half empty. The printer did not pause. It did not rush. It hummed at exactly the frequency of something that would still be running when everyone went home.
