Take Michigan. The state that builds America's cars now faces the biggest tariff headache in the country. Companies there paid $3.3 billion in tariff costs in just five months of 2025. That makes Michigan the third most impacted state nationwide.1

Here's what actually happened when Trump declared "Liberation Day" and restructured global trade.

### The Japan Deal: $550 Billion and What It Really Means

Trump called it "perhaps the largest Deal ever made."2 Japan agreed to invest $550 billion in the United States. The president claimed America would "receive 90% of the Profits" from this investment.3

What that $550 billion actually includes:

  * 100 Boeing aircraft purchases

  * 75% increase in U.S. rice purchases

  * $8 billion in other agricultural products

  * Joint LNG development in Alaska4




The deal reduced the threatened 25% tariff on Japanese cars to 15%. Japanese automaker stocks surged. Toyota, Honda, and Mazda all saw immediate gains.5

Here's the catch. The 50% tariffs on Japanese steel and aluminum stayed in place. Those weren't part of the deal.6

### The Auto Industry Gets Hammered

No sector took a bigger hit than automotive. The numbers tell the story.

The Center for Automotive Research calculated that tariffs will raise production costs for all domestic automakers by $107.7 billion in 2025. The Detroit Three alone face $41.9 billion in additional costs.7

General Motors reported a 35% drop in second-quarter profits. They attributed $1.1 billion directly to new tariffs.8 Stellantis warned of a $2.68 billion loss for the first half of 2025. Ford expects a $1.5 billion hit to operating profit.9

Vehicle prices will rise. University of Michigan economists estimate the average cost increase at $6,220 per vehicle if automakers pass all costs to consumers.10

### Michigan Bears the Brunt

Michigan sits at the center of this trade disruption. The state depends heavily on automotive manufacturing and trade with Canada. More than 234,000 jobs in Detroit alone rely on trade and investment with Canada.11

The University of Michigan forecasts that tariffs will directly eliminate 13,000 Michigan jobs over the next few years.12 A Washtenaw County business survey found 35% of companies experienced significant negative impacts. They reported higher raw material costs, reduced profit margins, hiring delays, and postponed growth plans.13

The Detroit Regional Chamber calls the policy a "yo-yo tariff policy" that creates massive disruption.14

### The Manufacturing Paradox

The administration designed these tariffs to force companies to move production back to the United States. It works, sort of.

General Motors announced $4 billion in spending over two years to expand plants in Michigan, Kansas, and Tennessee. The goal: build more vehicles domestically and avoid import tariffs.15

Ford and other automakers make similar moves. These investments come at a steep price. Companies fund this re-shoring by absorbing massive losses and squeezing profit margins.

This reactive capital reallocation wastes money. Money that could fund research and development goes to emergency factory expansions instead. Companies build domestic capacity under pressure, not as part of strategic growth plans.

### The Bigger Economic Picture

Multiple research institutions agree: the tariffs damage the broader economy.

The Yale Budget Lab projects tariffs will reduce U.S. GDP growth by 0.9% in 2025. Average households will lose $2,800 in income.16 The Penn Wharton Budget Model forecasts worse long-term damage, with GDP falling 6% over time.17

Manufacturing output might grow 2.6%. Construction contracts 4.1% and agriculture shrinks 0.8%. The net effect on employment: a loss of 641,000 jobs and unemployment rising 0.5 percentage points.

Consumer prices will jump. Shoes and apparel could cost 40-44% more. Food prices will rise over 4%.18

### Winners and Losers

Some sectors benefit. Protected industries like steel and aluminum gain market share. U.S. agriculture gets new access to Japanese markets through the trade deal.19

The automotive sector pays the biggest price. The industry's global supply chains make it vulnerable to any tariff regime. Parts cross borders multiple times before final assembly. Each border crossing adds costs.

The administration accepts this economic pain as the price of reshaping global supply chains away from China. They view reduced GDP and higher inflation as necessary costs for achieving national security objectives.

### What Comes Next

The August 1 deadline is approaching… with mixed results. Some deals are done. Others face ongoing uncertainty.

Companies now operate in a world where trade policy changes rapidly and unpredictably. Long-term planning becomes difficult when tariff rates can shift based on political negotiations.

[![Business executives in suits standing at neon-lit crossroads with directional signs reading "TRADE WAR," "NEW DEALS," and "UNCERTAINTY" under stormy skies.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Pq9f!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fea257b99-166a-4b34-bef7-4641c860c653_1392x752.png)](<https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Pq9f!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fea257b99-166a-4b34-bef7-4641c860c653_1392x752.png>)

The old globalization playbook no longer works. Companies need multiple manufacturing locations, scenario-based financial planning, and constant political risk assessment.

American manufacturing is changing under the new trade rules. Whether it gets stronger remains an open question. The early evidence suggests the transition costs more than expected and delivers fewer benefits than promised.

Michigan workers and automotive companies learn this lesson the hard way.
