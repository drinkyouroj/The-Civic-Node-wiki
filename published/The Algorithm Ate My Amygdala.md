### 7 Ways Algorithms Killed Your Ability to Care About NATO Collapsing

*Before we dive in, I want to talk about something that feels increasingly critical: media literacy. Not the condescending “here’s how to spot fake news” variety that treats you like a child who can’t read a URL. The real kind. The uncomfortable kind that starts with acknowledging that everyone, including the person writing this sentence, gets things wrong.*

*So I’m trying something new. I’ve opened comments to free subscribers specifically to encourage you to call me out anywhere you think I’ve made a mistake, misrepresented research, or let my own biases cloud the analysis. This isn’t performative humility. This is survival. If we can’t build systems where people can challenge claims without being labeled enemies, we’re cooked.*

*The irony isn’t lost on me that I’m writing about algorithmic echo chambers while publishing on a platform that could very easily become one. The difference, I hope, is that I’m actively inviting the counter-evidence. If you think I’m wrong about something in here, tell me. Bring sources. Let’s figure it out together.*

*Media literacy starts with admitting you might be the one who needs correcting.*

*Now, about those algorithms destroying your brain...*

## The Spark: When Geopolitical Crisis Becomes Wallpaper

Trump just threatened to slap tariffs on eight NATO allies because they won’t let him buy Greenland.

Read that sentence again. Denmark, Norway, Sweden, France, Germany, the UK, the Netherlands, and Finland now face 25% tariffs by June unless they surrender sovereign territory to a man who thinks international diplomacy works like haggling over a condo in Atlantic City.

This should be the biggest story of your life. NATO has kept the peace for 77 years. The Atlantic Council says the alliance is experiencing “what could become the worst crisis of its existence.” The post-WWII order that prevented global conflagration for eight decades is crumbling in real time while a US president threatens military allies with economic warfare.

You scrolled past it between a recipe video and someone’s take on celebrity drama.

![Split screen comparison showing identical user interface treatment for NATO crisis news article and cat video, demonstrating algorithmic feeds make no architectural distinction between geopolitical threats and entertainment content](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tTdI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1ada2b8e-0441-4941-99ed-b49270fa4628_2048x1168.png)

Same dopamine delivery mechanism. Different civilizational stakes.

Not apathy. Architecture.

For years I built the feeds that are now destroying your ability to distinguish between entertainment and existential threat. We optimized every pixel for engagement while systematically eliminating the difference between “important” and “interesting.” Nobody set out to kill democracy. We just needed you to click one more time.

Now those same systems ensure you can’t sustain attention on the collapse of the security order that’s kept you alive your entire life. The algorithm didn’t just give you an echo chamber. It gave you outrage fatigue, reality fragmentation, and a dopamine delivery system so effective that NATO’s dissolution feels like background noise.

What follows are seven specific mechanisms that killed your ability to care.

## 1\. The Repetition Hammer: Why You’ve Seen “Greenland” 87 Times and Still Don’t Care

Repetition doesn’t create urgency. Repetition creates numbness.

Trump floated buying Greenland in 2019. Then again in 2025. Now in early 2026, he’s weaponizing tariffs and threatening military action. Each time, your feed showed you the headline. Each time, you saw the outrage. Each time, nothing happened. Pattern recognition kicked in. Brain learns fast. “Greenland news” equals “scroll past this, nothing changes.”

Psychologists call this semantic satiation. Repeat a word enough times and it loses meaning. Say “Greenland” eighty-seven times in a row until it becomes pure mouth noise, divorced from the actual ice-covered landmass with approximately 56,000 people who definitely don’t want to be American. The syllables become sounds. The concept evaporates.

Feed algorithms weaponized this effect.

Research published in 2025 shows that Facebook’s recommendation algorithms create stronger echo chambers than any other platform precisely because they optimize for what you’ve already seen.[^1] The system learns you clicked “Trump threatens thing” in 2019. Serves you variants forever. Except now the threat isn’t performative. NATO members are deploying troops to Greenland. European leaders are coordinating retaliatory tariffs. Germany is sending military forces to counter potential American aggression. Yes, Germany.

Think about that. German troops deploying to prevent American territorial expansion. In 2026.

You’d know this matters if algorithms hadn’t trained you to treat “Trump threatens X” as a genre of content rather than escalating crisis. The repetition didn’t inform you. The repetition inoculated you against caring.

## 2\. The Outrage Slot Machine: When Dopamine Hits Replaced Sustained Attention

Your news feed functions as a variable-reward delivery system, and variable-reward systems are the most addictive mechanism humans have ever engineered.

Slot machines work because you never know which pull delivers the jackpot. Your feed works the same way. Scroll, scroll, scroll, dopamine hit. Scroll, scroll, scroll, dopamine hit. Content doesn’t matter. Pattern does.

I built these systems. We measured “time in app” and “sessions per day” because those metrics drove ad revenue. Know what we didn’t measure? “Did the user learn something that makes them a more informed citizen?” That question never appeared in a single product meeting I attended. Not once. Not ever.

Studies from 2025 demonstrate that algorithmic systems privilege “novelty and emotion over accuracy,” actively eroding what researchers call “epistemic safeguards.” [^2] Translation: the math makes you dumber on purpose. Not because engineers are evil. Because dopamine-optimized systems structurally cannot prioritize truth over engagement.

Understanding NATO’s collapse requires sustained attention. Deep reading. Connecting dots across multiple sources. Building a coherent mental model of cascading consequences. Sitting with discomfort long enough to grasp second-order and third-order effects.

Your feed trained you to scroll.

The NATO crisis requires the opposite of what your feed engineered you to do. So you don’t do it. You can’t do it. The muscle atrophied from disuse. Like asking someone who’s only ever sprinted to run a marathon. Wrong training. Wrong system.

When I was getting clean, my therapist explained that addiction isn’t about the substance. Addiction is about the pattern. Your brain learns a specific sequence: discomfort triggers behavior, behavior delivers relief, relief reinforces pattern. Doesn’t matter if the behavior is drugs or scrolling. Pattern’s the same.

Your feed is the pattern. NATO collapsing is the discomfort. Scrolling is the relief. Nothing changes.

## 3\. The Engagement Hostage Situation: How Platforms Profit From Your Numbness

Facebook doesn’t lose money when you become desensitized to geopolitical crisis. Facebook makes more money.

Numbness drives engagement. Overwhelm your capacity to process importance and you default to whatever the algorithm serves next. Cat video? Sure. Recipe carousel? Why not. Fifteen seconds of someone yelling about pronouns? The dopamine hits the same.

The business model requires your incapacity.

Every second you spend trying to understand NATO’s structural collapse is a second you’re not generating ad impressions. Deep reading is expensive. Scrolling is profitable. Reading a 3,000-word Atlantic Council analysis on transatlantic security architecture costs Meta zero ad revenue for three thousand words of your attention. Serving you forty three-second videos in that same timeframe? Forty ad impressions. Do the math.

Meta’s 2025 financial reports show strong engagement growth while press freedom rankings hit their lowest point in US history.[^3] Those aren’t contradictory data points. They’re the same phenomenon measured from different angles. As institutional journalism dies, algorithmic feeds get stronger. More content, less gatekeeping; infinite scroll, infinite profit.

You think you’re choosing to scroll past the NATO crisis. You’re not choosing. Feed architecture made sustained attention economically impossible to support. The platforms won. You lost. Democracy is the collateral damage.

## 4\. The Reality Fragmentation Bomb: 100 Million Echo Chambers vs. 32 NATO Members

NATO has 32 member nations attempting to maintain consensus about collective defense. Research analyzing over 100 million pieces of content shows Facebook users clustering in ideological echo chambers that no longer share basic facts about reality.[^4]

Do the math on that ratio.

Studies published in January 2025 found that Facebook produces the strongest echo chamber effects of any major platform.[^5] Users don’t just cluster with like-minded people. The algorithm actively prevents exposure to counter-evidence. You’re not choosing your bubble. The recommendation system is choosing for you based on what keeps you clicking.

This creates something worse than disagreement. This creates incompatible realities.

One bubble sees Trump defending America from European freeloaders who’ve been exploiting US military protection for decades. Another bubble sees Trump destroying the alliance that prevented World War III. Both bubbles have data. Both have narratives. Both have internal logical consistency. Neither can persuade the other because they’re not watching the same movie.

When you take apart a radio, every component has to agree on voltage and frequency. Mismatch those basic parameters and nothing works. Doesn’t matter how sophisticated your circuit design is. Without shared electrical reality, you get noise.

Shared reality fragments; collective action becomes structurally impossible. You can’t build consensus about NATO’s importance when half the population has been algorithmically fed a five-year narrative that NATO is a protection racket and the other half can’t understand why that framing gained traction. The components don’t share voltage anymore. Just noise.

The alliance that kept the peace for 77 years requires consensus about external threats. Algorithms make consensus mathematically impossible.

## 5\. The Importance Inverter: Why Cat Videos Get Same UX as Geopolitical Crisis

Open your feed right now. Find a video of a cat doing something cute. Note the interface: autoplay video, engagement buttons, recommended content below, infinite scroll enabled.

Now find coverage of Trump threatening NATO allies. Same interface. Exact same UX patterns. Autoplay video, engagement buttons, recommended content, infinite scroll.

The platform makes no architectural distinction between “your brain needs this information to understand threats to your continued survival” and “haha look at the kitty.” Both produce engagement. Both generate ad revenue. Both get identical treatment.

Not bias. Worse than bias. This is the complete absence of editorial judgment replaced by engagement optimization.

Traditional journalism had problems. Plenty of problems. But traditional journalism had an architecture for importance. Front page versus buried inside. Lead story versus human interest. Column inches signaled weight. The interface told you what mattered.

Algorithmic feeds eliminated that signal entirely.

Everything is equally sized, equally promoted, equally consumable. The only variable is whether you personally clicked similar content before. If you’ve never shown interest in NATO, the algorithm won’t suddenly decide you need to care just because the alliance is collapsing. The algorithm will show you more of whatever you clicked yesterday. The algorithm has no conception of “importance” outside of “does this specific user engage with this specific content type.”

Your feed doesn’t know NATO exists. Your feed only knows what makes you click.

In my recovery, I learned that the most dangerous lies are the ones that feel like truth. The scroll feels like staying informed. Feels like you’re keeping up, maintaining awareness, being a responsible citizen. You’re not. You’re training your nervous system to treat crisis as background noise.

## 6\. The Desensitization Protocol: How Algorithms Trained You to Scroll Past Danger

Repeated exposure to threatening stimuli without consequence produces habituation.

Neuroscience research shows your amygdala stops firing when a threat appears repeatedly but never materializes into actual danger.[^6] The alarm weakens. Eventually, your brain categorizes the stimulus as irrelevant. This adaptation is useful when the stimulus actually is irrelevant. Traffic noise outside your window. Neighbor’s dog barking. Normal environmental patterns.

Catastrophic when the stimulus is “the military alliance preventing global war is disintegrating.”

Your feed showed you Trump threatening things for eight years. Very few consequences materialized in your immediate life. Your brain learned: “Trump threatens thing” equals “ignore this, nothing happens.” Pattern recognition. Efficient adaptation. Survival mechanism working exactly as designed.

Now Trump’s threatening NATO with tariffs while European nations coordinate military responses, and your amygdala is silent. The alarm broke. The algorithm broke it.

This is why you can’t care even when you want to. Not moral failure. Neurological adaptation to an information environment designed to overwhelm your threat-detection systems until they shut down.

Think about fire alarms. First time it goes off, you evacuate immediately. Adrenaline spike, heart racing, grab your stuff and run. Second time it’s a false alarm, you’re still cautious but less panicked. By the tenth false alarm, you’re annoyed. By the fiftieth, you don’t even look up from your phone.

Then the building’s actually on fire and you burn to death because the alarm system trained you to ignore danger.

Your feed is the false alarm. NATO collapsing is the fire. You’re still scrolling.

## 7\. The Gatekeeper Genocide: Why Neither The New York Times Nor Facebook Can Save You

The mainstream media didn’t fail to cover Trump’s NATO threats. Mainstream media is covering it extensively. The problem is that coverage no longer reaches you through a system that prioritizes importance.

In 2002, there were 40 journalists for every 100,000 Americans. By 2025, that number dropped to just over eight.[^7] The infrastructure for information gathering is collapsing while algorithmic distribution grows stronger. Even when good journalism exists, it competes for attention against infinite content optimized for engagement.

The New York Times can write the definitive investigation into NATO’s collapse. Won’t matter if the algorithm shows you culture war rage-bait instead because culture war content generates more clicks from your demographic. The journalism exists. The distribution system doesn’t care.

But here’s the darker truth: Facebook can’t fix this either, even if they wanted to.

The business model is the problem. Ad-supported algorithmic feeds structurally cannot prioritize importance over engagement without destroying their revenue model. Every attempt to surface “important” content reduces time in app. Reduces ad impressions. Reduces profit. The economic incentives point one direction. That direction is not “informed citizenry.”

We killed the old gatekeepers. Editors, broadcast standards, institutional journalism with separation between business and editorial. Replaced them with math that optimizes for profit. Math doesn’t care if you understand geopolitics. Math doesn’t care if NATO collapses. Math cares if you clicked.

The centralized gatekeepers failed. The distributed gatekeepers are worse. Nobody’s at the wheel. Just algorithms optimizing for metrics that have nothing to do with democracy.

## Personal Code: Watching The Feed I Built Burn Everything Down

I left feed architecture before I understood what we’d built.

Took years of recovery from substances, from outrage addiction, from the illusion that infinite information equals understanding before I could see it clearly. The algorithms didn’t create new problems. Algorithms took existing human vulnerabilities and optimized them for profit at scale.

We’re tribal creatures who trust familiar voices. We’re dopamine-driven animals who chase novelty. We’re pattern-matchers who confuse repetition with truth. Every one of those traits helped us survive the African savannah. Every one of them is exploitable by recommendation systems designed to maximize engagement.

Trump isn’t smarter than the algorithm. Trump is just the first president to fully understand that the algorithm is the government now. Algorithms determine what people see, what they care about, what they believe is real. Control the feed, control reality. You don’t need to convince anyone of anything. You just need to make sure counter-evidence never reaches their screen.

NATO is collapsing while you scroll past because 77 years of peace made us forget what the alternative looks like. Algorithms made forgetting profitable. Trump made forgetting strategic.

In recovery, they teach you that hitting bottom isn’t about how far you fall. Hitting bottom is about when you stop digging. I stopped digging when I realized the relief I was chasing was the thing destroying me. The scroll felt like staying informed. The scroll was making me dumber.

Maybe you’re there now. Maybe you’re reading this and feeling the sick recognition that you can’t remember the last time you sustained attention on something important long enough to actually understand it. Maybe you’re realizing the feed trained you to treat everything like entertainment, including the collapse of the security architecture that kept your entire civilization alive.

The feed I built taught you that nothing matters. Now we get to find out if that’s true.

*What will you do when you realize you can’t remember how to care? Will there be anything left worth saving?*
