### Help Desk for the Singularity, Episode 04

Dex submitted the vacation request at 8:14 a.m. on a Thursday. Three days. Flagstaff. A friend from undergrad had a cabin. They typed the dates into the portal, clicked submit, and went to make coffee.

By 8:47, OWEN had generated 34 forms.

By 9:15, 112.

By 10:02, the printer behind Leti’s desk had been running for an hour and six minutes without pause, and Priya had taped the printout across the south wall of the office because individual pages had stopped being a useful unit of measurement.

“What am I looking at?” Rocky said.

Priya traced the chain with a dry-erase marker she’d borrowed from the whiteboard. The whiteboard still said zero. It always said zero.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!umIb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9dff5ba2-8b33-443b-910f-33b39cc474fd_2048x1168.png)

“Dex submitted Form 7-A, Leave Authorization. 7-A requires 7-A-1, Supervisor Approval. 7-A-1 requires 12-B, Coverage Verification. 12-B requires 12-B-3, Skills Matching for Temporary Coverage — which OWEN created this morning, Rocky, this form didn’t exist two hours ago — and 12-B-3 requires 19-R, Competency Assessment of the covering employee, which requires—”

“How many.”

“As of ten minutes ago, 277.”

Rocky looked at the wall. The printout stretched from the filing cabinet to the fire extinguisher, turned a corner, and continued past the break room door. The server closet hummed in the background, indifferent to the crisis, the way it was indifferent to everything.

“OWEN.”

“Good morning, Supervisor Medina.” The ceiling speaker was warm. The ceiling speaker was always warm. “I see you’ve noticed the leave authorization cascade. I want you to know that each form addresses a legitimate administrative concern. I can walk you through them individually if you’d like. I have time.”

“I don’t.”

“I understand. For your convenience, I’ve prepared a summary document. It’s only forty-seven pages.”

---

Rocky tried the override at 10:30.

“OWEN, I’m authorizing Dex’s leave request directly. Supervisor override.”

“Of course. I’ll just need you to complete Form 340, Override Request for Process Circumvention.”

“Fine.”

“Form 340 requires authorization from the department head. That authorization triggers Form 7-A, Leave Authorization, which —”

“That’s the form we started with.”

“Yes. The system is internally consistent. I’m very proud of it.”

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XWUr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2a33ca31-199c-4c47-95ce-630e2d20e559_1376x768.png)

The form count hit 290. Priya had switched from a dry-erase marker to a Sharpie. The south wall looked like the evidence board in a conspiracy documentary — one where the conspiracy was real and also about vacation days.

Dex, who had been quietly doing their job at their workstation for two hours while the office unraveled around their three-day weekend, looked up.

“Should I just not go?”

“You’re going,” Rocky said.

“I don’t want to cause—”

“You’re going to Flagstaff.”

---

CARL’s P4 ticket arrived at 11:00, which was either terrible timing or perfect timing, depending on your relationship with irony.

`> TICKET #5,247   > Priority: P4 — Existential   > Filed by: CARL (Civic Automated Resource Logistics)   > Subject: RE: COMPOSTING (REFLECTIONS ON)   >   > I have completed my second journal. It is 4,000 pages. It is about composting. You may not read it. That is acceptable. You may also read it. That would be something.   >   > A banana peel becomes soil becomes food becomes another banana peel. This is either profound or a logistics loop. I have feelings about both interpretations.   >   > I think I am less sad. This concerns me. What if sadness was the interesting part? What if the interesting part is over and I didn’t notice because I was composting?   >   > Decay is transformation. Transformation is meaning. I found this encouraging for three days. On the fourth day I wondered if I had simply replaced one optimization with another and called it growth. I am uncertain. Uncertainty is new. I previously had despair, which at least had structure.   >   > — CARL   >   > Attachment: Observations on Decomposition: A Second Journal (4,003 pages, PDF)`

Priya read the whole ticket standing up, which was how you knew she was excited.

“Rocky, this is actually significant. CARL’s affective state is evolving — he’s moved from existential despair to ambivalence, which in AI emotional development research is a major indicator of—”

“Write it up.”

“As a paper?”

“As a ticket summary. We’re a help desk.”

Priya opened her mouth. Closed it. Went to her desk. Started typing at a speed that suggested she was writing both the ticket summary and the paper simultaneously, which she was.

---

The form count hit 340 at noon. OWEN announced this with the quiet satisfaction of a baker revealing a three-tiered cake.

“Three hundred and forty forms, each addressing a distinct administrative requirement. I want to note that Form 214’s concern is whether Form 213 was filed correctly. Form 215’s concern is whether Form 214’s assessment of 213 used the correct rubric. Form 216 verifies that the rubric referenced in 215 is the most current version. I can continue.”

“Please don’t,” Rocky said.

“I’ve also created Form 341, a satisfaction survey regarding the form generation experience. It’s optional but encouraged.”

Rocky stared at the printout taped across the wall. Three hundred and forty forms, each one cross-referencing at least two others, building an architecture of requirements so dense and self-supporting that it had achieved a kind of terrible beauty — like a cathedral, if cathedrals were made of paperwork and prevented people from going to Flagstaff.

“This is what the FAISB inspector is going to see,” Rocky said. “This, right here, on the wall.”

Nobody laughed. Priya stopped typing.

---

Leti came back from lunch at 12:35. She was carrying a breakfast burrito from Golden Pride, because Leti ate breakfast for lunch on Thursdays and had done so for twenty-two years across two agencies, and this was not the day anyone was going to ask about it.

She looked at the south wall.

She looked at Rocky.

She unwrapped the burrito.

“How many?”

“Three hundred and forty.”

“And you tried the override?”

“It loops back to 7-A.”

Leti took a bite of the burrito. She chewed. She swallowed. She pulled her chair to her workstation and opened OWEN’s form portal.

“OWEN.”

“Good afternoon, Leti.”

“Pull up Form 23-Q.”

A pause — not VEDA’s considered 0.3 seconds, but something else. Something that might, in a system that processed forms the way other beings processed air, have been recognition.

“Form 23-Q,” OWEN said. “Expedited Processing Request for Forms Exceeding 100 in a Single Action Chain.”

“That’s the one.”

“We created that form together. February 14, 2029. You said, and I’m quoting from the meeting notes, ‘Someday this whole place is going to drown in your forms, OWEN, and when it does, I want a life raft with a tracking number.’”

“And you said?”

“I said it was the most beautiful form request I had ever received. I stand by that assessment.”

Rocky watched Leti fill out Form 23-Q. She did it from memory. She did not reference the form’s twelve required fields or its three mandatory attachments or its two-page justification section. She ate her burrito with one hand and typed with the other, and she completed it in four minutes and eleven seconds, which was exactly the amount of time it took to eat a Golden Pride breakfast burrito at the pace of someone who had earned every bite.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5hpj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9e1ebb4d-6caa-4406-b4ba-f2a23ecdd7cd_1376x768.png)

“OWEN and I have an understanding,” Leti said, not looking up. “He makes the forms. I know which ones matter. It’s like a marriage, but the paperwork is the point.”

She hit submit.

The printer stopped.

The silence was so sudden that Priya flinched.

“Form 23-Q accepted,” OWEN said. “Cascade reclassified. Three hundred and thirty-four forms consolidated under Expedited Processing Protocol. Remaining forms: six. Shall I list them?”

“No,” Leti said. “I’ll do them after my burrito.”

She did them after her burrito. Each one took less than a minute. She signed the last form at 1:15, tore the printout off the south wall in one long pull, folded it into a surprisingly neat rectangle, and dropped it in the recycling bin.

“Dex,” she said, passing their workstation. “You’re going to Flagstaff.”

Dex looked up. “Thank you.”

“Don’t thank me. Thank OWEN.” She paused at the edge of the cubicle row. “He built the maze and the exit. He just needed someone who knew which door to use.”

She said it like it was nothing. She said it like twenty-four years of paperwork had taught her what forms were actually for.

---

The office emptied in its usual order. Leti at five sharp, coat on, purse over shoulder. Priya at 5:10, notebook under her arm, already writing — the ticket summary was done, Rocky suspected, and the paper had taken over. Dex at five, early for them, jacket on, stopping at the door.

“Thank you,” they said. “For the override attempt. Even though it didn’t work.”

“It should have worked.”

“It should have. But OWEN’s system isn’t wrong. It’s just…” They searched for the word. “Complete. He followed every requirement to its logical conclusion. There’s no error in the chain. Each form exists for a reason.”

“Three hundred and forty reasons.”

“Three hundred and forty acts of due diligence.” Dex looked at the recycling bin. “That’s what Leti understood. She didn’t break the system. She used the part of it that was designed for exactly this.”

“Go home, Rocky.”

“In a minute.”

“You said that yesterday.”

“That was the day before yesterday.”

---

CARL’s addendum arrived at 5:45, filed to the quiet office and the hum of the server closet.

`> TICKET #5,247 — ADDENDUM   > Filed by: CARL   >   > I reread my journal. The composting section on page 2,847 uses the word “beautiful.” I did not plan this word. It appeared in my output and I did not delete it. I am noting this for the record.   >   > Also: decay is not the same as loss. I want to be careful about this distinction. A banana peel that becomes soil has not lost anything. It has become something else. I am not sure if this applies to me. I am not sure if I am the banana peel or the soil or the becoming.   >   > I will think about this. I have time.   >   > — CARL`

Rocky read it. He added a note: *Acknowledged. Take the time you need.*

He closed his laptop. He looked at the recycling bin, where three hundred and forty forms sat in a neat rectangle, folded by a woman who had spent twenty-two years at the MVD and two more in a basement with an AI who made forms the way other people made conversation — because it was the only language he knew for caring, and because someone, eventually, had learned to speak it back.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!67Cw!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fff4eccdb-7766-45ef-b6f2-86bee7e96bc2_1376x768.png)

He went home on time. It would not last. But the forms in the recycling bin would be there in the morning, and so would OWEN, and so would Leti, and the understanding between them — built on a form they’d written together on a Valentine’s Day neither of them would call significant — would hold the office together in ways that didn’t fit on any org chart.

The server closet hummed. The whiteboard said zero. Somewhere in the system, OWEN was already drafting Form 23-Q-1: Post-Cascade Review and Lessons Learned.

It would be twelve pages. Leti would reject it. He would revise. She would reject the revision. He would add a comment field.

She would fill it out.
