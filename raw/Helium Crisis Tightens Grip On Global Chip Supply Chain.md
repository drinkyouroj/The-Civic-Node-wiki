The invisible but indispensable helium gas powering the world’s most advanced semiconductor processes is suddenly in short supply, threatening to slow down production of the chips driving the AI boom. Qatar’s Ras Laffan Industrial City — the planet’s largest single helium production hub, responsible for roughly one-third of global supply — has been largely offline since early March following Iranian strikes and the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz. The resulting disruption has removed an estimated 27-30% of global helium supply, with spot prices surging 40-100% within weeks, according to Bank of America. For the semiconductor industry, where ultra-high-purity (6N or better) helium is essential in multiple critical steps, the squeeze is already being felt in asian supply chains.

Helium’s unique combination of extreme chemical inertness, the highest thermal conductivity of any gas and the smallest atomic radius makes it irreplaceable for wafer backside cooling during lithography and etching, maintaining stable thermal and vacuum conditions in advanced process tools, leak detection, gas purging and as a carrier gas in plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition (CVD) and extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography. Even trace impurities or temperature fluctuations at the nanometer scale can signficantly reduce yields.

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. (TSMC) has stated it is monitoring the situation closely and does not anticipate any significant impact at this time. However, supply-chain leaders at Semicon China reported that the helium shortage has already begun causing early production impacts, with companies scrambling to secure alternative supplies.

Nowhere is the vulnerability more acute than in South Korea, home to some of the world’s leading Dynamic Random Access Memory (DRAM) and high-bandwidth memory (HBM) producers. In 2025, South Korea imported approximately 64.7% of its helium from Qatar, the highest dependency among major chip-making nations according to Fitch Ratings. Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix together account for roughly 70% of global DRAM supply and a commanding share of HBM used in AI accelerators.

Memory fabrication is particularly helium-intensive because of the repeated high-heat etching and deposition steps required for dense 3D stacking. With on-site fab inventories measured in days to weeks (though strategic supply-chain reserves extend to roughly six months), the two giants are actively diversifying sources, ramping up recycling and reallocating volumes. Samsung Semiconductor has already deployed its industry-first Helium Reuse System (HeRS), which recovers, purifies and reuses high-purity exhaust helium, achieving measurable reductions in net consumption. Yet executives acknowledge that prolonged disruption could force yield trade-offs or prioritization of high-margin AI memory over legacy products.

“HBM is the gating component in modern AI accelerators. If supply tightens at the memory level, the effects cascade almost immediately into server builds and data-center deployments,” said Kevin Hein, Senior Research Analyst at Tirias Research.

TSMC, the foundry that produces roughly 90% of the world’s most advanced logic chips (including those powering Apple, Nvidia and hyperscalers), faces its own exposure. Taiwan sourced the majority of its helium from Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) countries, predominantly Qatar, with one estimate at approximately 69% in 2024. However, TSMC has publicly stated it is “monitoring the situation closely” and does not anticipate significant near-term production impacts, citing diversified contracts, robust on-site recycling (often 80-90% recovery rates at leading fabs) and existing inventories.

U.S.-based semiconductor manufacturers, particularly those with significant domestic fabrication capacity such as Intel, are less exposed to the immediate helium shortage compared to their asian counterparts. The United States is the world’s largest helium producer (approximately 81 million cubic meters annually), and most domestic fabs source most of their helium from U.S. production sites in Texas, Wyoming, Kansas, and Oklahoma, supplemented by supplies from Algeria. This heavy reliance on non-Gulf sources provides a natural buffer against the Qatar disruption.

However, the U.S. industry does not remain insulated. Fabless companies like Nvidia and AMD, which depend on TSMC for advanced logic chips and on Samsung/SK Hynix for memory, face indirect risks through their asian supply chains. Micron, a major memory producer with substantial U.S. operations, benefits from domestic helium access for its own fabs but could still encounter pressure on global pricing and availability for memory components. Prolonged shortages may lead chip makers to prioritize higher-margin AI and data-center products (such as Nvidia’s GPUs) over consumer-grade components. This exacerbates the already short memory supply driven by AI data center demand.

Analysts predict that U.S. industrial gas suppliers such as Air Products, Linde and ExxonMobil could see increased demand and greater pricing power as global supply tightens and customers shift toward more reliable U.S. and North American sources. JPMorgan and Wells Fargo recently upgraded Air Products and Linde, citing the tightening helium market and expected price recovery as a positive, while ExxonMobil’s large domestic production capacity (notably at its Shute Creek facility in Wyoming) positions it as a key beneficiary.  
  
U.S. producers now offer some global offset, but high-purity semiconductor-grade helium remains tightly concentrated. Domestic operations, including new capacity from the Rudyard project in Montana (which began production in early 2026 as the state’s first helium producer), provide a buffer for North American fabs. However, these sources cannot quickly scale to replace Qatar’s former output, particularly to meet the ongoing demand in Asia. Emerging Canadian projects are also contributing to North American supply diversification, though meaningful additional volume for semiconductor-grade material remains limited in the near term.

The helium crunch is not isolated to foundries. Downstream impacts are already emerging in electronics, automotive and data-center supply chains, with early reports of tightened availability and elevated costs rippling from Shanghai to Seoul. Gas suppliers such as Air Liquide, Linde and Air Products are redirecting inventories and accelerating conservation programs, while fabs worldwide are exploring higher-efficiency recycling technologies capable of upgrading lower-purity exhaust back to 6N-8N grades.

The current situation—already the fifth major helium crunch since 2006—highlights chronic supply-chain fragility. “Helium is a critical process gas in semiconductor manufacturing,” says Tirias Research Senior Analyst Kevin Hein. “Even brief supply disruptions can slow or halt production, especially at advanced nodes lacking practical substitutes.”

For now, the industry is in conservation-and-diversification mode. But if the Persian Gulf conflict drags on and Ras Laffan’s recovery stretches into months, higher costs, selective production cuts and accelerated investment in domestic helium sources across Asia could become the new normal. In an era when every nanometer of progress depends on an invisible, odorless gas from halfway around the world, the helium squeeze has become yet another reminder of how geopolitical shocks can rewrite the semiconductor roadmap overnight.
