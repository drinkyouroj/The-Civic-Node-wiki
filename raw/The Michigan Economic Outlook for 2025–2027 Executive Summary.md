Introduction 

Michigan’s  labor  market  has  lost  momentum  relative  to  the  nation’s  since  early  last  year.  The 

state’s unemployment rate has climbed more steeply than the U.S. rate, while local job growth has also 

slowed more meaningfully. Against this concerning backdrop, Michigan now faces the dual challenges of 

a new and unsettled tariff regime and weaker national growth prospects. Despite those obstacles, we 

believe that the state economy can continue to grow over the next few years. Yet we expect that growth 

to be modest in the face of macroeconomic headwinds and heightened policy uncertainty.

Michigan’s Unemployment Rate is Diverging from the Nation’s 

Figure 1 illustrates the path of the unemployment rate in Michigan and the United States. Michi-

gan’s unemployment rate climbed from 4.2 percent in March 2024 to 5.5 percent in March 2025, during 

which time the U.S. jobless rate climbed from 3.9 percent to 4.2 percent. The increase in the jobless rate 

has been widespread across Michigan; all 83 counties in the state had a higher unemployment rate in 

2 

 
 
 
 
 
 
January 2025 than in January 2024. Michigan’s payroll employment count has held up better, but its year-

over-year growth decelerated from 1.1 percent to 0.5 percent over the same twelve months, while the 

U.S.  rate  slowed from  1.5  percent to  1.2  percent. We  believe that Michigan’s  economic challenges  in 

2024 stemmed from the Federal Reserve’s policy of monetary tightening and high interest rates to re-

strain inflation, challenges that were exacerbated by Michigan’s rate-sensitive economy. 

Unfortunately, just as the Fed has neared victory in its campaign to bring inflation back to target, 

the economy is facing another challenge in the form of sharply higher import tariffs. We expect the tariffs 

to reduce American consumers’ overall real purchasing power, but we also consider Michigan’s economy 

to be particularly sensitive to trade policy. The sectoral tariffs on auto and parts imports, the length and 

delicacy of the automotive supply chain, and the local importance of the automotive industry all place the 

state economy at a higher risk than the nation overall. We estimate that the auto and parts tariffs, com-

bined with the steel and aluminum tariffs, will reduce Michigan payrolls by roughly 13,000 jobs over the 

next several years. We emphasize that there is considerable uncertainty around that estimate, both be-

cause  the  tariffs  themselves  remain  fluid,  and  because  any  given  configuration  of  tariffs  will  have  an 

uncertain effect on the state economy. 

It may seem surprising that we are nonetheless expecting the state economy to add jobs over the 

next few years. The basis for our relative optimism lies in what we call the state’s “non-cyclical industries,” 

particularly private education and health services, leisure and hospitality, and government. Those three 

sectors account for nearly 95 percent of the total payroll job growth we are projecting in Michigan from 

2025–2027.  Overall,  the  job  growth  in  our  forecast  follows  an  uneven  path,  with  payroll  employment 

holding flat from the first to the fourth quarters of this year before growth resumes in 2026 and 2027. 

Consistent with that path, we anticipate Michigan’s unemployment rate to climb to 6.0 percent by the first 

half of next year before edging down slightly through the end of 2027.  

It is certainly possible that we will turn out to have been too pessimistic in this forecast. Recent 

news has suggested progress toward agreements with some trade partners, although details were limited 

as of the writing of this report. Ultimately, although we project Michigan’s economy to grow at a moderate 

pace over the next three years, the risk of a downturn is now substantially higher than we would prefer.

The Impact of Tariffs on the Domestic Auto Industry and Michigan’s Economy 

Michigan’s economy is particularly sensitive to the path of tariffs and trade policy because of the 

importance of the local auto industry. Our U.S. forecast report outlines our assumptions related to the 

evolution of tariffs in detail. Here, we summarize our estimates of how the projected tariffs will affect the 

domestic auto industry, and in turn, Michigan’s economy. We emphasize that these estimates are highly 

uncertain,  both  because  we  believe  that  the  ultimate  path  of  tariffs  remains  fluid  and  because  of  the 

inherent complexity involved in assessing the effects of such a large change in policy.   

This analysis focuses primarily on the tariffs’ medium-run impacts, after supply chains have had 

time to adjust. Estimating the short-run effects of the tariffs is more difficult due to the risk that crucial 

participants in the supply chain might fail, causing cascading difficulties throughout the industry. The data 

required to evaluate such risks is closely held. Recently, the Trump Administration offered some short-

term tariff relief to the auto sector in the form of partial reimbursements for auto parts tariffs over the next 

two  years.  It  also  removed  some  tariff  “stacking”  that  had  threatened  to  raise  costs  substantially.  We 

expect that relief to help the industry bridge the gap as it adjusts its supply chains, which should help 

reduce the risk of cascading failures. 

We follow authors such as Flaaen and Pierce (2024) in decomposing the tariffs’ effects on do-

mestic manufacturing output into three main channels: 

• 

• 

• 

First, the tariffs offer protection to domestically assembled vehicles by imposing a tax on imported 
vehicles. 

Second, the tariffs raise the costs of domestic production by imposing a tax on imported parts. 

Third, the tariffs are likely to incur retaliatory tariffs from our trade partners, hurting U.S. exports. 

We also add a smaller additional channel to our analysis: 

• 

Fourth, the tariffs reduce consumers’ real purchasing power and therefore their ability to afford 
new light vehicles. 

4 

 
 
 
To quantify these effects, we first estimate the various effective tariff rates and the resulting cost 

and price increases based on our assumptions about where tariff rates will settle. We estimate that the 

average effective tariff rate on imported auto parts after reimbursements phase out will come to roughly 

13.8 percent, plus another 0.8 percent from tariffs on raw steel and aluminum. After accounting for the 

share of imported parts in U.S. assemblies and their share of production value, we estimate that the tariffs 

will raise the average cost of producing a vehicle in the United States by 5.7 percent. 

Next, we estimate that tariff rates on imported vehicles will average 21.6 percent, resulting in a 

weighted average price increase of 13.2 percent across both domestic and imported vehicles. Based on 

2024  prices,  our  estimate  would  translate  into  an  average  price  increase  of  $6,220  if margins  did  not 

change. We also estimate that retaliatory tariffs from our trade partners will impose an average effective 

tariff rate of 12.7 percent on U.S. automotive exports.

To translate these projected cost and price impacts into broader economic effects, we draw esti-

and  real  vehicle  prices,  as  well  as  how  the  sales  mix  between  imported  and  domestically  assembled

Figure 2 summarizes the results of our analysis. By combining the estimated changes in costs 

and prices caused by the tariffs with the key behavioral parameters, we are able to estimate the tariffs’ 

medium-run effects on domestic light vehicle production. We anticipate that the protective effect of the 

tariffs will be large, leading consumers to switch nearly 1.9 million purchases away from imported vehicles 

toward domestically produced vehicles. Yet that large substitution behavior is outweighed by the com-

bined decline in total U.S. light vehicle sales and U.S. exports. We estimate those declines at a bit over 

1.6 million units per year and a bit over 400,000 units per year, respectively. Altogether, our results imply 

that  the  tariffs  will  reduce  U.S.  light  vehicle  production  by  nearly  200,000  units  per year,  although  we 

again caution that there is substantial uncertainty surrounding that estimate. 

Detroit Three Light Vehicle Sales 

 Figure 3 shows our quarterly forecast for total U.S. and Detroit Three light vehicle sales. Light 

vehicle sales reached 15.8 million units in 2024, the strongest annual performance since 2019. In March 

and April of 2025, the sales pace surged past 17 million units, likely fueled by consumers rushing to make 

purchases  ahead  of  the  anticipated  auto  tariffs.  However,  we  anticipate  a  sharp  sales  decline  in  the 

coming months as tariffs take effect and the surge in tariff front-running subsides. We expect the sales 

pace to bottom out at 14.8 million units in the third quarter of 2025 before beginning a modest recovery. 

Despite the slowdown, the strong early-year momentum results in a projected total of 15.6 million units 

sold in 2025. The pace of light vehicle sales is then expected to decline to 15.2 million in 2026, as tariffs 

continue to exert upward pressure on vehicle prices, before nudging up to 15.3 million in 2027. 

The current auto tariffs are set to reshape the industry in several complex ways. Ultimately, we 

anticipate that many sectors and countries will receive exemptions from the tariffs or negotiate reduced 

rates for key industries, similar to the agreement the U.K. reached for auto imports.

state added 27,600 jobs in 2024 with most of those gains occurring in the first and fourth quarters of the 

year. Job growth continued in the first quarter of 2025 as the state added another 13,000 jobs, but we 

expect that pace to slow significantly over the remainder of the year as the impacts of the Administration’s 

trade policy begin to take hold; we are projecting growth that amounts to only 300 jobs in the last three 

quarters of 2025. We forecast growth to pick back up in early 2026, leading to 15,200 new jobs on the

After two years of languishing growth in a high interest rate environment, Michigan’s financial 

services industry enjoyed a stronger 2024, adding 2,700 jobs, with growth accelerating in the last quarter 

of the year and into the first quarter of 2025. As a result, we are projecting the finance industry to add 

4,600 jobs in 2025 followed by 1,100 in 2026 and 2,200 in 2027, supported by the gradual decline in 

mortgage rates. 

Michigan’s non-cyclical industries have, in general, displayed a much steadier growth path than 

the state’s cyclical industries. As a group, the non-cyclical industries have gained jobs every quarter since 

the onset of the pandemic in the second quarter of 2020. Even so, we expect these industries to shed 

about 700 jobs in the second quarter of 2025 with the tariff-induced contraction in economic activity before

they resume their upward trend. We are projecting the non-cyclical industries to account collectively for 

nearly all of Michigan’s job growth over the next few years, with 29,800 job gains this year, 15,300 next 

year, and 12,800 in 2027. 

The private education and health services sector, which is dominated by health services, has 

been an engine of growth for the state recently. It added 28,700 jobs in 2023 and 25,000 in 2024. We 

are projecting the pace of growth to moderate to 14,000 jobs this year and then to slow further to 3,000 

jobs in 2026 and 2,100 in 2027. The possibility of substantial reductions in federal healthcare spending 

poses an important risk, but we believe Michigan’s aging population will continue to support growth in 

this sector. 

  Job growth in the leisure and hospitality industry moderated significantly to only 5,300 jobs 

last year after averaging 33,000 job gains per year from 2021–2023. We project that it will maintain a 

similar pace over the forecast, with 5,500 job gains this year, 6,400 next year, and 5,800 in 2027. Even 

with the slower growth that we are forecasting, Michigan’s leisure and hospitality sector is projected to 

surpass its pre-pandemic share of total employment in early 2027. 

The other private services sector added 3,100 jobs last year, down slightly from the year before. 

We expect job growth to pick up slightly to 4,500 new jobs this year, although that growth is driven mostly 

by gains in the first quarter of the year. Looking forward, we expect growth to stay muted; the other private 

services sector nudges up by 600 jobs in 2026 and 300 jobs in 2027. 

Michigan’s government sector finally made its complete recovery from the pandemic last June. 

Overall, government added 14,700 jobs in 2024, a pace that we are projecting to slow substantially over 

the next three years. We forecast consistent job growth that averages 5,400 jobs per year from 2025–

2027. We are projecting roughly 1,600 federal government layoffs and separations in Michigan as part of 

the Trump Administration’s efforts to reduce the size of the federal workforce. The end of pandemic-era 

federal aid for state and local governments and the potential reductions in federal funding for health and 

education also weigh on government employment over the next few years.

Figure 6 displays the historical data and our forecast for Michigan's quarterly unemployment rate. 

Michigan’s labor market has faced a tough macroeconomic environment over the past 12 months, and it 

has shown in the numbers. From March 2024 to March 2025, the state’s unemployment rate rose by 1.3 

percentage  points,  reaching  5.5  percent,  the  largest  increase  of  any  state  during  that  period  and  the 

second-highest unemployment rate in the nation, trailing only Nevada. Despite these recent challenges, 

Michigan’s unemployment rate remains below its average from 2000–2024 of 6.8 percent, though the 

state faced significant hardships during that period, including the Great Recession and the COVID-19 

pandemic.

Local Inflation 

Figure 7 shows the history and our forecast of headline (all-item) and core (excluding food and 

energy) Detroit CPI inflation on an annual basis. Local headline inflation slowed significantly to 3.3 per-

cent in 2024, down from 5.4 percent in 2023 and its recent peak of 8.2 percent in 2022. There is consid-

erable uncertainty about both the final scope of the tariffs and their exact impact on consumer prices. Our 

forecast assumed that effective tariff rates would ultimately settle at lower levels than were in effect in 

early May. Additionally, we do not expect these price pressures to prompt the Federal Reserve to raise 

interest  rates.  We  believe  the  downside  risks  to  economic  activity  are  likely  to  weigh  more  heavily  in 

policy decisions, as was the case during the 2018–19 trade war with China. 

Moving forward, we project headline inflation to ease further to 2.1 percent in 2025, largely due 

to a notable decline in local shelter prices late last year. However, with the implementation of new tariffs, 

inflationary pressures are likely to re-emerge. By the end of 2025, we expect year-over-year headline 

inflation to register 3.0 percent. Unfortunately, we expect calendar year headline inflation to remain ele-

vated at 3.5 percent in 2026 and 3.0 percent in 2027 as tariffs continue to bite.

Local core inflation ran slightly hotter than headline inflation last year at 3.9 percent because it 

did not benefit from weaker energy inflation. We project local core inflation to decline to 2.6 percent in 

2025, again owing to a decline in local shelter prices late last year. Looking ahead, we expect core infla-

inflation over the next few years. Nonetheless, we believe it is unlikely that inflation will return to the levels 

seen in 2021–23, when local inflation peaked over 8 percent and ran above 4 percent for three consec-

utive years.

Michigan Real Disposable Income Slightly Below Trend

Our forecast for real disposable income per capita in Michigan combines our forecast of nominal 

personal income with our forecasts for taxes and local inflation. After surging during the pandemic, real 

disposable income per capita fell by 8.3 percent in 2022 due to high inflation, bringing living standards 

back  to  2019  levels.  As  headline  inflation  eased  somewhat,  real  disposable  income  increased  by  2.3 

percent in 2023. However, inflation remained elevated in 2024, and growth slowed to just 0.6 percent. 

We expect a modest improvement in 2025, with growth ticking up to 1.0 percent as local inflation finally 

falls below 3.0 percent. As the tariffs begin to bite but are largely offset by federal tax cuts, we forecast 

real disposable income growth to decelerate to an average of 0.7 percent per year in 2026 and 2027. 

Still, by 2027, Michigan’s real disposable income per capita is projected to stand 6.8 percent above its 

2019  level.  The  resulting  level  would  be  slightly  lower  than  what  the  trend  from  2000–19  would  have 

implied  but  nonetheless represents  growth  in  a  challenging  period  characterized  by  a  hefty  degree  of 

policy uncertainty.
