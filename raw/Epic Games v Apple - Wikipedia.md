# Epic Games v. Apple: Comprehensive Case Summary

Note: This is a Wikipedia-sourced summary scraped on 2026-04-08 for ingestion into the App Store chokepoint legal framework research. Not a native raw article but a compiled reference document.

## 1. Case Summary & Core Issues

**Epic's Allegations:**
Epic Games challenged Apple's iOS App Store practices, specifically: (1) Apple's 30% revenue commission on in-app purchases; (2) prohibition of alternative payment systems within apps; (3) anti-steering provisions preventing developers from directing users to external payment options.

**Apple's App Store Rules at Issue:**
- Mandatory use of Apple's In-App Purchase (IAP) system for digital goods
- Prohibition on apps informing users about payment alternatives
- Content curation and app approval requirements
- Exclusivity of iOS distribution through the App Store

**Trigger Event:** On August 13, 2020, Epic intentionally triggered a hotfix in Fortnite offering V-Bucks purchases directly from Epic at 20% discount, bypassing Apple's payment system. Apple removed Fortnite from the App Store within hours.

## 2. Procedural History

**August 2020:** Epic filed suit in U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California; Apple filed countersuit asserting breach of contract.

**Key Pre-Trial Rulings:**
- August 24, 2020: Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers granted temporary restraining order protecting Epic's developer licenses (preventing Unreal Engine damage) but denied preliminary injunction to restore Fortnite
- October 2020: Made Unreal Engine protection permanent; scheduled bench trial (no jury)

**Trial:** May 3–24, 2021 (three-week bench trial)

**District Court Decision (September 10, 2021):**

Judge Rogers issued split decision:
- **9 of 10 counts: Ruled for Apple** — Apple not found to be monopoly; 30% commission upheld; third-party marketplace restrictions upheld
- **1 of 10 counts: Ruled for Epic** — Anti-steering provisions violated California Unfair Competition Law (UCL)

**Key Holding on Market Definition:** Rogers identified the relevant market as "digital mobile gaming transactions" (not games broadly, not just iOS). Under this narrower definition, Apple held ~55% market share with "extraordinary high profit margins" but was deemed a duopoly with Google, not a monopoly.

**Injunction Ordered:** 90-day injunction (by December 9, 2021) prohibiting Apple from preventing developers from: (a) linking users to external payment systems within apps; (b) collecting contact information to notify users of alternative payment options.

**Damages:** Epic ordered to pay Apple $3.6 million (30% of withheld revenue).

## 3. Ninth Circuit Appellate Ruling (April 24, 2023)

- Ninth Circuit heard arguments November 14, 2022
- Three-judge panel unanimously affirmed District Court decision
- Stayed anti-steering injunction in July 2023 to allow Supreme Court appeal

**Specific Holdings:** The panel agreed with Judge Rogers's market definition and conclusion that Apple was not a monopoly. The court upheld the injunction requiring Apple to allow payment links but temporarily blocked its enforcement pending Supreme Court review.

## 4. Supreme Court Certiorari Denial (January 16, 2024)

**Significance:** The Supreme Court declined to hear appeals from both Apple and Epic, leaving the Ninth Circuit's affirmance intact. This meant:
- Apple could continue restricting app distribution to its store
- Apple could continue restricting in-app purchases to Apple's payment systems
- But Apple must allow developers to link to external payment options

This outcome constituted "largely a victory for Apple."

## 5. Contempt Ruling & Enforcement (April 2025)

**March 2024:** Epic filed enforcement motion alleging bad faith compliance.

**Apple's Initial Compliance (January 2024):** Allowed "metadata buttons, external links, or other calls to action" but required Apple retain **27% of sales within 7 days** of directing users elsewhere, plus a "scare screen" warning users about third-party payment risks.

**April 2025 Ruling:** Judge Rogers found Apple "willfully" violated prior injunctions. New order required Apple to:
- **Cease collecting any fees** from third-party payment methods
- **Remove all restrictions** on app interfaces and external links (beyond neutral user notification)
- **Eliminate warning screens** designed to discourage alternative payments

Rogers also referred case to federal attorney for possible criminal contempt proceedings, noting executives "knowingly took an anti-competitive route" to demonstrate compliance.

**Fortnite Return:** Sweeney stated plan to bring Fortnite to iOS within one week; app submitted May 9, 2025. After approval delays, Apple approved the app May 20, 2025.

**Appeal Status:** Apple appealed April 2025 ruling to Ninth Circuit; Ninth Circuit denied Apple's emergency stay request June 4, 2025.

## 6. App Store Fees & Anti-Steering Provisions

**30% Commission:**
- Upheld by all courts; not found anticompetitive under federal antitrust law
- Judge Rogers stated it may be "unjustified" relative to value provided but lacked comparators to order changes

**Anti-Steering Provisions — Evolution:**
1. **Original (2020):** Complete prohibition on mentioning alternative payment systems
2. **December 2021 (after injunction):** Apple allowed in-app links but maintained restrictions on prominence
3. **January 2024 (Supreme Court denial):** Added 27% "tax" on linked transactions and warning screens
4. **April 2025 (Rogers contempt ruling):** All restrictions on links eliminated; no fees allowed

**External Payment Links Status (post-April 2025):** Developers may direct users to alternative payment systems without Apple fees, warnings, or interface restrictions (U.S. only; EU rules under Digital Markets Act differ).

## 7. EU Digital Markets Act (DMA) Connection

**January 2024:** Apple announced compliance with EU's DMA, allowing third-party app storefronts on iOS in EU beginning March 2024.

**March 2024 Incident:** Apple terminated Epic's developer account in Sweden, citing untrustworthiness. Epic published Apple letters alleging retaliation for Sweeney's DMA criticism. EU launched investigation.

**March 8, 2024:** Apple reversed account termination following EU pressure.

**July 2024:** Apple approved Epic's application after Epic modified UI elements.

**August 16, 2024:** Epic Games Store and Fortnite launched for iOS in Europe only.

**UK Digital Markets Bill:** Similar requirements to DMA; passed May 2024. Epic announced plans to bring Epic Games Store and Fortnite to UK iOS in second half of 2025.

## 9. Apple's Responses & Compliance Measures

**Initial Response (September 2021):** Apple stated ruling affirmed that "the App Store is not in violation of antitrust law."

**Appellate Strategy:** Filed appeal challenging anti-steering injunction; claimed resolution in separate *Cameron v. Apple* settlement class action (August 2021) made injunction moot.

**Compliance Measures (Progressive Timeline):**
1. **December 2021:** Reluctantly implemented link provisions while adding 27% fee
2. **January 2024 (DMA):** Announced third-party storefront allowance in EU
3. **March 2024:** Attempted account termination (reversed under pressure)
4. **April 2025:** After contempt finding, eliminated fees and restrictions
5. **May 2025:** Approved Fortnite with third-party payment system

**Stated Position:** Apple claimed 30% commission reflects "immense value" including development tools, marketing, customer service, and distribution; argued fees necessary for privacy/security/content review.

## 10. Related Actions & Industry Impact

**Epic's Parallel Cases:**
- *Epic Games v. Google* (filed Aug 2020): Similar Android/Google Play claims; jury found Google unlawfully maintained monopoly (Dec 2023)
- **Australia:** Combined Epic suits against Apple and Google; Justice Jonathan Beach found violations of Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Aug 2025)
- **EU & UK:** Separate complaints filed; cases ongoing

**Industry Response:**
- **Microsoft** (Oct 2020): Committed to 10 fairness principles for Microsoft Store
- **Coalition for App Fairness** (Sep 2020): Epic and 13 other companies launched industry fairness initiative
- **40 U.S. States** (July 2021): Filed antitrust suit against Google on similar grounds
- **Senate Bill:** Open App Markets Act (introduced Aug 2021 by Senators Blumenthal, Blackburn, Klobuchar) died at end of 117th Congress
- **South Korea** (Aug 2021): First national law requiring alternative payment system allowance on app stores

## Direct Quotes

**Judge Rogers (September 2021):** "The point is that a third-party app store could put pressure on Apple to innovate by providing features that Apple has neglected."

**Judge Rogers on Market Definition:** Apple was not a monopoly under the identified "digital mobile gaming transactions" market, though it held "55% market share with extraordinary high profit margins."

**Apple (Post-September 2021):** "Today the Court has affirmed what we've known all along: the App Store is not in violation of antitrust law."
