Approximately half of US data centers scheduled to open in 2026 face cancellation or delays, according to Bloomberg reporting on Sightline Climate analysis.

Current construction status:
- 12 gigawatts of data center capacity planned for 2026
- Only 4 gigawatts (one-third) actually under construction
- Remaining capacity stuck in pre-production, subject to cancellation

Future years show worse trends:
- 2027: 6.3 GW under construction vs. 21.5 GW announced
- 2028-2032: Vast majority haven't broken ground
- 37 GW of planned infrastructure lacks firm completion dates; only 4.5 GW has begun work

The critical constraint involves electrical components manufactured overseas — batteries, transformers, and circuit breakers. Though representing less than 10% of construction costs, these parts are impossible to substitute.

Andrew Likens, energy lead at Crusoe Energy, explained: "If one piece of your supply chain is delayed, then your whole project can't deliver." He added supply constraints "can make or break a project."

Data center firms source components from Canada, Mexico, South Korea, and China, creating extended timelines for assembly and transatlantic shipping.

Additional pressures include helium shortages, cash constraints, and community opposition.
