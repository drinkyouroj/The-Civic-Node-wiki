On October 13, 2025, a group of plaintiffs filed a class action lawsuit in the United States District Court for the Northern District of California against Microsoft, alleging that the company engaged in anticompetitive conduct that artificially inflated the price of ChatGPT subscriptions.

The named plaintiffs are individual ChatGPT Plus subscribers from multiple U.S. states who seek to represent a nationwide class of consumers who purchased the service from late 2022 onwards. The complaint centers on claims that Microsoft, as a horizontal competitor to OpenAI, entered into a secretive agreement giving it exclusive control over OpenAI’s compute infrastructure through its Azure platform.

According to the filing, Microsoft used this exclusivity to restrict the supply of compute resources to OpenAI’s products, allegedly inflating token prices by up to 200 times those of competitors and degrading service quality. Even after partially relaxing the restraint in mid-2025, Microsoft retained contractual power over OpenAI’s infrastructure, maintaining what plaintiffs describe as an ongoing “sword of Damocles” over the market.

Additionally, the plaintiffs contend that this conduct violated Section 1 of the Sherman Antitrust Act and resulted in significant overcharges for millions of ChatGPT users, particularly those subscribing to ChatGPT Plus.

Consequently, in the prayer for relief, the lawsuit seeks treble damages, restitution, injunctive relief to bar Microsoft from imposing such restraints in the future, attorneys’ fees, and other appropriate remedies under federal and state antitrust laws. The plaintiffs also ask the court to dismantle Microsoft’s agreement over OpenAI’s compute supply and to restore competitive conditions in the consumer generative AI market.

**Allegations In Detail**

The plaintiffs allege that Microsoft and OpenAI, though “horizontal competitors in the Consumer Generative AI Market,” entered into a secretive agreement that gave Microsoft control over the supply of compute to OpenAI’s generative AI products. Through an exclusivity clause, Microsoft required OpenAI to “exclusively use Microsoft (specifically, its Azure cloud service) for compute services,” effectively allowing it to “restrict the output of its horizontal competitor.”

As demand for ChatGPT surged, Microsoft allegedly “exercised its control over compute supply to restrict output,” causing “massive price inflation for OpenAI tokens”, the price charged for API users to access OpenAI models, and visible degradations in “quality, speed, sophistication, and product choice.”

The plaintiffs further argue that this artificial scarcity was strategic, enabling Microsoft to extract “supracompetitive profits from OpenAI’s customers” while advancing its own competing products, including Copilot. The complaint claims that OpenAI’s ChatGPT prices were “100 to 200 times competitors’ prices on a per-token basis” during a 2025 price war, a disparity they attribute directly to Microsoft’s restraint.

Moreover, the filing asserts that Microsoft “imposed a term on OpenAI” that locked the start-up into dependency and guaranteed its subordination. In essence, the plaintiffs accuse Microsoft of “mercilessly choking OpenAI’s compute supply” to inflate prices, delay product innovation, and preserve its own market advantage, conduct they claim distorted fair competition and directly harmed millions of early ChatGPT users.

**Price Drop After DeepSeek**

In early 2025, the entry of the Chinese firm DeepSeek radically disrupted the Consumer Generative AI (CGAI) market. As the complaint states, DeepSeek released “its own model at a fraction of the cost and using a fraction of the computing power used by OpenAI and others,” triggering a global price war.

However, “OpenAI’s flagship model was 136x the price charged by the competition,” as Microsoft’s contractual compute restraint continued to bind OpenAI exclusively to its Azure cloud services. This artificial output restriction, the plaintiffs allege, “set a price floor for ChatGPT products,” preventing OpenAI from adjusting prices or scaling up to remain competitive.

By February 2025, DeepSeek’s aggressive strategy had intensified. It “announced that it would drop its ‘off-peak’ pricing by 75%,” a move that Reuters reported had “put pressure on rivals in China and overseas to cut prices.” These reductions, timed to coincide with daytime hours in the U.S., sent shockwaves through the market and “triggered a sell-off in tech stocks.”

Although nearly every other U.S. CGAI company slashed prices to stay competitive, OpenAI could not. Microsoft allegedly refused “to release OpenAI from its exclusivity requirement,” continuing to “constrain the supply of compute available to OpenAI,” thereby keeping its prices “supracompetitively inflated” even as rivals dropped charges.

Tensions culminated in June 2025 when OpenAI obtained “compute from Google, ending its Microsoft exclusivity.” The complaint asserts that OpenAI’s high-end model prices “immediately dropped 80%,” and the company “launched long-delayed new image generation features and more powerful models.” This dramatic reversal, according to plaintiffs, demonstrated that Microsoft’s “compute restraint was the but-for and proximate cause” of inflated prices and degraded quality.

The price collapse that followed DeepSeek’s entry and Google’s involvement, they argue, exposed the full extent of Microsoft’s anticompetitive control over OpenAI and the broader AI market.

**Computation As A Barrier To Entry**

The complaint argues that computation itself functions as a powerful “barrier to entry” in the CGAI market, one that Microsoft deliberately leveraged to maintain dominance. According to the filing, “large-scale generative AI—the foundation of CGAI products—requires massive amounts of computation,” and Microsoft “long dominated the supply of GPUs necessary for such large-scale computation.”

The document cites reports that Microsoft and Meta spent a combined $9 billion on Nvidia’s H100 chips in 2023 and that Microsoft “reportedly plans to amass some 1.8 million GPUs by the end of 2024.” This concentration of computing power, the lawsuit alleges, allowed Microsoft to erect what it calls an AI Computational Barrier to Entry (AICBE), preventing smaller firms from training and deploying competitive models.

DeepSeek’s emergence in early 2025 partially eroded this barrier. The Chinese firm released its own model at a fraction of the cost and computing requirement, training its system for only $6 million, a “rounding error” compared to OpenAI’s multibillion-dollar expenditures. By open-sourcing its models, DeepSeek “significantly eroded the AICBE” and made it possible for other entrants with sufficient compute access to build competitive models.

Still, the lawsuit notes that “switching costs” and “network effects” preserved Microsoft’s advantage, allowing it to “continue to extract supracompetitive prices” even after DeepSeek’s entry.

The complaint defines the CGAI market as a “distinct submarket of the general market for AI software,” encompassing products such as ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, Gemini, Perplexity, and DeepSeek.

As of July 2025, OpenAI held 82.65% of this market, with a Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) score of 6,916.59, far above the U.S. Department of Justice threshold for a “highly concentrated” market. These figures, plaintiffs argue, demonstrate both the anticompetitive effects of compute concentration and Microsoft’s ongoing control of the generative AI ecosystem.

**Why This Matters**

This lawsuit matters well beyond the specifics of its parties because it strikes at the heart of how future AI ecosystems will evolve.

First, the case spotlights the strategic centrality of compute and infrastructure as the new levers of power in tech. If firms can manipulate access to computational resources, they can distort competition before rival models ever gain traction. In that respect, the plaintiffs challenge a paradigm where incumbents like Microsoft might wield compute as a gatekeeper rather than a neutral utility.

Moreover, the dispute over DeepSeek’s entry and the price collapse underscores how even disruptive innovation faces structural drag when it collides with entrenched compute control. The argument that DeepSeek eroded the barrier but could not fully overcome it suggests that competition might be technically possible, yet economically fragile, under the control of core inputs.

Finally, defining CGAI as a distinct, highly concentrated market with figures such as an 82%+ market share for OpenAI and an HHI nearing 7,000 highlights that this is not a niche dispute but one about market power in a foundational domain. If courts accept that CGAI is a separate product market, the implications will ripple through regulation, antitrust doctrine, and how AI platforms license, integrate, or bundle services.

This lawsuit isn’t merely about ChatGPT pricing; it may serve as a precedent for how antitrust law confronts control over compute, AI‐platform gatekeeping, and the contestability of generative AI markets.
